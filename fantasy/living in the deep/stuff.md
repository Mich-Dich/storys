### 6. The Weight of Gold

**Purpose:** Show the practical shopping trip. Establish Greymouth's character through the lens of its markets. Give John and Lilith their first extended interaction outside the auction house.

**Scene:** They leave Velsa & Sons. John turns not toward the residential district, where most buyers would take a new slave, but toward the market streets. Lilith follows without comment, her footsteps light on the cobblestones. Her tail curls close to her legs—the same habit he noticed earlier.

John leads her through the morning market, buying what he needs. A bed frame and mattress from a carpenter who deals in delver furniture—sturdy, collapsible for transport. Two sets of work clothes for Lilith, sized by eye. Towels, blankets, a washbasin. Lead ingots from a metalsmith—ten pounds, for reasons he does not explain.

**Exposition woven in:**
- The market is a place where magic and craft blend seamlessly. The carpenter uses a precision-cutting pattern to shape joints faster than any saw. The clothier's measuring tape has a simple detection pattern that records dimensions without repeated measurements. The metalsmith's forge is lined with heating crystals that maintain consistent temperature, and his apprentice uses a modified Levitation weave to move ingots from stack to scale.
- This is not a world of high fantasy wonder. It is a world where people have found practical uses for the tools available to them, just as people on Earth used fire and wheel and lever. Magic is work. Magic is trade. Magic is the thing that lets a tanner cure leather in three days instead of three weeks, and that is why everyone uses it.
- John moves through this environment with the ease of someone who understands systems. He knows which vendors are honest, which will try to overcharge a young-looking buyer, which will accept a small overpayment in exchange for loading the goods onto a cart and delivering them to the quarry gate without asking questions.

**Character beat:** Lilith watches him. She has been sold twice before—once to the merchant who died, once to Velsa after his estate was settled. She knows how buyers behave. They ignore slaves, or they watch them too closely, or they make small comments to assert ownership. John does none of these things. He treats her as he might treat a piece of equipment he has just purchased: he does not talk to it, but he does not insult it either. He simply... uses it. She is not sure if this is better or worse than what she expected.
