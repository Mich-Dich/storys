# The Artificer of the Deep

## Chapter 2: The Price of Silence

John Doe sat in his workshop, seventeen years old, three years lost, and watched the weapon he had built grow dim in the failing light.

The torch burned low, its flame retreating into embers that cast more shadow than illumination. The luminescent moss on the northern wall provided its steady, unwinking glow, but even that seemed muted now, as if the cavern itself was settling into the particular stillness that came in the hours before the dungeon's magicule tide shifted from ebb to flow. He had not moved from his chair in what felt like hours. His hands were folded on the table before him, the gun resting a few centimeters from his fingertips, and his eyes had long since stopped seeing the weapon's shape and turned instead to the calculations that ran behind them like water finding its level.

He did not sleep that night. Or perhaps he did—it was difficult to tell, in the absence of windows or sun or any of the ordinary markers that divided one day from the next. The dungeon had its own rhythms, its own tides, and John had learned to sleep when the magicule concentration was at its lowest ebb and to work when it rose. Tonight, or what he thought of as night, the concentration held steady at a level that was neither restful nor energizing, and he sat through it with his eyes half-closed and his thoughts turning in slow, deliberate circles around the problem that had been waiting for him to acknowledge it.

The problem was not the gun. The gun worked. The problem was everything that came after.

He rose when the torch finally died, leaving only the moss-light and the dim glow of the spring water reflecting off the far wall. His body moved through the motions of waking with the efficiency of long practice—stretch the spine, roll the shoulders, flex the fingers that had cramped during the night's stillness. The unconscious Wave pulsed across his skin, clearing the residue that had accumulated while he sat motionless, and he breathed out a thin fog of expelled waste that dissipated into the cavern's ambient magicules before it could settle.

The perimeter check was first. It was always first. Three years of living alone in a place where death could come from any shadow had taught him that security was not a one-time installation but a constant, ongoing process. He moved through the tunnels that branched from his workshop, checking each of the simple traps he had placed at key choke points. The string-and-bell alarm at the eastern entrance had been triggered—the bell was missing entirely, knocked from its perch and lying on the tunnel floor, and the string had been snapped rather than merely disturbed. Something larger than a Chitin Crawler had passed through here, and recently. The edges of the break were still frayed, not yet coated with the fine dust that accumulated on older damage.

He reset the trap with new string and a replacement bell from his stores, making a mental note to check the eastern tunnel for tracks later. The northern entrance had held. The southern route was undisturbed. The western entrance, a narrow fissure that opened into a maze of smaller tunnels he had never fully explored, showed signs of recent movement but nothing large enough to trigger his alarms. He reset two of the three traps that needed attention, noted the third for replacement when he returned, and made his way back to the workshop.

The food stores took longer to inventory than he expected. The chest near his bed held the results of his last four hunts: salted meat wrapped in oiled cloth, dried mushrooms strung on leather cords, preserved roots that he had learned to recognize as edible during his first desperate months underground. He counted each item, weighed each portion, calculated how many days of food remained. The numbers were not good. At his current consumption rate—carefully calibrated to maintain strength without waste—he had perhaps twelve days of food remaining. Less if he pushed himself hard, more if he stretched it, but the margin was narrower than he liked. The hunting had been poor recently, the Rockback that had triggered his alarm and others like it driving the smaller prey deeper into the tunnels or into hiding entirely.

He closed the chest and stood in the center of his workshop, letting the silence settle around him. The list of tasks that needed doing had been growing in the back of his mind for weeks, and now he pulled it forward, examined it, let it spread across his awareness like a schematic that had been folded too long and needed to be flattened before its lines could be read.

The crystal growth process required three days of absolute focus. Three days during which he could not hunt, could not cook, could not maintain the traps, could not do anything except hold the weave and ensure that the growing lattice carved its hollows correctly. The last time he had attempted a major growth—the fourteen-centimeter crystal that now powered the gun—he had emerged from the three days to find mold spreading through his food stores, two of his perimeter alarms broken, and a Rockback nest established in a side tunnel less than an hour's walk from his workshop. It had taken him a week to clear the nest and another three days to restore his food supplies to a safe level. He had not attempted another major growth since.

That had been three weeks ago. Three weeks of hunting, trapping, maintaining, and—in the hours between—working on the gun's design, refining the barrel, casting more bullets, thinking about what came next. The gun worked, but it was not finished. The fourteen-centimeter crystal had achieved eighteen percent efficiency, but he had calculated that a longer crystal—perhaps twenty centimeters, perhaps twenty-five—could push that efficiency to twenty-five percent or higher, could double the muzzle velocity, could turn a weapon that killed a Rockback into a weapon that could kill anything in the dungeon. The crystal he needed for the next iteration required precious days. And every day he spent growing crystals was a day he did not survive.

He pulled out his chair, sat down, and pulled a fresh page from the stack he kept in a waterproofed leather folder. His stylus was already in his hand—it was always in his hand, these days, as natural an extension of his fingers as the magicules that flowed through them—and he began to write, not in the careful, measured language of his experiment notes but in the blunt, abbreviated shorthand he used when he was thinking aloud.

*Tasks requiring attention:*

He wrote for several minutes, filling the page with items that had accumulated over the past weeks. *Hunt (urgent). Reset eastern trap (done). Clear western fissure (small nest, 2-3 Chitterers based on tracks). Check northern vein for crystal growth (overdue by two weeks). Replace moldy preserves (3 jars, discard). Treat leather (starting to crack). Cast more bullets (5 remaining, need 20 for testing). Inventory tools (hammer handle loose). Inventory materials (low on growth medium, need more chitin). Growth attempt #27 (need 3 days, need food buffer, need trap maintenance during).*

The list went on, two pages front and back, until his hand ached and the ink was running low in his stylus. He set the pen down and looked at what he had written. The tasks were not impossible. They were not even particularly difficult, taken one by one. But they were not one by one. They were a pile, a weight, a slow accumulation of needs that his single pair of hands could not address quickly enough to keep all the plates spinning at once. The hunting took time. The trap maintenance took time. The crystal growth took time he did not have to spare, and the food that would sustain him through three days of weaving needed to be hunted and preserved *before* he began, which took time he also did not have to spare. The math was simple and brutal, and he had been avoiding it for weeks because he did not want to see the numbers laid out in black and white.

He pulled a fresh page and wrote three lines, each one a column heading, and began filling in the variables beneath them.

*One person. Twenty-four hours per day. Hunting: 4-6 hours per successful hunt, 1-2 hunts per week to maintain food. Trap maintenance: 1-2 hours per day, more if damage extensive. Crystal growth: 72 consecutive hours, requires minimum 5 days of food stockpiled beforehand. Sleep: 5-6 hours per night, less during growth cycles. Research: variable, but impossible during growth cycles.*

He stared at the numbers, then added them up in the margins. The answer was obvious. He had known it for weeks. He had just not wanted to see it written down.

One person could not sustain a workshop, a hunting territory, a living space, and a research program simultaneously. Something had to give. He could abandon the research—stop growing crystals, stop refining the gun, stop filling notebooks with theories and diagrams—and focus entirely on survival. The gun already worked. He did not need to make it better. He could simply *live*, hunting and trapping and maintaining, and let the days pass as they had passed for the first two years, when survival had been enough. Or he could abandon the hunting and the maintenance and the other tasks that kept him alive, pour everything into the research, and hope that what he produced would be worth the cost. The gun was not worth dying for. But the unified theory of magic that he was building, page by page, experiment by experiment—that might be. He was not sure. He had not yet decided.

He pulled out a fresh page and wrote three options, not because he needed to see them but because writing made them real, made them something he could examine and weigh and compare.

*1. Hire help from surface — too many questions, no guarantee of discretion, would require revealing workshop location, would require explaining crystals, would require explaining gun. Not viable.*

He crossed it out before the ink was dry. The surface was where other people lived, people who talked and gossiped and sold information to the highest bidder. He had spent three years making himself invisible, and the moment he hired someone to work in his workshop, that invisibility would end. The guild would hear. The black market dealers who bought his crystals would hear. Someone would decide that a seventeen-year-old who had survived three years alone in the Deep must have something worth taking, and then the questions would become demands, and the demands would become threats, and he would have to leave or fight or—

He wrote the second option.

*2. Train an assistant — requires trust he does not have and time he cannot spare. Would need to teach pattern weaving, purge techniques, workshop procedures. Would need to explain everything. Would need to trust. Not viable.*

But trust was not the only problem. Time was the problem. Time was always the problem. Training an assistant would take weeks, months, years of the same hours he did not have to spare. He would need to teach pattern weaving from the beginning, because no mage in this world approached magic the way he did—as a system of primitives to be understood and combined rather than spells to be memorized and repeated. He would need to teach the purge weave, which he had developed over months of trial and error and which required a comfort with one's own internal channels that most mages never achieved. He would need to teach the crystal growth process, the maintenance routines, the hunting patterns, the thousand small pieces of knowledge that made his life possible. And while he was teaching, he would not be researching. He would not be building. He would be treading water, surviving, waiting for his student to learn enough to be useful, and in that waiting he would lose the momentum that had carried him this far.

A free assistant could simply leave. After weeks or months of teaching, after John had poured his limited hours into bringing someone else up to a useful level of competence, that person could walk back to the surface, taking everything they had learned with them. There would be no way to compel them to stay, no way to ensure they did not sell his secrets to the highest bidder. Even if they stayed, they would have their own will, their own priorities, their own judgment about what work was worth doing and what was not. He could not force them to spend three days maintaining a crystal growth while he hunted; they would have to choose to do it, and a free person with marketable skills would have many choices. The teaching investment would be enormous, and the return would be uncertain, contingent on the goodwill and continued presence of someone whose survival did not depend on John in the slightest.

The third option stared up at him from the page, its letters still wet with ink.

*3. Acquire guaranteed loyalty — slave, B-rank or higher for residue tolerance, discretion guaranteed by collar, expensive but viable.*

He read the words three times, not because he was uncertain of their meaning but because he was testing them against the constraints he had already established. *Guaranteed loyalty.* The collars used by the slave markets were not subtle. They were enchanted iron bands inscribed with Anchor, Cage, and Lock patterns that created an absolute binding between master and slave. The slave could not harm the master, could not disobey direct orders, could not reveal the master's secrets. Any attempt triggered a cascading failure in the binding patterns that killed the wearer before the information could be spoken or the action completed. The collars were permanent. Removal killed the slave. They were brutal, absolute, and—for his purposes—perfect.

A slave would also need to be taught. That was inescapable. No one on the surface knew his methods, his patterns, his particular way of understanding magic. But a slave could be compelled to learn. The collar ensured obedience, and obedience meant that every hour John spent teaching would be an hour of focused attention from the student, not a favor to be repaid or a gift to be squandered. A slave could be ordered to maintain the perimeter traps while John grew crystals, to cook and clean while he researched, to stand watch while he slept. There would be no negotiation, no bargaining, no risk that the assistant would simply decide to leave on a bad day. And because the collar bound the slave's life to John's continued survival in the Deep—any attempt to flee would kill the wearer, and any failure to obey direct orders would do the same—the assistant's own survival would become entangled with the success of the work. A free assistant could quit. A slave could not.

*B-rank or higher for residue tolerance.* The Deep was not a place where ordinary humans could survive. The magicule concentration was too high, the residue accumulation too rapid. A slave without sufficient tolerance would sicken within days, die within weeks, and be useless for the work he needed done. B-rank was the minimum threshold for extended dungeon stays. Higher would be better, but higher would also be more expensive and harder to find.

*Discretion guaranteed by collar.* This was the critical variable. The collar did not simply prevent betrayal—it made betrayal impossible. There was no risk that a slave would talk to the wrong person, sell his secrets to the highest bidder, lead the guild to his workshop with promises of treasure. The collar enforced silence with death, and death was a more reliable guarantee than any contract or oath or bond of trust could ever be.

*Expensive but viable.* He had money. Not in the form of coins—he rarely bothered to exchange his crystals and monster parts for currency, since currency was useless in the Deep—but in the form of materials that could be sold. The Deep Viper scales that he had been saving for a project he had never quite started. The preserved glands of a C-rank Lurker, useful for alchemical reagents that no one in Greymouth could afford. The Aether crystals he had grown during his early experiments, too crude for his own purposes but valuable to surface mages who did not understand the difference between a hollow-carved channel and a natural lattice. He had wealth. He had just never needed to spend it.

He circled the third option, pressing the stylus hard enough to leave a groove in the paper. It was not a moral choice. He did not allow himself to think of it in those terms. Morality was a luxury for people who had the time and safety to consider the consequences of their actions beyond the immediate need to survive. He had neither. This was an engineering choice, a matter of constraints and variables and finding the solution that satisfied all the requirements. He needed help. He could not hire it, could not train it, could not trust it. Therefore, he would buy it.

He set the stylus down and stood, his chair scraping against the stone floor with a sound that seemed too loud in the cavern's stillness. The list of tasks was still there, two pages of things that needed doing, and now he had added a new task to the top of it: *Acquire assistant. Three days minimum. Maybe more.*

He walked to the chest where he kept his stores of preserved monster parts, the materials that were too valuable to use in experiments and too bulky to keep on the workbench. The chest was carved from the same dense wood as his tables, its joints sealed with resin to keep out moisture and the small insects that sometimes found their way into the workshop despite his best efforts. He lifted the lid, and the smell that rose from within was not unpleasant—dried herbs, cured leather, the faint metallic tang of preserved organs—but it was the smell of time, of effort, of hunts that had cost him blood and exhaustion and the kind of focus that left him hollowed out for days afterward.

He pushed aside the top layer of materials—Lurker glands wrapped in oiled cloth, preserved Chitterer mandibles that he had been meaning to trade for better tools, a bag of powdered Aether crystal that had not made it into any of his growth mediums—and reached deeper. His fingers found the bundle at the bottom, wrapped in soft leather that had been treated to resist moisture and mold. He lifted it out, carried it to the table, and laid it flat under the light of the nearest glowing stone.

The scales of a Deep Viper were not like the scales of surface snakes. They were larger, for one thing—each one roughly the size of his palm—and they caught the light differently, refracting it into spectra that shifted from deep green to pale gold as the angle changed. They were also tougher. He had tested them during the long weeks after he had killed the viper, had subjected sample scales to his plasma torch, to the impact of his heaviest hammer, to the sustained pressure of a hydraulic press he had constructed from scrap wood and a modified Levitation weave. The scales had resisted everything he could throw at them. They were light, flexible, and capable of stopping a crossbow bolt from close range without cracking. A full suit of armor made from Deep Viper scales would make a delver nearly invulnerable to anything short of an A-rank monster's attack.

He had been saving them for something. He was not sure what. A project that had never quite crystallized, a use that had never seemed urgent enough to justify cutting up such a perfect set. He had killed the viper in his first year, before he had fully understood how dangerous they were, before he had developed the plasma torch or the gun or any of the tools that made his current life possible. He had killed it with a spear and a desperation that he did not like to remember, and he had taken its scales because taking was what you did when you survived something that should have killed you. He had kept them because they were beautiful, and because beauty was rare in the Deep, and because some part of him that he did not acknowledge had wanted to keep something that reminded him he was still capable of surprise.

He laid the scales out on the table, twelve of them in perfect condition, and ran his fingers across their surfaces. The texture was smooth, almost oily, with a faint warmth that spoke of the magicules still bound into their structure. A Deep Viper absorbed magicules from its environment, used them to strengthen its hide and power its venom, and the scales retained some of that absorption even after death. They would be worth a fortune on the surface. Enough to buy a B-rank slave with money left over for supplies, for tools, for months of uninterrupted research.

He began wrapping them again, carefully, the way he had learned to wrap fragile components during his engineering days—each scale separated by a layer of soft leather, the bundle secured with leather cord that would not cut or abrade. The movement was automatic, his hands working while his mind turned over the implications of what he was about to do. He would need to go to the surface. He would need to find a fence who could move goods of this quality without asking too many questions. He would need to find the slave market, navigate its transactions, select an assistant who met his requirements. He would need to bring that assistant back into the Deep, into his workshop, into the life he had built alone and intended to keep building.

He tied the final knot and sat back, the bundle resting on the table before him. The torchlight caught the edges of the wrapped scales, throwing small highlights across the worn wood. He looked at the bundle for a long time, then at the gun still lying where he had left it, then at the crater in the far wall where the bullet had carved its wound into solid stone.

He did not know what he was building. He had told himself, for three years, that he was building knowledge, a unified theory of magic, an understanding of the dungeon that no one else had ever achieved. But the gun was not knowledge. The gun was a weapon, and weapons had uses, and those uses were not theoretical. He did not know what he would do with the gun, what he would do with the crystals he was learning to grow, what he would do with the power that was accumulating in his hands like magicules in a crystal waiting to be released. He did not know, and he had not let himself think about it, because thinking about the future was a luxury for people who were not spending every hour of every day trying to survive.

But now, with the Deep Viper scales wrapped and ready and the gun waiting beside them, he let himself think about it for just a moment. Not for long. Just long enough to acknowledge that the path he was walking led somewhere, and that he did not know where that somewhere was, and that he was going to walk it anyway because the alternative was to stop walking and he had not survived three years in the Deep by stopping.

He picked up the bundle, carried it to the chest, and placed it on top of the other materials where he could reach it easily when he was ready to leave. Then he sat back down at the table, picked up his stylus, and began writing the plan that would carry him through the next days.

*Surface trip. Depart in two days, after hunting and trap reset. Take Deep Viper scales. Trade for gold. Acquire B-rank slave with domestic skills. Return. Begin growth attempt #27 within one week of return, using food stores maintained by assistant during growth.*

He wrote the words in the same flat, technical language he used for his experiment notes, as if he were describing the steps of a crystal growth or the parameters of a pattern weave. It was not a moral choice. It was an engineering choice. He needed a solution that worked, and this was the only one that satisfied all the constraints. The world was a system of cause and effect, of inputs and outputs, of problems and solutions. He had identified a problem. He had found a solution. The rest was implementation.

He closed the notebook, blew out the torch that was beginning to gutter, and sat in the darkness with the gun before him and the wrapped scales waiting in their chest and the weight of what he was about to do pressing against his chest like the first pressure of a purge weave that had not yet begun to move. He did not sleep. He did not move. He sat in the silence of his workshop, three years lost, and watched the faint glow of the luminescent moss paint silver patterns on the walls, and waited for the dungeon to breathe its next tide.
















The dungeon breathed its next tide, and John Doe rose to meet it.

He had been waiting for this moment—not consciously, perhaps, but his body had learned to read the rhythms of magicule flow the way a surface-dweller learned to read the position of the sun. The concentration in the air had been falling for hours, the slow exhalation that followed each peak, and now it had reached the point where the tunnels would be as safe as they ever got. The monsters would be less active, the ambient residue less aggressive, the journey upward less likely to end in a confrontation he did not have time for. He had spent the intervening hours packing, checking his equipment, running through the mental checklist of what he would need and what he would leave behind. The Deep Viper scales were secured in his pack, wrapped in oiled cloth and cushioned by a layer of dried moss to prevent even the smallest scratch. The gun was disassembled into its component parts—crystal, barrel, housing, stock—each piece wrapped separately and hidden in compartments he had sewn into the lining of his coat over a year ago, during a period when he had nothing better to do with his hands and a growing awareness that he might one day need to carry things he did not want seen. The rest of his equipment was minimal: a change of clothes, a small pouch of Aether crystals for barter, a knife for the kinds of problems that could not be solved with coin or conversation.

He took one last look at the workshop before he left, his eyes moving across the space with the particular attention of someone who did not know when he would return. The tables were clear of anything valuable, the notebooks locked in the waterproofed chest he had built into the wall, the crater in the far wall was a darker shadow against the darker stone, and he looked at it for a moment longer than the rest before turning away. The gun had been his first real creation in this world, the first thing he had built that was not simply a tool for survival. He was not sure what that meant, but he knew it was important, and he knew he would come back to it. He always came back.

The ascent took longer than he remembered. It always did, after he had been deep for a while. The tunnels stretched and twisted in ways that his memory had smoothed into something more direct, and he found himself pausing at intersections he had navigated a hundred times before, checking the small marks he had carved into the stone to guide his way. The marks were simple—a dot for safe passage, an X for danger, a line pointing toward the nearest exit—but they had kept him alive in the early days, when every tunnel looked the same and the darkness had a way of swallowing direction. He still used them, not because he needed them but because the habit was old and comfortable and because he had learned never to assume that familiarity would protect him from a wrong turn.

The collapsed quarry tunnel was the last obstacle before the surface. A mining operation that had dug too deep, too fast, and had brought the ceiling down on itself in a cascade of stone and dust that had killed seven men and left a scar on the hillside that nothing had grown over in the years since. The tunnel that remained was narrow, a gap between fallen boulders that required a person to turn sideways and slide through with their pack held above their head. John had widened it slightly over the years, chipping away at the most obstructive stones with his plasma torch during trips when he had more time than patience, but it was still a tight fit. He moved through it slowly, feeling the rough stone press against his shoulders, his coat, his carefully concealed cargo. A few more centimeters and he would have been forced to leave the gun behind.

The light hit him before he was fully through the gap.

It was weak light—the gray of an overcast morning, filtered through clouds that had been gathering over the mountains for days—but after four months in the Deep, it was blinding. He stopped where he was, half-in and half-out of the tunnel, and let his eyes adjust. The process was slower than it used to be. His pupils had adapted to the dungeon's perpetual twilight, had learned to read darkness the way other people read faces, and the sudden flood of daylight sent spikes of pain through his skull that resolved, slowly, into the shapes of things he had almost forgotten. The quarry walls rose on either side, pale stone streaked with the dark lines of old water flow. The sky above was a flat gray that seemed to go on forever, empty of clouds in any meaningful sense, just a blanket of light that pressed down on everything and washed the color out of the world. A bird called somewhere in the trees that bordered the quarry's edge, and John flinched at the sound before he could stop himself. There were no birds in the Deep. There were no birds anywhere that he had been, not for four months, and the sound was so foreign that it took him a moment to identify it, to place it in the catalog of things he had once known and had not thought about since.

He pulled himself through the gap, his boots finding the familiar holds in the rock, and stood in the open air for the first time in a hundred and twenty-three days. The wind was cold against his face, carrying the smell of damp earth and dead leaves and something else, something green that he could not name. He stood very still, letting the sensations wash over him, and waited for the part of him that was supposed to feel something about this to wake up. It did not. The surface was where other people lived. He was passing through.

The shelter was a shallow overhang at the edge of the quarry, where a slab of stone had broken away from the wall and wedged itself against a boulder in a way that left a gap just large enough for a person to stand upright. He had found it during his first year, had reinforced it with branches and canvas he had bought from a merchant who asked no questions, had used it as a staging point for his trips to the surface ever since. The canvas was still there, gray and weather-beaten, and the small cache of supplies he kept hidden behind it was undisturbed. He ducked inside, pulled the canvas closed behind him, and began the transformation that turned John Doe the dungeon hermit into a young delver with a successful hunt to sell.

The clothes came first. His dungeon clothes were functional—leather and wool, dark colors that did not show dirt, reinforced at the knees and elbows where he did most of his crawling—but they were also the clothes of someone who lived underground, stained with monster blood and the residues of a dozen different growth mediums, worn thin in places where the fabric had rubbed against stone. He stripped them off, folded them into a bundle, and pulled on the clothes he kept in the cache: a simple linen shirt, a wool vest that was too warm for the season but covered the compartments in his coat, trousers of heavy cotton that had been patched twice and would probably need a third patch soon. The coat went on last, its weight familiar against his shoulders, the hidden compartments pressing against his ribs and spine in ways that had become as natural as the unconscious Wave that still pulsed across his skin. He combed his hair with his fingers, wiped the worst of the dungeon grime from his face, and looked at himself in the small piece of polished metal he had hung from the overhang's ceiling. The face that looked back was not the face of a seventeen-year-old. It was older, leaner, with eyes that had learned to see in darkness and a stillness around the mouth that spoke of too many hours spent alone. He looked like a delver who had been in the Deep too long, which was exactly what he needed to look like. Forgettable. Just another young man who had tried his luck underground and was coming back to sell what he had found.

He stepped out of the shelter, pulled the canvas back into place, and began the walk to Greymouth.

The cart track from the quarry joined the main road after a few hundred meters, and the road became a street as the scattered buildings at the town's edge condensed into something more organized. The change was gradual—a farmhouse here, a stable there, then a row of cottages with smoke rising from their chimneys—and John moved through it with the practiced ease of someone who had made this walk many times before. His feet knew the rhythm of it, the places where the cobblestones were uneven, the corners where the wind funneled down from the hills and cut through his coat like a knife. He kept his head down, his shoulders hunched, his pace steady. There was nothing remarkable about him. There was nothing to see.

The town woke around him as he walked. A baker was opening his shutters, the smell of fresh bread rolling out into the street in a wave of warmth that made John's stomach clench with a hunger he had not noticed until now. The baker's oven was visible through the open door—a massive stone structure lined with pattern crystals that glowed a steady orange, their surfaces inscribed with Spiral and Anchor weaves that maintained a constant temperature through the night. The crystals had been installed by the baker's grandfather, according to the story John had heard from a merchant two years ago, and they had not needed replacement or repair in the sixty years since. He filed the information away without conscious thought, noting the arrangement of the patterns, the way the Spiral weaves nested inside the Anchor constraints, the efficiency of a design that had lasted three generations. The baker himself was a large man with flour dusted across his apron, and he nodded at John as he passed, the automatic greeting of a merchant to a potential customer, and John nodded back with the automatic blankness of someone who had nothing to buy.

The streetlamps along the main road were still burning, their Aether crystals flickering in the gray morning light like candles that had forgotten to be extinguished. The guild maintained them, replacing the crystals every month with fresh ones charged by the guild's mages, and the steady orange glow they cast had become so familiar to the town's residents that no one looked at them anymore. John looked. He always looked. The crystals were a demonstration of how magic was used in this world, not as something wondrous or sacred but as something practical, something that solved problems and made life easier. The baker's oven kept bread warm. The streetlamps kept the roads lit. The tanner's heating stones cured leather in half the time. Magic was infrastructure. It was the technology of this place, as natural to its inhabitants as combustion engines had been to his old world, and he had stopped being surprised by it years ago.

The street widened as he approached the center of town, the buildings growing taller and closer together, their facades decorated with signs that advertised the services within. A smith's hammer rang out from an open doorway, the sound rhythmic and steady, and John glanced inside as he passed. The smith was working on a sword—not a delver's weapon, something finer, with a fuller that caught the light and threw it back in a long, straight line. A heating crystal glowed in the forge, its surface inscribed with a Spiral so tight that the lines were almost invisible, and the smith's apprentice was using a modified Levitation weave to move ingots from the stack to the anvil, the magicules flowing from his hands in a steady stream that lifted the metal as easily as a hand might lift a feather. The boy could not have been more than fourteen, and the weave was so natural to him that he was doing it without visible concentration, his attention on the smith's instructions rather than the magic flowing through his fingers. He had probably learned it from his father, John thought, the way children in his own world learned to ride bicycles or tie their shoes. It was not magic. It was just something people did.

The people on the streets moved with purpose, their faces set in the particular expression of those who had work to do and not enough hours to do it. Delvers in mismatched armor, their weapons visible and their pouches full of crystals and monster parts, headed toward the guild hall to sell their hauls or toward the taverns to spend the profits. Merchants called out prices from stalls that lined the wider avenues, their voices competing with the clatter of carts and the low murmur of conversation that was the background noise of any town in any world. Children ran errands with small crystal lamps clipped to their belts, the light bouncing off the cobblestones as they darted between the legs of adults who did not slow down to avoid them. A woman at a well was using a Levitation weave to lift her water bucket, the same pattern the smith's apprentice had used but cruder, less refined, the kind of thing someone learned from watching rather than being taught. She was not a mage. She was just a woman who needed to get water from the well to her house, and she had found a way to do it that did not require carrying a heavy bucket up a flight of stairs.

John watched these things without stopping, without slowing, his eyes moving across the town with the same analytical attention he applied to a crystal's internal structure. He knew which alleys to take to avoid the crowded streets, which vendors would sell him food without asking where he had been, which guards could be bribed with a small crystal and which would take the crystal and report him anyway. He had been doing this for three years, coming up from the Deep when he needed supplies or when he had something to sell, and the town had become as familiar to him as the tunnels that led to his workshop. It was not home. He did not have a home, not in the sense that other people meant when they used the word. But it was a place he knew, a system he understood, and there was comfort in that even if there was no warmth.

He passed the guild hall without looking at it—a large stone building at the center of town, its doors open to admit the stream of delvers who came to register their finds and collect their bounties—and turned onto a narrower street that ran behind the main market. This was the Merchant's Quarter, though it was not called that by anyone who lived here. The buildings here were older, their facades less polished, and the people who moved through the narrow alleys were not the delvers and merchants who dominated the main streets but something else: fences, black market alchemists, dealers in goods that the guild preferred not to see. John had found his way here in his first year, desperate to sell a small haul of Aether crystals and too afraid of the guild's questions to use the official channels. He had been cheated, at first. He had learned. Now he moved through the Quarter with the same confidence he brought to his workshop, his feet finding the path that led to the one fence he trusted to ask no questions and pay a fair price.

Marek's tannery occupied the ground floor of a narrow building on Tanner's Row, a street that was named for the industry that had once dominated it and that still smelled of the curing agents and wet hide that gave the place its character. The smell was constant, a mixture of sharp chemicals and organic decay that made John's nose wrinkle even after three years of exposure. It was also a useful smell, he had learned, because it kept away the kind of customers who preferred to do business in places that did not smell like death and chemicals. The customers who came to Tanner's Row were the customers who did not want to be seen, and Marek had built a comfortable business serving them.

John pushed open the door and stepped inside. The tannery's interior was dim after the gray light of the street, lit by a few Aether crystals mounted on the walls and the dull glow of heating stones that lined the curing vats. The vats themselves were the size of small boats, filled with liquids that bubbled and steamed in the constant warmth, and the air was thick with the smells that had become Marek's signature: ammonia, tannin, the particular sweetness of alchemical preservatives that had been imported from a city three days' ride away. Marek himself was at the back of the room, a large man whose bulk had once been muscle but had softened over years of standing behind a counter. He was examining a pile of hides that a young woman had brought in, his quick eyes moving across the surface of each one with the practiced assessment of someone who had been buying and selling leather for longer than John had been alive.

He looked up when John entered, and for a moment his expression did not change. Then something shifted in his eyes—recognition, perhaps, or the particular alertness that came when a profitable customer appeared without warning. He finished his examination of the hides, named a price that made the young woman's face flicker with something that might have been satisfaction or might have been resignation, and sent her on her way with a small pouch of coins that she tucked into her belt before disappearing into the street. Then he turned to John, his hands wiping themselves on his apron in a motion that was more habit than necessity.

"You've been gone a while," Marek said. His voice was flat, without curiosity, the voice of a man who had learned that questions cost more than they returned.

John shrugged, the motion lifting his coat enough to remind himself of the weight in its compartments. "Deep work."

Marek nodded, the response as automatic as the shrug. He gestured toward the counter, a broad slab of wood that had been worn smooth by years of transactions, and John stepped forward. He did not sit. He did not lean. He stood with his hands at his sides, his face neutral, and waited for the ritual that had played out between them a dozen times before.

"You have something to sell?" Marek asked.

John reached into his pack and pulled out the bundle of Deep Viper scales, still wrapped in oiled cloth and moss, still secured with leather cord. He set it on the counter between them, and Marek's eyes tracked the movement with the same quick assessment he had given the hides, the same evaluation of weight and shape and the way the cloth bulged around whatever was inside. His hands did not reach for it. That was one of the things John liked about Marek. He waited. He let the seller show him what they had.

John untied the cord, unwrapped the cloth, and laid the scales out on the counter in a pattern he had arranged a hundred times in his mind: twelve scales, arranged in three rows of four, their surfaces catching the dim light of the Aether crystals and throwing it back in the shifting greens and golds that made Deep Viper scales so valuable. Marek's face did not change. His eyes did. They widened, just for a moment, before the professional mask settled back into place. He reached out and picked up one of the scales, turning it over in his hands, holding it up to the light, running his thumb across its surface with the same touch John had used when he first examined them.

"Deep Viper," Marek said. It was not a question.

"Full set. Pristine."

Marek set the scale down and picked up another, then another, his examination methodical, professional, the examination of a man who knew what he was looking at and was taking the time to confirm what his eyes had already told him. When he had examined all twelve, he stacked them in a neat pile and looked at John with an expression that was as close to respect as John had ever seen on his face.

"Full set," he repeated. "How long have you been holding onto these?"

"Long enough."

Marek's mouth twitched, something that might have been a smile or might have been the beginning of a calculation. "I'll give you eighty gold."

John had expected the number. Marek always started low, and eighty gold for a full set of Deep Viper scales was insultingly low, the kind of offer that was meant to test whether the seller knew what they had. John had known what he had from the moment he killed the viper. He had done the research, asked the questions, learned the prices that the guild paid for scales of this quality and the prices that fences like Marek charged when they resold them. He had been waiting for this moment for three years.

"One twenty," he said.

Marek's eyebrows rose. "One twenty? You could buy a house for one twenty."

"I don't need a house."

The fence picked up a scale again, turning it over in his hands as if the weight of it might tell him something the appearance had not. "One hundred. That's my final."

"One fifteen."

Marek set the scale down with a sigh that was almost theatrical. "One ten. And you let me have first refusal on whatever you bring up next."

John considered the offer. One hundred ten gold was more than he had expected, more than he had calculated as the upper bound of what Marek would pay. The scales were valuable, but they were also difficult to move—a full set of Deep Viper scales was the kind of thing that attracted attention, and attention was the one thing that men like Marek could not afford. He was taking a risk, buying them, and the price reflected that. But one hundred ten gold was enough. More than enough.

"One ten. Deal!" John said.

Marek's eyes flickered, and for a moment John thought he might push back, might try to extract a promise of exclusivity or a share of future finds. Then the fence looked at the scales again, at the perfect condition, at the money he would make when he sold them to the right buyer, and something in his expression softened into acceptance.

"Agreed," he said. He reached under the counter and pulled out a heavy wooden box, its surface scarred by years of use, and began counting gold coins onto the worn wood between them. They were not the small copper coins that most transactions used in Greymouth. They were gold, each one stamped with the seal of the kingdom that claimed this region, each one worth more than most delvers made in a month of hunting. Marek counted them out in stacks of ten, ten stacks, and John watched each coin fall without moving to touch them. When the last coin was in place, Marek pushed the pile across the counter.

John did not pick them up immediately. He stood, looking at the gold, and then at Marek, and then at the Deep Viper scales still stacked beside the coins.

"What's the slave market like these days?"

The question hung in the air between them. Marek's expression did not change—his face was too well-trained for that—but something in his posture shifted, a subtle recalibration that John had learned to recognize as the fence's way of processing unexpected information. Marek did not ask why. He did not ask who. He answered the question with the same flat professionalism he brought to every transaction.

"Velsa and Sons runs the main market in the Plaza of Chains. A good dungeon-adapted slave runs fifteen to fifty gold, depending on age, health, and skills. Collared slaves with guaranteed obedience are more expensive than untrained ones." He paused, his eyes moving across John's face with the same assessment he had given the scales. "Looking for a porter?"

John did not answer. He reached out, swept the coins into his pack, and tucked the empty leather pouch that had held the scales into his coat. The weight of the gold was heavier than he expected, denser than the crystals he usually carried, and he felt it settle against his hip as he turned toward the door.

"Pleasure doing business," Marek said to his back.

John paused at the threshold, his hand on the door, the gray light of the street filtering through the gaps in the frame. "Same," he said, and stepped out into the morning.
















John stepped out into the morning, and the door closed behind him with a solid thunk that cut off the tannery's smells and left him standing in the gray light of Tanner's Row with the weight of gold against his hip and the shape of his next destination already forming in his mind. He did not look back. There was no need. Marek would take the scales, would sell them to some wealthy merchant or high-ranking delver who wanted armor that could stop a monster's claws, and would never mention where they came from. That was the arrangement they had built over two years of transactions, the unspoken contract between a fence who asked no questions and a supplier who gave him no reason to. 

The street was quiet at this hour, the morning's business still concentrated in the main market rather than the narrow alleys of the Merchant's Quarter. John walked with his head down, his hands in the pockets of his coat, his footsteps steady on the worn cobblestones. The gold coins shifted in his pack with each step, a solid weight that reminded him of what he had just done and what he was about to do. One hundred ten gold was more money than most people in Greymouth saw in ten years. It was enough to buy a house, enough to outfit a party of delvers for a month-long expedition, enough to live on for a lifetime if a person was careful and wanted nothing more than to survive. He was not most people. He did not want a house. He did not want to live on the surface, watching the seasons change and the years pass and the world move on without him. He wanted to go back to the Deep, back to his workshop, back to the work that had consumed him for three years and showed no sign of releasing its hold. And to do that, he needed help. He needed a pair of hands that were not his own, a set of eyes that could watch the perimeter while he grew crystals, a body that could carry supplies and prepare food and perform the thousand small tasks that ate the hours he needed for his research. The gold in his pack was the means to that end, nothing more.

The Plaza of Chains occupied the northern edge of the Merchant's Quarter, a wide square that had been named for the iron chains that hung from the auction house's facade, great rusted loops that had been there for so long that no one remembered if they had ever served a purpose beyond decoration. The plaza itself was empty at this hour—the slave markets operated on a schedule that favored the afternoon, when the delvers had sold their hauls and the merchants had counted their profits and the crowds were thick enough to provide the anonymity that buyers and sellers both preferred—but the building at its center was open, its doors standing ajar to admit the morning light. John paused at the edge of the square, his eyes moving across the facade, the iron chains, the heavy wooden doors that had been reinforced with bands of enchanted steel. The building was three stories tall, its windows barred, its walls built from stone that had been quarried from the same hills that had given Greymouth its name. It looked like a fortress. It looked like a prison. It looked like what it was: a place where human beings were bought and sold, and where the magic that made that possible was as much a part of the architecture as the stone and the iron.

He crossed the square, his footsteps echoing off the wet cobblestones, and pushed open one of the heavy doors. The interior was cooler than the street, the air carrying the faint scent of incense that had been burned to mask the smells of the city. A clerk sat behind a desk near the entrance, a middle-aged woman with grey-streaked hair and the flat, professional courtesy of someone who had seen everything and stopped being surprised by any of it. She looked up when John entered, her eyes moving across his face, his clothes, the pack at his side, and found nothing that required more than the standard greeting.

"We're not open for viewing until noon," she said, her voice as flat as her expression. "If you're looking to buy, you'll need to come back."

John reached into his coat and pulled out a handful of gold coins, letting them clink against each other in his palm. The sound was louder than he expected in the quiet of the entrance hall, and the clerk's eyes flickered to the coins, then back to his face, then to the coins again.

"I'm not looking to browse," John said. "I have specific requirements. B-rank resistance to the deep, guaranteed discretion. I'm prepared to pay for the right candidate."

The clerk studied him for a moment longer, her expression unreadable. Then she nodded, rose from her desk, and gestured toward a door at the back of the hall. "This way. We have a few that might meet your needs. I'll bring the files."

The viewing room was small, furnished with a table and four chairs, its walls painted a pale cream that had been chosen to show dirt and make cleaning easier. John sat in one of the chairs, his pack on the floor beside him, and waited. The room had no windows—the barred openings he had seen from the outside belonged to the holding cells on the upper floors—and the only light came from an Aether crystal set into a sconce on the far wall, its glow steady and unchanging. He counted the seconds as he waited, measuring the time by the pulse of the unconscious Wave that still played across his skin, and had reached three hundred and forty-seven when the clerk returned with a stack of papers in her hands.

She set the papers on the table and spread them out, each one a single sheet of parchment covered in the precise, standardized handwriting of a bureaucracy that had been processing slaves for generations. John leaned forward, his eyes moving across the documents with the same analytical attention he applied to his experiment notes, and began to read.

The files were organized by rank, each sheet stamped with the letter that denoted the slave's resistance to magicule accumulation. Most were C-rank or lower, their names accompanied by notes that spoke of limited usefulness in the Deep, of the careful monitoring required to prevent residue sickness, of the short windows of productivity that made them unsuitable for anything beyond shallow delves. John set those aside without finishing them. He needed someone who could survive in his workshop, six kilometers deep, where the magicule concentration was high enough to kill an ordinary human in days. B-rank was the minimum. Anything less was a waste of gold.

There were three B-rank files. He read them in order, his finger tracing the lines of text as he absorbed the information with the same methodical efficiency he brought to a new pattern weave.

The first was a human man, twenty-two years old, his file marked with the seal of the guild that had certified his rank. Former delver, C-rank, sold to Velsa after a gambling debt default. The certification notes indicated that his resistance had been measured at B-rank through controlled exposure tests, the kind that subjected a subject to increasing concentrations of magicules while monitoring the residue accumulation in their channels. He had combat training, experience as a porter in deep delves, and the particular stillness of someone who had learned that movement drew attention. His price was sixty-five gold.

The second was a dwarf, older than the first by a decade, his file marked with the seal of a mining guild from a city John had never heard of. Sold for debt, the notes said, or perhaps for theft—the language was carefully ambiguous, the kind of phrasing that let a buyer imagine whatever circumstances made the transaction most palatable. His resistance was B-rank, his skills were heavy labor and tunnel navigation, and his price was fifty gold.

The third was a young woman. Demon heritage, according to the file, though the term was applied loosely in this world to anyone with enough non-human blood to manifest visible traits. Her skin was noted as light blue, her hair dark, her eyes an unremarkable brown that the file's author had not bothered to describe beyond the word "normal." She was twenty years old, and her resistance had been certified at B plus rank through the same controlled exposure tests that had validated the others. Domestic skills were listed in the file's second section: cooking, cleaning, basic alchemy, food preservation. No combat experience. No combat training. Her price was fifteen gold.

John set the files down and looked at the clerk, who had been standing by the door with the patient stillness of someone who had learned to wait.

"I'd like to see them. The human and the demon."

The clerk nodded and disappeared through the door, and John sat in the small room with the files spread before him and the weight of gold in his pack and the knowledge that he was about to do something that would change the shape of his life in ways he could not yet calculate. He did not allow himself to think about what that change might mean. He did not allow himself to think about anything except the variables: rank, skills, price, suitability. It was an engineering choice. He needed a solution that worked. Everything else was noise.

The door opened, and the clerk returned with two figures behind her. They entered the room in silence, their movements careful, their eyes downcast, their bodies arranged in the particular posture of people who had learned that looking at a buyer was an invitation to something they did not want to invite. The clerk gestured for them to stand in the center of the room, and they did, side by side, their hands at their sides, their feet planted on the worn floorboards.

John studied them.

The human was Kael, according to his file. He was broad-shouldered and scarred, his face marked by the particular weathering of someone who had spent years underground and then years in the holding cells of a slave market. He stood with the stillness of a man who had learned to be invisible, his eyes fixed on a point on the far wall, his breathing so controlled that John could barely see the rise and fall of his chest. His hands were large, calloused, the hands of someone who had carried loads and swung weapons and done the kind of work that left marks that did not fade. He was strong. He was trained. He was everything a person might want in a porter for the Deep.

The demon was Lilith. Her skin was the pale blue of a winter sky at dusk, a color that seemed to shift in the Aether crystal's light, and two thin horns curved back from her temples, their surfaces dark and polished. A tail hung motionless behind her, its tip curled inward in a way that suggested a habit of keeping it out of the way, and her hands were clean and well-kept, the hands of someone who had worked indoors, who had learned to cook and clean and preserve food, who had not been required to carry loads or swing weapons or do the kind of work that left scars. Her eyes were brown, as the file had said, but they were not the flat, empty eyes of someone who had been broken by her circumstances. They were watching. Not looking at him directly, not meeting his gaze, but watching, the way a prey animal watches a predator, waiting to see what would happen next.

John stood, walked around the table, and approached the two figures in the center of the room. He stopped a few feet away, close enough to see the details that the files had not captured, and addressed the human first.

"Kael."

The man's eyes flickered to him, then away. "Yes."

"You were a delver. C-rank. How deep did you go?"

A pause. The man's hands twitched, the first movement John had seen from him. "Two kilometers. Sometimes more, on a good day."

"And your resistance? The file says B-rank, but that's a certification, not a lived experience. How long could you stay at two kilometers before the residue became a problem?"

Kael's jaw tightened, the muscles working beneath the scarred skin. "Three days. Maybe four, if I pushed it. After that, the sickness would set in. Hallucinations, muscle spasms. I never pushed past that."

John nodded, filing the information away. Three days was not enough. His workshop was six kilometers deep, and the magicule concentration at that depth would accelerate the residue accumulation far beyond what a surface tunnel at two kilometers could produce. Kael's B-rank resistance would buy him perhaps a day, maybe two, before the sickness began. After that, he would be useless. After that, he would be dying.

He turned to the demon. "Lilith."

Her voice was low, careful. "Yes."

"You have no combat training. The file says domestic skills. Cooking, cleaning, basic alchemy. Where did you learn those?"

The answer came without hesitation, as if she had rehearsed it a hundred times. "I was purchased for a surface home. A merchant who wanted a housekeeper. He taught me what he needed me to know."

"And your resistance? B plus rank certification. Have you ever been in the Deep?"

A pause, longer this time. "No. The merchant who owned me did not believe in taking his property into dangerous places."

John watched her as she spoke, his eyes moving across her face, her hands, the tail that was still curled tight against her legs. She was nervous—he could see it in the way her shoulders were set, the way her fingers pressed against each other, the way her breathing had become shallower as he questioned her—but she was not afraid. Or she was afraid, and she had learned to hold it behind a mask that was nearly perfect. He could not tell which, and that was interesting.

He stepped back, giving them space, and let his eyes move between them. Kael was the obvious choice. He was strong, trained, experienced in the Deep. His resistance was certified, his skills were directly applicable, and his price—sixty-five gold—was within John's budget. He could carry loads, stand watch, fight if he had to. He was the kind of assistant that any delver would want for an expedition into dangerous territory.

But John was not going on an expedition. He was going back to his workshop, to the life he had built six kilometers underground, to the research that consumed his days and would consume his assistant's days as well. Kael's combat training was useless in a workshop. His experience as a porter was useful only until the supplies were unloaded. His three-day tolerance for the Deep's residue meant that he would need to surface regularly, which was impossible, or undergo a purge regimen that John would have to teach him, which would take time John did not have. And there was something in the man's eyes, that flicker of hope or calculation, that made John uneasy. Kael was watching him, too, and John could not tell what he was watching for.

Lilith was the opposite. She had no combat training, no experience in the Deep, no obvious skills beyond the domestic tasks that John could perform himself if he had the time. But her B+ rank was not merely a certification—it was a baseline, a starting point. Living in the Deep would not degrade her tolerance; it would train it. The constant magicule exposure, managed carefully and paired with the purge techniques he had already developed, would push her resistance higher over time. A B+ today could become A-rank in months, maybe less. Kael's tolerance was already worn, his channels scarred by years of accumulation his body had never fully cleared. Lilith's were clean. Untouched. Malleable.

And her skills—cooking, cleaning, food preservation, basic alchemy—were exactly the skills that would free John from the tasks that ate his hours. He did not need a porter to carry his supplies. He needed someone to cook his food while he grew crystals. He needed someone to clean his workshop while he hunted. He needed someone to watch the perimeter, to reset the traps, to perform the thousand small tasks that kept a home running in a place where there were no shops or servants or any of the infrastructure that made surface life possible. And with time and exposure, she would only become more useful—her growing resistance extending her endurance, her deepening familiarity with the Deep making her more capable of handling the work he could not delegate to anyone else.

And she was cheaper. Fifteen gold left him ninety-five for supplies, for materials, for the things he would need to continue his research without interruption.

He turned to the clerk. "I'd like to speak with them separately. The demon first."

The clerk nodded and gestured to Kael, who followed her out of the room without looking back. The door closed, and John and Lilith were alone in the small, cream-walled room with the Aether crystal casting its steady light across the table and the chairs and the files that still lay open where John had left them.

He gestured to one of the chairs. "Sit."

She sat, her movements careful, her hands folded in her lap, her tail curling around her legs in the same unconscious gesture he had noticed before. John took the chair across from her, not close enough to be threatening but close enough to watch her face, her eyes, the small movements that would tell him what her words would not.

"You've been sold before," he said. It was not a question.

"Yes. Twice."

"The file says the first owner died. What happened to the second?"

She hesitated, and John watched the calculation behind her eyes—how much to say, how much to hold back, how to answer without giving away something that could be used against her. "He did not die. He lost interest. I was returned to Velsa when his business failed."

"Returned. Not sold."

Her hands tightened in her lap, just for a moment. "He had no use for a housekeeper when he had no house."

John nodded, filing the information away. The merchant had failed, had liquidated his assets, and Lilith had been part of that liquidation. It was a common enough story. Slaves were property, and property was bought and sold and discarded when it no longer served a purpose. The file did not mention any disciplinary notes, any attempts to escape or disobey or cause the kind of trouble that would have lowered her value. She had been a good slave. She had done what she was told. And when she was no longer needed, she had been returned like a tool that had outlived its usefulness.

"Why should I choose you over Kael?" John asked.

The question was blunt, deliberate, designed to see how she reacted. Some slaves would plead, would list their virtues, would promise anything to escape the market and its endless cycle of viewing rooms and buyers who looked at them like livestock. Others would freeze, would retreat into the stillness that Kael had shown, would give the questioner nothing because nothing was safer than anything.

Lilith met his eyes for the first time.

"I can cook," she said. "I can clean. I know how to preserve food, how to treat leather, how to organize a workspace so that everything is where it needs to be. I can learn new skills. I am quiet. I do not cause trouble." She paused, and something flickered in her expression—not fear, not hope, something else. "I have been in this market for eight months. Buyers come for fighters. They come for porters. They come for girls who are pretty and young and willing to be whatever the buyer wants them to be. I am none of those things. You are the first person who has asked to see me since the merchant's estate was settled."

John waited. There was more, he could see it, the words gathering behind her eyes like magicules before a weave.

"I will not cause you trouble," she said, and her voice was steady now, the careful mask settling back into place. "I will do what you tell me. I will learn what you teach me. I will not ask questions. I only want—" She stopped, and her hands tightened again, and for a moment John thought she would not finish the sentence. Then she did, her voice lower than before. "I only want to not be in this place anymore."

The silence stretched between them. John looked at her hands, still tight in her lap, at the tail that had coiled so close to her legs that it was almost invisible, at the horns that curved back from her temples like the beginnings of a crown that had never been finished. She was twenty years old, the file said. She had been in this market for eight months. Before that, she had been the property of a merchant who had taught her to cook and clean and preserve food, who had not believed in arming his property, who had returned her when his business failed. Before that, she had been sold to him by the same house that was selling her now, and before that—the file did not say. The file never said.

“You understand the conditions,” John said. It was not a question.

Lilith’s eyes did not leave his face. “Yes.”

He studied her for a long moment, his eyes moving across her face, her hands, the careful stillness of her body. She was afraid. He could see it now, in the way her breath caught at the mere mention of the Deep, in the slight tremor in her fingers, in the tension that held her shoulders rigid. Six kilometers down, the magicule concentration would kill an ordinary human in days. Her B+ certification said she could endure it, but certification was not the same as experience. She might sicken. She might not recover. The work would be hard, unrelenting, the dungeon indifferent to fatigue or illness. One mistake—hers or his—could end them both.

She was not refusing. She was not bargaining. She was accepting, because the alternative was to go back to the holding cells and wait for another buyer who would never come, to spend more months in the market, to be returned again and again until she was old enough and worn enough that even the fifty‑gold price tag was too much for anyone to pay.

"Stand up," John said.

She stood, her movements quick, her hands still folded in front of her. He walked around her, his eyes on the collar that circled her throat—a band of enchanted iron, inscribed with the Anchor, Cage, and Lock patterns that would bind her to him. The collar was dull, unpolished, the patterns almost invisible against the dark metal. He had seen collars like it before, on the slaves who worked in the deeper tunnels of the dungeon, carrying loads that no free man would carry, doing work that no free man would do. He had never thought about them, not really. They were part of the landscape of this world, as natural as the streetlamps and the heating stones and the woman at the well with her Levitation weave. They were infrastructure. They were technology. They were a solution to a problem that he had not needed to solve until now.

He stopped in front of her and met her eyes again. "I will tell you what I need. You will do it. If you have questions, you will ask them. If you see something you do not understand, you will not speak of it to anyone. The collar will ensure that, but I prefer to be clear about expectations."

She nodded, her face still, her eyes still on his.

"I'll take the demon," he said to the clerk, who had been waiting by the door with the patient stillness of someone who had learned to be invisible. "Fifteen gold."

The clerk nodded, her expression unchanged, and gestured for Lilith to follow her out of the room. John watched them go, watched the demon's tail uncurl just slightly as she walked, watched her hands unclench and relax at her sides. Then he sat back down, pulled the stack of gold from his pack, and began counting out the coins.

Fifteen gold. The pathetic price of a B plus rank dungeon diver slave with domestic skills, a demon with no combat training, a woman who had been in this market for eight months and had stopped expecting anyone to want her. Fifteen gold was more than most people in Greymouth earned in a year. It was around a seventh of what he had received for the Deep Viper scales. The street outside Velsa and Sons was empty, the morning's gray light pressing down on the cobblestones with the weight of an overcast sky that had not yet decided whether to burn off or settle into rain. He turned left at the end of the street, away from the residential district where most buyers would take a new slave to settle into their household, and toward the clatter and smell of the morning market. Lilith followed, her feet finding the same cobblestones his had just left, her pace matching his without being told. The collar was a dull weight against her throat, warm from the residual magic of the binding, and she could feel the patterns pressed into her skin like the memory of a hand that had been there too long. She did not touch it. Touching it was forbidden, and the collar would remind her of that if she forgot, would send a jolt of cold through her nerves that was not quite pain but was close enough to serve as a lesson.

The market street opened before them as they rounded the corner, a wide thoroughfare lined with stalls and carts and the temporary tables of merchants who came to Greymouth for the morning trade and were gone by afternoon. The noise was immediate—voices calling prices, the clatter of carts on cobblestones, the ring of a smith's hammer from a forge that had been built into the ground floor of a stone building at the street's midpoint. John moved through the crowd with the ease of someone who knew this space, who had walked it a dozen times before and had learned which stalls to approach and which to avoid. He did not look at Lilith, did not check to see if she was still following, but he adjusted his pace to something she could match without hurrying, and when the crowd pressed close, he angled his body to create a gap that she could slip through behind him. She noticed. She did not know what to do with the noticing, so she filed it away with the other small observations she had made since the binding: that he did not speak to her unnecessarily, that he did not watch her the way buyers watched their purchases to ensure they were not stealing or running or thinking, that he moved through the market with the same focus he had brought to the auction house, as if the world were a problem to be solved and he was simply working through the variables.

The carpenter's stall was at the far end of the market, a wide table stacked with furniture. John stopped in front of the stall and looked at the selection. Bed frames, chairs, tables. The carpenter looked up from his work, his eyes moving across John's face, his coat, the gold coin that John had already pulled from his pack and placed on the table between them. He picked up the coin, bit it with the automatic gesture of a man who had been cheated once and had learned the lesson, and nodded. "Deliver?"

"To the residential district. I'll have more to add to the load before you go."

The carpenter nodded again, set the coin in a leather pouch at his belt, and added the bed frame to a growing pile of goods that were waiting for the midday cart. John moved on, and Lilith followed.

She was watching him now, not openly, but with the particular attention of someone who had learned that survival depended on understanding the people who held her collar. She had learned to read buyers, had learned to see the shape of a person's wants in the way they looked at the slaves on offer, had learned to make herself small and quiet and unremarkable so that she would not attract the kind of buyer who wanted something more than a cook or a cleaner.

John wanted something, she could see that. He had come to the auction house with gold in his pack and a purpose behind his eyes, and he had chosen her over a trained delver with combat experience and a higher price tag. But he did not look at her the way buyers looked at slaves they intended to use for the things that were not listed in the files. He did not look at her at all, except when he needed to confirm she was still there, and even then his gaze was the same flat assessment he had given the bed frame and the mattress—a check for fit, for function, for whether the thing he had acquired would serve its purpose.

She did not know if this was better or worse than what she had expected. She did not know if it was better or worse than the merchant who had wanted her to be invisible, who had not looked at her either, but who had looked at the space she occupied in his house as a problem that had been solved. She had been a solution to a problem for that man, too, and she had learned to cook and clean and preserve food because those were the skills he needed, and she had done them well enough that when he died, his estate had returned her to Velsa in good condition, with no marks on her file and no reduction in her price. That had been a kind of pride, she thought, though she was not sure if pride was something she was allowed to have.

The clothier's stall was set up against the wall of a building that had been whitewashed once, years ago, and had faded to a soft gray that matched the sky. Bolts of cloth were stacked on tables and hung from ropes strung between iron rings set into the stone, and a woman with sharp eyes and faster hands was measuring a length of wool for a customer who wanted a coat that would keep out the damp of the coastal winters. "Two sets for her," he said. "Work clothes. Whatever fits."

The clothier looked at Lilith, her eyes moving across the demon's shoulders, her waist, the length of her arms. She pulled a measuring tape from her belt—a strip of leather marked with symbols that glowed faintly, a detection pattern that recorded dimensions without the need for repeated measurements—and gestured for Lilith to hold out her arms. Lilith did, and the clothier ran the tape across her shoulders, down her sides, along her arms, her legs. The symbols pulsed with each measurement, recording the numbers into the leather, and when the clothier was satisfied, she turned to the rack and began pulling clothes with the efficiency of someone who had done this a thousand times.

"Good bones," the clothier said, not to Lilith but to John, as if the demon were not there. "Strong shoulders. She'll last."

John did not respond to the comment. He watched the clothier select a shirt of heavy cotton, trousers of reinforced wool, a coat of oiled canvas that would shed water and resist the damp. He did not look at Lilith to see if she approved of the choices, did not ask her opinion, did not treat the selection of her clothes as anything other than a transaction between himself and the seller. When the clothier held up a second shirt—a softer fabric, a lighter color—John nodded without comment, and the two sets were folded and wrapped in brown paper and added to the growing pile of goods that would be delivered to the residential district.

He paid. He turned. He walked to the next stall without waiting to see if Lilith was following.

She was. She always would be, now. The collar ensured it, and she had made her peace with that eight months ago, in the holding cells, when she had stopped hoping for a buyer who would see her as something other than a problem to be solved or a tool to be used. The merchant had used her as a housekeeper. Velsa had used her as inventory. This man—this boy, really, though his eyes were older than his face—would use her for whatever he had bought her for, and she would do it, because the collar would not let her do anything else, and because she had learned that resistance was a luxury for people who had something to resist for.

She watched him move through the market, watched him buy led ingots from a metalsmith whose forge was lined with heating crystals that glowed a steady orange, watched him buy salt and preserved meat from a merchant who recognized him and did not ask questions, watched him buy a washbasin and towels and blankets from a woman. He did not explain any of these purchases. He did not tell her where they were going or what they would do when they got there. He simply bought, and she followed, and the pile of goods at the residential district grew with each stop until there was enough to fill a cart twice over.

She almost asked, at the clothier's, when he had not looked at the clothes she was meant to wear, had not considered that she might have preferences or needs or any of the small dignities that made a life bearable. But she did not. The collar was warm against her throat, and she could feel the patterns waiting, and she had learned years ago that questions were a privilege she could not afford.

The gathering point was in the residential district where the carpenter's delivery cart had deposited its load an hour before.
