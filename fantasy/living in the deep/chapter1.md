The light in his hands faded to nothing, and the workshop settled into the familiar rhythm of torch flicker and moss-glow. John sat for a long while in the silence, the weight of the gun across his knees, his shadow stretching and shrinking with the flames. When the last torch began to gutter, he stood, placed the weapon on its rack beside his bed, and lay down without undressing. Sleep came not as a release but as a slow dimming of consciousness, the way a forge cools after the bellows fall still—residual heat bleeding into the dark until there was nothing left but the slow pulse of his own unconscious Wave.

The morning came cold.

He woke to the particular chill that crept into the workshop during the deep hours, when even the dungeon's breathing seemed to slow and the ambient magicules thinned enough that the temperature dropped by a few precious degrees. His eyes opened on darkness that was not quite absolute—the luminescent moss on the north wall had faded to a pale glow, and the last torch had died sometime in the night, leaving only the faint blue-green of the spring-water trickling over glowing stones in the corner. He lay still for a moment, cataloguing his body: the ache in his shoulders from three days of sustained weaving, the hollow hunger in his stomach that had become so constant he barely registered it, the slight pressure behind his eyes that meant his internal residue was creeping toward the threshold where active purge would be necessary again. Then he swung his legs off the bed and began the day.

The routines had worn themselves into his muscles over three years, as automatic as the Wave that pulsed across his skin. He checked the perimeter traps first—a circuit that took twenty minutes through the tunnels he had mapped and marked, his footsteps falling in patterns that avoided the loose stones he had deliberately left as acoustic triggers. Three of the bells had been disturbed: one by a small tunnel scavenger that had left tracks like a child's handprint in the dust, two by nothing he could identify. He reset them, tightened the sinew cords, tested each trigger with the careful pressure of a man who had learned that complacency killed faster than any monster. The crystal experiment in the side chamber—a slow-growth attempt using a new medium ratio of crushed carapace to Aether powder—showed no change from the day before, the seed still clouded with the particular opacity that preceded failure. He made a mental note to discard it when he returned.

The food stores were worse than he remembered.

He knelt before the chest in the workshop's southeast corner, lifting the lid on hinges of cured leather, and ran his fingers through the contents. Salted Rockback strips, enough for perhaps four days if he rationed. Dried Wisp Caps, a handful. A small cache of preserved roots he had traded for three months ago, now shriveled and beginning to show spots of mold he scraped away with his thumb. The Deep Viper meat he had butchered after killing the snake—his last major hunt before the three-day weave—was gone, consumed during the recovery days when he had eaten everything in sight to fuel his body's repair. What remained would not last the week.

He closed the chest and sat back on his heels, staring at the wall where the crater from the gun's first test still marked the stone. The silence pressed in, familiar and heavy. He had known this was coming. The math had been clear for weeks: the crystal growth that made everything else possible consumed time he could not afford to lose, and the hunting that kept him alive consumed time he could not spend on growth. The workshop required maintenance, the traps required attention, the notebooks demanded documentation, and his body demanded sleep and food and the occasional active purge that left him hollow and useless for hours afterward. One person, no matter how efficient, could not sustain all of it simultaneously.

He pulled his notebook from the table and flipped past the pages of crystal diagrams, past the test results and efficiency calculations, to a blank spread near the back. With the stylus that never left his hand, he began to list what needed doing.

*Hunt—Deep Viper territory, four hours minimum return, meat for ten days if successful.*
*Reset east tunnel traps—three hours including travel, overdue by two weeks.*
*Growth medium preparation—new batch requires two hours mixing, settling time twelve hours.*
*Crystal growth attempt—seventy-two hours continuous, no other tasks possible.*
*Workshop cleaning—mold on food chest, dust on tables, torch fat rendering.*
*Notebook transcription—seven incomplete experiments unlogged.*
*Active purge—one hour, leaves residual weakness four hours.*
*Repair south tunnel alarm—broken since last Rockback incursion, requires new sinew.*

He filled two pages, front and back, the cramped handwriting growing smaller as he added items he had been postponing for weeks. When he finished, he sat back and let the list rest on the table before him. The numbers were not abstract. Each task represented hours he did not have, days he could not spare, choices he had been avoiding because the answer was not one he wanted to write down.

He pulled a fresh page from the stack he had made from dried monster hide, smoothed it flat, and wrote three lines.

*Hire help from surface. Questions. No guarantee of discretion. Knowledge spreads.*

*Train an assistant. Requires trust not earned. Requires time not available. Requires someone willing to live here.*

*Acquire guaranteed loyalty. Slave. B-rank or higher for residue tolerance. Discretion guaranteed by collar. Expensive but viable.*

He stared at the words for a long moment, watching the torchlight catch the edges of the ink. The first option was suicide—the surface world would ask where a seventeen-year-old had found the resources to hire permanent help, and questions led to discoveries, and discoveries led to the weapon on his rack and the notebooks filled with patterns no one else had ever written down. The second was impossible—he had no one to trust, no time to build trust even if he had, and the mere act of teaching would consume the hours he was trying to save. The third was a transaction. Clean. Defined. The collar that bound a slave to a master was absolute; the binding patterns were old magic, well-understood, as reliable as the Anchor weaves that held his barrel together. A collared slave could not speak of what they saw, could not betray, could not even conceive of disobedience in a way that mattered. The system existed because it worked. He did not have to like it to use it.

He circled the third option with a single stroke of the stylus, then closed the notebook and stood.

The stores chest drew him again, this time not for food but for the leather sack he kept at the bottom, wrapped in oilcloth to protect its contents from moisture. He lifted it out, untied the cord, and let the cloth fall away. The Deep Viper scales spilled into his palm, each one the size of his thumbnail, layered like the plates of ancient armor, their surface catching the torchlight with the dull iridescence of oil on water. He had killed the snake on a hunt six weeks ago, before the three-day weave that had consumed his reserves and left him scrambling. It had been a good kill—a clean shot from twenty meters, the gun's first real test against a living B-rank threat, the bullet punching through the Viper's skull before it could strike. He had butchered it with care, preserving every scale, every fang, every scrap of venom sac for trade. The scales alone were worth enough to keep a family in Greymouth for a year. For his purposes, they would buy him what he needed and leave enough for supplies besides.

He wrapped them again, tucked the sack into his coat, and stood in the center of his workshop with the weight of the decision settling into his bones. The gun hung on its rack beside his bed, the crystal still seated in its housing, the Anchor weaves pulsing faintly with the rhythm of his own unconscious Wave. He looked at it, at the crater in the wall, at the notebooks filled with patterns and efficiencies and the slow accumulation of a language no one else spoke. He looked at the food chest with its four days of rations, at the traps he had not reset, at the crystal experiment that was already failing for lack of attention.

Then he walked to the entrance of his workshop, pulled the hide aside, and stepped into the tunnel that would take him to the surface.
