# The Artificer of the Deep

## Chapter 3: The Long Silence

The cavern's light had not changed.

Lilith woke to the same dim glow that had surrounded her when she fell asleep—torchlight flickering in iron sconces, the cool blue pulse of glowing stones, the soft green breath of luminescent moss on the northern wall. There was no dawn here, no gradual brightening to mark the passage from night to day. The dungeon simply *was*, unchanging and eternal, and she had slept for what felt like hours but could have been minutes or days for all she could tell.

Her body ached. The descent had carved its memory into her muscles—her shoulders tight from the bed frame rails, her lower back sore from the pack, her calves burning from the endless downward slope of the tunnels. But the residue that had pressed against her channels, that fog in her thoughts and weight in her limbs, was gone. She had practiced the purge weave through the night, had held the Net and Wave in her mind as she drifted in and out of sleep, and her body felt clean in a way that surprised her. Not refreshed—she was still tired, still sore—but *clear*. The dungeon's pressure still surrounded her, the magicules still thick in the air, but she could feel her own channels processing them, the residue building slowly rather than flooding in all at once.

She sat up. The bed frame creaked beneath her, the mattress shifting as she swung her legs over the side. Her tail uncurled from around her legs, stiff from hours in the same position, and she stretched it out behind her, feeling the joints pop. The washbasin sat on the floor beside her bed, filled with water she had drawn from the spring the night before, and she splashed her face, rubbed her eyes, and tried to make sense of the cavern in the morning light that was not morning at all.

John was already awake.

He sat at the larger table, his back to her, his shoulders hunched over a notebook. The stylus in his hand moved in short, precise strokes, and she could see the stack of papers beside him—notes, diagrams, the accumulated work of three years compressed into pages that were yellowing at the edges from the dungeon's damp. He did not turn when she stood, did not acknowledge that she had woken, and she stood for a moment in the center of the cavern, uncertain, before the memory of his instructions settled into place.

*Tomorrow I will show you how I need things organized.*

She walked toward him, her footsteps soft on the stone floor, and stopped a few meters from the table. He continued writing, his stylus scratching across the page, and she waited, her hands clasped in front of her, her tail curling around her legs in the familiar gesture of patience she had learned in the merchant's house.

He finished the sentence, set the stylus down, and turned to look at her. His face was the same as it had been yesterday—flat, assessing, giving nothing away. The unconscious Wave pulsed across his skin, invisible to human eyes but visible to hers in the dim light, and she watched it for a moment before he spoke.

"You slept."

It was not a question. She nodded anyway.

"The purge weave?"

"I practiced it. Through the night. I think—I think it helped. The residue is lower than it should be."

He studied her for a moment, his eyes moving across her face, her hands, the way she held herself. Then he nodded, a small, almost invisible movement. "Good. You will need to practice it every day. Several times a day, at first, until it becomes automatic. The longer you wait between purges, the more residue accumulates, and the harder it is to clear."

He stood, pushing his chair back from the table, and walked toward the far end of the cavern. She followed, her feet carrying her after him without conscious thought, and he began to show her the workshop.

The tour was not gentle. He did not explain why things were where they were, did not offer stories about how he had acquired this tool or carved that shelf. He simply pointed, named, and moved on. The spring was at the northern end of the cavern, a crack in the stone wall that wept water into a basin he had carved from a single block of stone. The water was cold and clean, he said, and it never ran dry. The torches burned rendered monster fat, and the fat was stored in clay pots near the entrance, along with spare wicks and the iron rods he used to relight them when they guttered out. The glowing stones were natural, chipped from a vein two levels up, and they would last for years before their light began to fade. The moss needed no tending—it grew where it wanted, and he had learned not to fight it.

The tools were arranged on the larger table, each one in its place, each one worn from use. Carving knives with blades thin from sharpening, their handles wrapped in leather that had darkened with sweat and residue. Calipers made from monster bone, the joints reinforced with wire he had pulled from a broken trap. A set of small vials filled with powders and liquids, each one labeled in his cramped handwriting—*Crushed Ember Hound bone*, *Powdered Aether crystal*, *Wisp Cap slurry*, *Chitin Crawler carapace*. A mortar and pestle carved from dark stone, its interior stained with the residues of a dozen different substances.

He showed her the food stores next, opening the chest near his bed to reveal the salted meat and dried mushrooms and preserved roots that had kept him alive for three years. The chest was organized with the same precision as the tools—meat on the left, mushrooms on the right, roots at the bottom, each wrapped in oiled cloth and separated by layers of dried moss to prevent mold. He explained the preservation process in the same flat, clinical voice he used for everything: salt from the surface, alchemical agents extracted from Wisp Caps, a drying rack he had built near the spring where the constant moisture kept the air from becoming too dry and cracking the meat.

"The salt is running low," he said, closing the chest. "I will need to buy more on my next surface trip. Until then, we ration."

She nodded, filing the information away.

The perimeter traps were last. He led her to the eastern entrance—the tunnel where she had heard the Chitin Crawler scrape against stone on their first night—and showed her the simple alarms he had set. String stretched across the tunnel at ankle height, connected to clusters of hollow chitin that clattered when disturbed. Bells hung on monofilament sinew, positioned so that the slightest brush would set them ringing. Stones balanced on narrow ledges, designed to shift under weight and tumble down in a cascade that would echo through the tunnels.

"Check them twice a day," he said. "Morning and evening, or as close as you can judge. If a trap has been triggered, reset it. If the string is broken, replace it. If a bell is missing, find it or make a new one."

He showed her the supply of spare parts—a leather pouch hanging near the entrance, filled with lengths of string, spare bells, chitin clusters, and the small stones he used to weight the edges of the hide curtain that covered the workshop's main entrance.

"Why not magical traps?" she asked. "They would be more effective. More reliable."

John looked at her, and for a moment she thought he would not answer. Then he spoke, his voice flat. "Magical traps require maintenance I cannot always provide. The crystals degrade. The battery crystals deplete. Down here the residue accumulates fast and interferes. A bell and a string work the same way every time, regardless of the magicule concentration or my own exhaustion."

Back at the table, he pulled a stack of papers from a waterproofed leather folder and set them in front of her. The pages were worn, the edges soft from handling, and the diagrams on their surfaces were drawn with the same precise, cramped hand she had seen in his notebooks. Patterns. Primitives. Line, Spiral, Net, Wave, Point, Arc, Chevron, Zigzag, Spur, Lens, Möbius, Cage, Fork, Spike, Anchor, Spring, Sieve, Star, Braid, Hook, Pinwheel, Dome, Ribbon, Prong, Scatter, Lock. Each one drawn in careful detail, with notes in the margins about its shape, its input and output, its effect on magicules.

"Learn these," he said. "You will need them for the purge weave, and for other things."

She looked at the stack, at the diagrams, at the dense blocks of text that accompanied each one. There were dozens of pages, hundreds of patterns, and she had never seen most of them before. The merchants who had owned her had not believed in educating their property, and the holding cells at Velsa and Sons had offered nothing but the blank walls and the cold iron of her collar.

"I don't—" She stopped, her throat tight. "I don't know how to read. Not well. The merchant taught me numbers, for measuring ingredients, but letters—"

John stared at her for a long moment. Then he reached out, pulled the stack of papers back toward himself, and flipped through them until he found a page near the bottom. The diagram on it was simpler than the others—a single straight line, with arrows at each end to show the direction of flow.

"Line," he said. "Kinetic force. Linear acceleration. The most basic pattern. Every other pattern is built from this one, or modifies it, or combines it with others."

He set the page in front of her, his finger tapping the diagram. "You do not need to read the words to understand the shapes. Look at the pattern. Trace it with your finger. Feel how the lines connect, how the magicules would flow if you injected them at the input."

She looked at the page. The line was straight, unbroken, its ends marked with small circles—input on the left, output on the right. She reached out and traced it with her fingertip, feeling the grooves where his stylus had pressed into the paper. The shape was simple, almost insultingly simple, and she could feel the magicules in her own channels responding to the memory of the pattern, wanting to flow the way the diagram showed.

"I can try," she said.

He nodded, set the stack of papers beside her, and returned to his table. She sat on her bed, the pages spread out before her, and began to study.

The purge weave practice came an hour later.

John had finished whatever he was writing and walked to her bed, gesturing for her to sit cross-legged on the mattress. He sat behind her, the same position he had used the night before, and placed his hands on her shoulders. She tensed at the touch—not from fear, exactly, but from the memory of other hands, other owners, other moments when someone had touched her without warning. But his hands were still, not gripping, not exploring, just resting against her coat, and after a moment she forced herself to relax.

"Cast the weave," he said. "Net and Wave. Show me what you practiced."

She closed her eyes and reached inward.

The Net came first—the sieve, the structure, the interlocking grid of lines that would catch the residue in her channels. She had practiced it through the night, had traced its shape in her mind a hundred times, and the pattern formed more easily than she expected, the magicules in her channels aligning themselves into the grid without conscious effort. The Wave came next, the pulse that would loosen the residue and push it out, and she layered it over the Net, feeling the two patterns interlock the way John had shown her.

But something was wrong. The Wave was too fast, its rhythm stuttering against the Net instead of flowing through it. The residue in her channels shifted but did not release, caught in the grid but not pushed out, and she felt the pressure build behind her eyes as the patterns began to interfere with each other instead of working together.

"Slow," John said. His voice was calm, clinical, and she felt his hands press slightly against her shoulders. "The Wave is not a hammer. It is a tide. It rises and falls. Let it find its own rhythm."

She tried again, slowing the pulse, letting the Wave stretch and contract like the dungeon's own breathing. The Net held steady, the grid firm, and this time when the Wave moved through it, the residue shifted. Not much—a trickle, barely enough to notice—but she felt it leave her channels, felt the fog in her thoughts lift by a fraction.

"Better. Again."

She practiced. Again and again, her hands resting on her knees, her eyes closed, her mind focused on the interlocking patterns. John corrected her—the angle of her hands, the flow of magicules through her fingers, the way she held the Net too rigid and the Wave too loose. His voice was flat, never encouraging, never frustrated, just *correcting*, and she found that she preferred it to the alternative. The merchant had praised her when she did well, had smiled and called her a good girl, and she had learned to hate his praise because it was always followed by something she did not want to give. John gave her nothing. He simply told her what she was doing wrong, and she fixed it, and the weave grew stronger.

After an hour, she could feel the residue clearing in earnest—not as efficiently as when John had cast the weave on her, but enough to make a difference. The fog in her thoughts was thinner. The weight in her limbs was lighter. She was still tired, still sore, but she was *cleaner*, and the relief of it brought tears to her eyes that she blinked away before they could fall.

"Enough," John said, removing his hands. "You will practice again tonight. And tomorrow. And every day until it becomes automatic."

He stood, walked to his table, and picked up his pack. She watched him check the contents—dried meat, water skin, the rounded block of wood and leather and metal that had killed the Shardprowler and the Deep Lurker. He slid the weapon into a compartment in his coat, adjusted the strap of his pack, and turned to face her.

"I am going to hunt. You will stay here. Tend the workshop, practice the weave, study the notes. Do not leave this cavern unless you are in mortal danger—and I mean mortal, not uncomfortable. I will return before the day ends."

He did not wait for a response. He walked to the hide curtain, pushed it aside, and stepped through into the darkness beyond. The leather fell back into place with a soft rustle, and the sound of his footsteps faded into the tunnel, swallowed by the stone and the silence.

Lilith sat on her bed, the stack of pattern notes beside her, the workshop spread out around her in the dim, unchanging light. The torches flickered. The moss glowed. The water dripped from the spring into its stone basin, a steady rhythm that was the only sound in the cavern.

She was alone.

She had never been alone before. Not truly alone, without walls or guards or the presence of other slaves. The merchant's house had been crowded with servants, with the merchant's family, with the endless stream of visitors who came to buy and sell and trade. The holding cells at Velsa and Sons had been filled with the breathing of other slaves, the rustle of their clothes, the soft sounds of their misery. Even during the descent, John had been there, walking ahead of her, his presence a constant pressure against the edge of her awareness.

Now there was nothing. Just the cavern, and the silence, and the slow pulse of the dungeon's magicule tide pressing against her skin.

She sat for a long moment, her hands folded in her lap, her tail curled around her legs. Then she stood, walked to the table where John's tools were arranged, and began to clean.












She stood, walked to the table where John's tools were arranged, and began to clean.

The work was familiar, almost soothing. Her hands moved across the wood, gathering the scattered crystal fragments into a small pile near the edge of the table, wiping away the residues that had dried in thin, crusted layers. The tools she arranged by size—knives together, calipers beside them, the vials of powders and liquids lined up in neat rows that matched the labels she could not read but could recognize by the colors of their contents. The mortar and pestle she set at the corner of the table, its stained interior wiped clean with a rag dampened in water from the spring.

She had done this work before. Not in a dungeon, not with tools meant for cutting crystals and measuring magicule flow, but the motions were the same—the wiping, the sorting, the careful placement of each object in its proper place. The merchant had expected cleanliness, had inspected the kitchen each morning with the same flat assessment John had turned on the workshop, and she had learned to clean so thoroughly that there was nothing left to inspect. Her hands remembered. Her body remembered. And as she worked, the tension in her shoulders began to ease.

The silence pressed against her ears, broken only by the drip of the spring and the soft rustle of her own movements. She was not used to silence. The merchant's house had been filled with noise—cooks shouting, servants running, the merchant's children laughing and crying and arguing. The holding cells had been worse, filled with the sounds of other slaves breathing, coughing, weeping in their sleep. Even the descent had been noisy, her own footsteps and John's echoing off the stone, the occasional scuttle of creatures in the darkness, the crack of the weapon and the wet thud of dying monsters.

Now there was nothing. Just her, and the cavern, and the slow pulse of the magicules pressing against her skin like a heartbeat that was not her own.

She finished the table and moved to the floor. Broom—a bundle of stiff reeds tied to a wooden handle, worn smooth from use—leaned against the wall near the entrance. She swept the stone in long, even strokes, gathering the dust and the small fragments of crystal and the dried clumps of growth medium that had fallen from the basin. The debris she collected in a leather pouch that hung near the door, to be emptied in the tunnels beyond the workshop where it would not attract pests.

The work took time. She did not rush. Rushing would mean mistakes, and mistakes would mean John would have to correct her, and she had learned that correction from owners was never pleasant even when it was not painful. She swept every corner, wiped every surface, checked the torches and trimmed their wicks and added fat from the clay pots to keep them burning. The moss on the northern wall she left alone—John had said it needed no tending, and she believed him.

The perimeter traps were next.

She walked to the eastern entrance first, the tunnel where the Chitin Crawler had triggered the alarm. The string was still intact, the bell still in place, the chitin clusters still hanging from their monofilament lines. She checked each component, her fingers moving across the rough stone where the string was anchored, the smooth surface of the bell, the hollow rattle of the chitin. Everything was as John had shown her. She moved on.

The southern route was undisturbed, the stones still balanced on their narrow ledges, the bells still silent. The western fissure—the narrow opening that led into the maze of smaller tunnels—showed signs of recent movement. Small scratches in the dust, a few grains of stone displaced, nothing large enough to trigger the alarms but enough to make her pause. She checked the trap John had set at the fissure's mouth: a cluster of chitin shells on a tripwire, positioned so that anything larger than a rat would brush against it. The wire was intact. The shells were in place. She left them alone.

The northern pinch point was where she found the damage.

The trap was simple—a string stretched across the narrowest part of the tunnel, connected to a bell and a cascade of small stones that would tumble down when triggered. The string was intact, but the bell was missing, knocked from its perch and lying on the tunnel floor a few meters away. The stones had not fallen—the trigger had not been pulled hard enough—but something had passed through here, something that had brushed against the string just enough to dislodge the bell without snapping the line.

She crouched, picked up the bell, and examined it. The metal was still warm, the clapper still attached, the mounting loop slightly bent where it had been pulled from its hook. She reset it carefully, her fingers working the soft iron back into shape, then reattached it to the string at the same height John had shown her. She tested it with a gentle touch, and the bell rang out, sharp and clear, echoing down the tunnel before fading into the silence.

She reset the other traps that needed attention, her hands remembering the motions from John's demonstration. The work was not difficult—string, bells, chitin, stones—but it required care, attention to detail, the same precision she had brought to cleaning the workshop. A trap that was set wrong was worse than no trap at all, because it created a false sense of security. John had not said this, but she understood it. She had learned to understand such things in the merchant's house, where a mis-set lock or a poorly barred door could mean the difference between safety and disaster.

When she returned to the workshop, she practiced the purge weave again.

She sat on her bed, cross-legged, her hands resting on her knees, and reached inward. The Net came more easily now, the grid of interlocking lines forming in her channels without the same struggle as before. The Wave followed, slower than she wanted but steadier, its rhythm finding the pulse of the dungeon's breathing rather than fighting against it. The residue shifted, released, flowed out through her pores and her breath and the subtle channels that connected her body to the ambient magicules. She held the weave for as long as she could, until her channels ached and her concentration frayed, and then she let it go and sat in the silence, breathing in the clean air that was never quite clean.

Again. She cast the weave again, and again, until the ache in her channels became a burn and the burn became a numbness and she could feel the residue building slower than she could clear it. Not gone—never gone, not at this depth—but managed. Controlled. The fog in her thoughts was thinner now, the weight in her limbs lighter, and she understood that this was the difference between surviving in the Deep and dying in it. The purge weave was not a luxury. It was not a tool for recovery after a delve. It was a constant, ongoing process, as necessary as breathing, and she was learning to make it part of herself.

The notes were next.

She pulled the stack of papers toward her and spread them out on the bed, the diagrams catching the torchlight in ways that made the lines seem to move. The first page showed the Line—straight, simple, almost insulting in its lack of complexity. She traced it with her finger, felt the magicules in her channels respond, and moved to the next page.

Spiral. A continuous curve winding around a center point, tighter at the middle, looser at the edges. The notes in the margin said something about heat and rotation, the words too dense for her to fully parse, but the diagram told her what she needed to know. Magicules flowing through a Spiral would turn, would spin, would generate heat as they coiled inward. She traced the curve, felt the magicules in her fingers stir, and wondered what it would feel like to cast a Spiral at full strength—to generate heat without flame, to create warmth in the cold darkness of the Deep.

Net. Grid of interlocking lines, closed cells, structure and binding. She had been casting this pattern for hours, had felt it catch the residue in her channels and hold it steady while the Wave did its work. The diagram showed the grid in perfect detail, each line intersecting with its neighbors, each cell a small, closed space. She traced the intersections, counted the cells, tried to memorize the geometry.

Wave. Sinuous, repeating, a pulse that moved through the grid like water through a sieve. She understood this pattern now, understood how its rhythm could loosen bonds and set particles in motion, understood why John had told her to slow down, to let the Wave find its own pace. A Wave that was too fast was a hammer, breaking what it should have loosened. A Wave that was too slow was a whisper, ignored by the residue it was meant to move. The right rhythm was somewhere in between, and she was learning to find it.

She read through the pages slowly, tracing each diagram, trying to commit the shapes to memory. Some patterns were simple—Line, Arc, Chevron—their geometries straightforward, their effects easy to guess. Others were complex—Möbius, Braid, Lock—their lines twisting and interweaving in ways that made her head hurt. She did not try to understand all of them. She focused on the ones John had mentioned: Net, Wave, Line, Spiral, Fork, Anchor. The primitives that would matter for the purge weave, for the crystal growth, for the work she had been bought to do.

The paper was valuable. John had said as much, had made a point of showing her the waterproofed folder where he kept his notebooks, the careful way he handled each page. She did not want to waste paper practicing patterns she might draw wrong, so she searched the workshop until she found what she was looking for—a small pot of ink, its contents thick and dark, and a flat stone that had been chipped from the wall and smoothed on one side.

She sat on the floor near her bed, the stone in front of her, the ink pot beside it. She dipped her finger into the ink—there was no brush, and she did not want to waste the stylus on practice—and began to draw.

The first Line was crooked, thicker at one end than the other, the edges blurred where her finger had pressed too hard. She wiped the stone clean with the hem of her coat, dipped her finger again, and tried again. Straighter this time, more even, the line running from one edge of the stone to the other without wavering. She looked at it, then at the diagram on the paper, then back at the stone. Not perfect. But closer.

She drew a Spiral. The curve wobbled, the spaces between loops uneven, the center off to one side instead of in the middle. She wiped the stone and tried again. Better. Not good, but better.

Net was harder. The grid required straight lines and right angles, intersections that met cleanly, cells that were roughly the same size. Her first attempt was a mess—lines crossing at odd angles, cells that were triangles instead of squares, the whole thing lopsided and ugly. She wiped the stone and tried again, more slowly this time, drawing each line one at a time, checking the intersections before she moved on. The result was still imperfect, but it was recognizable as a Net. A crude Net, a Net that would make John frown if he saw it, but a Net nonetheless.

She practiced for an hour, maybe longer. The ink dried on her fingers, staining them dark, and the flat stone grew crowded with patterns—Lines and Spirals and Nets and Waves, overlapping and intersecting, some drawn carefully and others scratched out and drawn again. She tried a Fork, the single line splitting into three branches, and watched the ink flow where her finger pressed. The branches were uneven, two of them thick and one thin, but they were there. She tried a Chevron, the V-shape with its sharp vertex, and found it easier than she expected—two lines meeting at an angle, nothing more. She tried an Anchor, the T-shape with its serrated base, and struggled to make the serrations even, the crossbar straight.

Her finger ached. The ink was running low. She sat back, looked at the stone covered in her attempts, and felt something that might have been satisfaction. Not pride—pride was dangerous, pride invited correction—but something close to it. She had drawn patterns. She had made her hand trace shapes that she had never seen before this morning, and some of them had come out right. Not perfect, but right enough.

She wiped the stone clean one last time, capped the ink pot, and returned both to the shelf where she had found them. Her hands were stained, the ink crusted under her nails, and she washed them in the spring's basin, watching the dark liquid swirl and dissipate in the clear water.

As she dried her hands on a rag, she looked around the workshop. The tables were clean, the tools organized, the torches burning steadily. The perimeter traps had been checked and reset. She had practiced the purge weave until her channels ached, had studied the pattern notes until her eyes blurred, had drawn diagrams on a flat stone until her finger cramped.

And still John had not returned.

The silence pressed against her again, heavier now than it had been in the morning. She had been too busy to notice it, too focused on the work and the patterns and the endless list of tasks he had left her. But now the work was done, and the silence remained, and she had nothing to fill it except her own thoughts.

She sat on her bed, her back against the wall, and looked at the empty workshop. The two tables dominated the center of the cavern, their surfaces bare, their edges scarred. The bed of fur and leather stood against the far wall, unmade, the blankets tangled where John had slept. The chest of food stores sat beside it, closed and silent. The crater in the far wall was a darker shadow against the darker stone, a wound that had not healed and would not heal, a reminder of the weapon that had killed monsters and carved holes in solid rock.

She thought about John, alone in this cavern for three years. Three years of waking to the same dim light, the same silence, the same endless cycle of work and sleep and work again. No one to talk to. No one to share the silence. Just the dungeon, breathing around him, indifferent and eternal.

She wondered if he had ever felt lonely.

The merchant's house had been crowded, but she had been lonely there. The holding cells had been filled with other slaves, but she had been lonely there too. Loneliness was not the absence of people. Loneliness was the absence of connection, of understanding, of someone who saw you as something more than a tool or a piece of property. She had been surrounded by people for most of her life, and she had been lonely for most of it.

John was different. She did not know him well—had known him for less than a day, had spoken to him for perhaps an hour in total—but she could see it in the way he moved, the way he worked, the way his eyes looked at the world as if it were a problem to be solved rather than a place to be lived in. He did not need people. He did not want them. The silence was not oppressive to him; it was comfortable. The loneliness was not a hunger; it was a choice.

She decided that he had probably never felt lonely. Not because he was strong or stoic or any of the things that surface-dwellers admired in their heroes, but because he was not like other people. He was something else, something that did not require the same connections, the same warmth, the same messy, complicated web of relationships that made life bearable for everyone else.

She did not know if that made him lucky or broken. She suspected he would not care about the distinction.

The hide curtain rustled.

Lilith looked up, her heart jumping in her chest before she could stop it. John stepped through the gap, his pack still on his back, the rounded weapon tucked into his coat. Across his shoulders, he carried the carcass of a Rockback—a squat, heavy creature with thick, overlapping plates of bone and a snout full of flat, grinding teeth. The carcass was still bleeding, dark blood dripping onto the stone floor in a trail that marked his path from the entrance to the center of the cavern.

He stopped, looked around the workshop. His eyes moved across the clean tables, the organized tools, the burning torches. They paused on the stone where she had drawn her patterns—now wiped clean, but still damp from the washing—and then moved to her, sitting on her bed with her hands folded in her lap.

"Good," he said.

He shrugged the carcass off his shoulders and let it fall onto the stone slab near the spring—a flat, slanted surface that she had not noticed before, its surface stained dark with old blood. The Rockback landed with a heavy thud, its legs splayed, its mouth open, its tongue lolling out between its teeth.

John pulled his knife from his belt—the same knife he had used to cut the Chitterer's gland, the same knife he had used to clean the Deep Lurker's leg—and set it on the slab beside the carcass. He turned to her, his face still flat, still unreadable, but something in his posture had shifted. Not softer, exactly. Less rigid. As if the hunt had drained something from him, some tension that she had not noticed until it was gone.

"Butcher this," he said, gesturing to the Rockback. "Keep the meat, the bones, the hide. Discard the rest."

He walked to his table, pulled out his chair, and sat down heavily. For a moment, just a moment, he looked tired—the same exhaustion she had seen after the crystal growth, the same hollow emptiness behind his eyes. Then he reached for his notebook, picked up his stylus, and began to write, his hand moving across the page in the same precise, cramped strokes she had seen before.

Lilith looked at the carcass. The Rockback's blood was already beginning to congeal, dark and thick, pooling on the stone slab and dripping onto the floor. She had butchered animals before—not Rockbacks, never anything from the Deep, but chickens and rabbits and the occasional pig that the merchant had bought for special occasions. The work was messy, bloody, and she had never enjoyed it.

But it was work. And work was what she had been bought to do.

She stood, walked to the slab, and picked up the knife.
