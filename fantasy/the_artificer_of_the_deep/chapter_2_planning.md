
# The Artificer of the Deep.

## Chapter 2 Outline: The Price of Silence

### 1. The Toll of Silence

**Purpose:** Establish that John's current situation is unsustainable. Show the accumulated cost of doing everything alone, and plant the seed of his decision.

**Scene:** A cold morning in the workshop. John wakes from five hours of sleep, his body moving through routines that have become automatic. He checks the perimeter traps (three need resetting), tends the slow-growth crystal experiment (no progress overnight), inventories his food stores (running low), and begins the hunt that will consume the next four hours.

**Exposition woven in:**
- The crystal growth process requires three days of absolute focus. During those days, nothing else happens. No hunting, no cooking, no trap maintenance. The last time he attempted a major growth, he emerged to find mold spreading through his food stores and a Rockback had broken two of his perimeter alarms.
- He has been putting off the next growth attempt for three weeks. The crystal he needs for the next iteration of his gun—longer, more efficient, capable of higher muzzle velocity—requires the three-day weave. But every day he spends growing crystals is a day he doesn't hunt, doesn't maintain, doesn't survive.
- The math is simple and brutal: one person cannot sustain a workshop, a hunting territory, a living space, and a research program simultaneously. Something has to give.

**Character beat:** John sits at his table with a list of tasks that need doing. Two pages, front and back. He stares at it for a long time, then pulls out a fresh page and writes three options:

1. *Hire help from surface* — too many questions, no guarantee of discretion
2. *Train an assistant* — requires trust he does not have and time he cannot spare
3. *Acquire guaranteed loyalty* — slave, B-rank or higher for residue tolerance, discretion guaranteed by collar, expensive but viable

He circles the third option. It is not a moral choice. It is an engineering choice. He needs a solution that works, and this is the only one that satisfies all constraints.

**Closing image:** John pulls out his stores of preserved monster parts, assessing what he can sell. His fingers brush the Deep Viper scales—a full set, pristine, worth a small fortune. He has been saving them for something important. This qualifies.

### 2. A Walk in the Light

**Purpose:** Transition from the dungeon to the surface. Establish Greymouth as a grounded fantasy town—not overly fantastical, but a place where magic is used pragmatically, like technology in a medieval setting.

**Scene:** John emerges from the collapsed quarry tunnel into weak morning light. He blinks, his eyes—adapted to dungeon darkness—slowly adjusting. The sky is gray with clouds. It has been four months since he last saw it.

He changes in the shelter of the quarry: cleaner clothes, hair combed, the gun disassembled and hidden in compartments sewn into his coat. He looks like a young delver who has had some success and wants to sell his haul. Forgettable.

The walk to Greymouth takes twenty minutes along a cart track that becomes a road, that becomes a street lined with buildings. The town wakes around him.

**Exposition woven in:**
- Greymouth exists because of the dungeon. Three thousand people live here, and every one of them serves the industry that the Deep creates. Delvers come to seek fortunes; merchants come to sell them supplies; smiths, alchemists, healers, and fences have built permanent businesses around the transient population of fortune-seekers.
- Magic here is not wondrous—it is *infrastructure*. The streetlamps burn with Aether crystals, fueled by the guild. The tannery uses heating stones to cure leather in half the time. The baker's oven is lined with pattern crystals that maintain steady temperature through the night. John notices these things because he notices all things, but he does not stop to admire them. They are simply the technology of this place, as natural to its inhabitants as combustion engines were to his old world.
- The people move with purpose. Delvers in mismatched armor, their faces marked by the particular weariness of those who have spent too long underground. Merchants calling out prices. Children running errands with small crystal lamps clipped to their belts. A woman at a well uses a simple Levitation pattern to lift her water bucket—not a spell, just a practiced weave that she probably learned from her mother.

**Character beat:** John moves through the streets with practiced ease. He knows which alleys to take, which vendors to avoid, which guards can be bribed with a small crystal and which cannot. He has been doing this for three years. The town does not know him, but he knows the town. He feels no nostalgia, no longing. The surface is where other people live. He is passing through.

**Closing image:** John enters the Merchant's Quarter, the Deep Viper scales wrapped in oiled cloth in his pack, and heads for the one fence he trusts to ask no questions.

### 3. The Merchant's Price

**Purpose:** Show the value of dungeon resources, establish John's wealth, and introduce the economic reality of slave pricing.

**Scene:** Marek's tannery occupies the ground floor of a narrow building on Tanner's Row. The smell is constant—curing agents, wet hide, the sharp tang of alchemical preservatives. Marek himself is a fat man with quick eyes and slower hands, and he has been buying from John for two years without ever learning his name.

John lays out the Deep Viper scales on Marek's counting table. The fence's eyes widen—he knows quality when he sees it.

**Exposition woven in:**
- Deep Vipers are B-rank monsters that live in the hot tunnels near the lava lakes. A full set of scales in good condition can outfit a suit of armor that resists both physical damage and magical degradation. Most delvers who try to hunt them don't come back.
- Marek examines each scale with professional appreciation. He names a price: eighty gold. Enough to keep a family in Greymouth for a year. John counters at one hundred twenty. They settle at ninety-five.
- As Marek counts out the coins, John asks, casually: "What's the slave market like these days?"
- Marek gives him a look—not judgment, just curiosity. He answers: Velsa & Sons runs the main market in the Plaza of Chains. A B-rank dungeon-adapted slave runs fifty to a hundred gold, depending on age, health, and skills. Collared slaves with guaranteed obedience are more expensive than untrained ones. "Looking for a porter?"
- John does not answer directly. He takes the gold, thanks Marek, and leaves.

**Character beat:** Marek watches John go with narrowed eyes. He has always wondered about this one—too young, too calm, too good at staying alive. But he is a fence, not a fool, and asking questions would only cost him a reliable supplier. He returns to his tannery and does not mention the visit to anyone.

**Closing image:** John walks through the crowded streets toward the Plaza of Chains, his purse heavy, his face neutral, his mind already calculating.

### 4. The Plaza of Chains

**Purpose:** Introduce the slave market and the system of binding collars. Show the grounded, pragmatic nature of this fantasy institution—magic used as technology for social control, brutal but functional.

**Scene:** The Plaza of Chains is a wide square dominated by the Velsa & Sons auction house. The building is stone, three stories, with iron bars on the ground-floor windows and a raised platform at the center of the square where slaves are displayed on market days. Today is not a market day—John has come for a private viewing.

Inside, the air is cool and smells of incense. The clerk who greets him is a middle-aged woman with grey-streaked hair and the flat, professional courtesy of someone who has seen everything and stopped being surprised by any of it.

John states his requirements clearly: B-rank or higher resistance to magicule accumulation. Physically capable of heavy labor. Trained in domestic skills. Discretion essential.

The clerk nods. She leads him to a viewing room and brings out the files.

**Exposition woven in:**
- The collars: enchanted iron bands inscribed with Anchor, Cage, and Lock patterns. Once keyed to a master's magical signature, they create an absolute binding. The slave cannot harm the master, cannot disobey direct orders, cannot reveal the master's secrets. Any attempt triggers a cascading failure in the binding patterns that kills the wearer before the information can be spoken or the action completed. The collars are permanent; removal kills the slave.
- Slave classifications use the same G-through-SSS system as delver ranks. A B-rank slave has the physical and magical tolerance to survive deep dungeon conditions—this is measured through controlled exposure tests, not combat ability. Most B-rank slaves are former delvers who fell into debt or were captured in border raids.
- Domestic skills are considered nearly worthless in the delver market. Buyers want fighters or porters. A slave who can cook and clean but cannot carry a load is a luxury item, and there are few luxuries in the Deep.

**Character beat:** John reads the files with the same detachment he applies to his experiment notes. Each slave is reduced to a set of attributes: rank, skills, health history, price. He does not enjoy this. He does not moralize. The system exists; he is using it; sentiment would be inefficient.

**Closing image:** The clerk returns from the back rooms, leading two slaves for John to inspect. John looks up from the files, his face giving nothing away.

### 5. The House of Velsa

**Purpose:** Introduce the two candidates through John's analytical eye. Establish Lilith's character—her stillness, her observation, the tension beneath her compliance. Show John's decision-making process.

**Scene:** The viewing room is small, furnished with chairs and a table. The slaves stand in the center, waiting.

**The first** is a human man, perhaps twenty-two, broad-shouldered, scarred. His file says his name is Kael, formerly a C-rank delver, sold to Velsa after a gambling debt default. B-rank resistance confirmed. Combat training. Experience as a porter in deep delves. He stands with the particular stillness of a man who has learned that movement draws attention. His eyes are flat. His price is sixty-five gold.

**The second** is a young woman. Demon heritage—light blue skin, thin horns curving back from her temples, a long tail that hangs motionless behind her. Her name is Lilith, according to her file. She is twenty. B-rank resistance confirmed. Domestic skills: cooking, cleaning, basic alchemy, food preservation. No combat experience. She has been on the market for eight months—bought by a merchant who wanted a housekeeper, resold after his death, deemed unsuitable for the delver market. Her price is fifteen gold.

John asks to speak with each alone.

Kael answers questions in monosyllables. Yes, he has been in the Deep. Yes, he can carry a load. No, he does not cook. He looks at John with something that might be hope or might be calculation—hard to tell. When John asks why he should choose Kael over other options, the man's eyes flicker. "I won't cause trouble," he says. "I'll do what I'm told."

Lilith meets John's eyes when he speaks to her. Her voice is low, careful. She can cook—she lists dishes, ingredients, preservation methods. She can clean—she knows how to treat leather, how to keep tools from rusting, how to organize a workspace. She knows basic alchemy: wound salves, preservation agents, the identification of common dungeon herbs. When John asks about her lack of combat training, she pauses.

"I was purchased for a surface home," she says. "My master did not believe in arming his property."

The words are careful, rehearsed. But John notices her hands: clean, well-kept, but there is a tension in them. The tension of someone who has learned to hold still even when every instinct says to move. Her tail, too—curled close to her legs, out of the way, a habit born of crowded spaces where a tail could be stepped on or grabbed.

He watches her for a long moment. She does not look away.

**Character beat:** John's decision is pragmatic. Kael is stronger, better suited for heavy labor. But Lilith has skills that directly address his biggest time sinks: cooking, cleaning, organization. Her lack of combat experience means she is less likely to do something stupid with the knowledge she will inevitably gain. And she is cheaper—fifteen gold leaves him eighty to spend on supplies.

There is another factor, though he does not name it even to himself. Kael's flat eyes speak of a man who has been broken. Lilith's stillness speaks of someone who has learned to survive by being invisible. Both are useful traits in a slave. But one of them, he suspects, has not stopped watching for opportunities.

"I'll take the demon," he tells the clerk.

**Closing image:** John pays, watches as Lilith's collar is keyed to his magical signature. The iron band glows faintly as the Anchor patterns settle, binding her to him. He accepts the master key—a small iron ring that can command absolute obedience—and puts it in his pocket. He does not intend to use it.

### 6. The Weight of Gold

**Purpose:** Show the practical shopping trip. Establish Greymouth's character through the lens of its markets. Give John and Lilith their first extended interaction outside the auction house.

**Scene:** They leave Velsa & Sons. John turns not toward the residential district, where most buyers would take a new slave, but toward the market streets. Lilith follows without comment, her footsteps light on the cobblestones. Her tail curls close to her legs—the same habit he noticed earlier.

John leads her through the morning market, buying what he needs. A bed frame and mattress from a carpenter who deals in delver furniture—sturdy, collapsible for transport. Two sets of work clothes for Lilith, sized by eye. Towels, blankets, a washbasin. Lead ingots from a metalsmith—ten pounds, for reasons he does not explain.

**Exposition woven in:**
- The market is a place where magic and craft blend seamlessly. The carpenter uses a precision-cutting pattern to shape joints faster than any saw. The clothier's measuring tape has a simple detection pattern that records dimensions without repeated measurements. The metalsmith's forge is lined with heating crystals that maintain consistent temperature, and his apprentice uses a modified Levitation weave to move ingots from stack to scale.
- This is not a world of high fantasy wonder. It is a world where people have found practical uses for the tools available to them, just as people on Earth used fire and wheel and lever. Magic is work. Magic is trade. Magic is the thing that lets a tanner cure leather in three days instead of three weeks, and that is why everyone uses it.
- John moves through this environment with the ease of someone who understands systems. He knows which vendors are honest, which will try to overcharge a young-looking buyer, which will accept a small overpayment in exchange for loading the goods onto a cart and delivering them to the quarry gate without asking questions.

**Character beat:** Lilith watches him. She has been sold twice before—once to the merchant who died, once to Velsa after his estate was settled. She knows how buyers behave. They ignore slaves, or they watch them too closely, or they make small comments to assert ownership. John does none of these things. He treats her as he might treat a piece of equipment he has just purchased: he does not talk to it, but he does not insult it either. He simply... uses it. She is not sure if this is better or worse than what she expected.

At the clothier's, he tells her to pick clothes that fit. She does. He pays. He does not comment on her choices.

When they leave the market, she speaks for the first time since the auction house. "Where are we going?"

John glances at her. "My home."

She waits for more. None comes. She follows.

**Closing image:** They reach the quarry gate, where a cart has delivered the purchased goods. John pays the driver, dismisses him, and begins carrying the load toward the collapsed tunnel entrance. Lilith stands at the quarry's edge, looking at the dark opening, at the faint haze of magicules that rises from it like heat from summer pavement.

"You live in the dungeon," she says. It is not a question.

John looks back at her. "Yes."

He does not ask if she has a problem with this. Her collar ensures that she does not.

### 2. The Deep Howler

**Appearance:** A bipedal reptile standing nearly three meters tall when fully upright, though it hunts in a crouch that brings its head level with a tall human's chest. Its skin is the color of old blood, slick with a viscous secretion that smells of copper and ozone, and its head is dominated by a crescent-shaped crest of bone that flares outward from its skull like a hammerhead shark's. Its mouth contains no teeth—instead, its jaw is lined with rows of serrated cartilage that can grind through bone and metal alike. Its arms are disproportionately long, ending in three-fingered hands with opposable thumbs, and its feet are broad and flat, distributing its weight so effectively that it can walk across loose scree without making a sound.

**Behavior & Abilities:** The Deep Howler is a pursuit predator, capable of maintaining a running speed of forty kilometers per hour for up to an hour without tiring. It hunts by scent and vibration, tracking prey through stone walls by the thumping of their hearts, and it uses its spell-like ability *Sonic Lament* to disable prey before closing. The Lament is a focused cone of infrasound that bypasses physical armor entirely, causing violent nausea, disorientation, and—in victims with low magicule tolerance—immediate cardiac arrest. The Howler can maintain the Lament while moving, and it often uses the ability to flush prey from hiding spots, driving them into open tunnels where it can run them down.

**Why it threatens Lilith:** Her B-rank resistance offers no protection against infrasound. The Sonic Lament would incapacitate her within seconds, leaving her vomiting and helpless on the ground while the Howler approaches at its leisurely, terrifying pace. Even if she tried to run, it would catch her in under a minute.

**Why John treats it as an inconvenience:** He has learned to recognize the subtle pressure change in the air that precedes the Sonic Lament, and his unconscious Wave—constantly venting magicule residue—disrupts the infrasound's coherence before it can affect him. To the Howler, John might as well be invisible; its primary weapon simply fails against his unique physiology. When one appears, he waits for it to open its mouth to begin the Lament, then fires a single bullet through the soft palate into its brain. He has collected four sets of cranial crests, which he uses as paperweights.

### 3. The Null Mantis

**Appearance:** A towering insectoid creature that stands on four segmented legs, its body a column of chitinous armor that shifts from deep purple to black as it moves. Two massive scythe-like forelimbs fold against its chest when not in use, each one edged with a serrated blade that can extend up to a meter from the joint. Its head is a triangular wedge, featureless except for a cluster of three crimson eyes arranged in a vertical line, and its mouthparts are hidden beneath a hinged plate that opens only when it feeds. A pair of translucent wings, veined like a dragonfly's but tattered and useless for flight, lie folded along its back—vestigial remnants of an earlier evolutionary stage. When it moves, it does so in short, jerky bursts, freezing between each motion in a way that makes it difficult to track with the human eye.

**Behavior & Abilities:** The Null Mantis is a territorial predator that claims a section of tunnel—usually a kilometer or more in length—and kills anything that enters it. Its spell-like ability is what gives it its name: *Null Field*. The mantis can project a sphere of disrupted magic up to thirty meters in diameter, within which all active magical effects cease to function. Enchantments fail, healing potions become inert, and—most critically—the collar that binds Lilith to John would become nothing more than a cold iron band. The mantis uses this field to neutralize its prey's advantages before moving in to kill with its scythes.

**Why it threatens Lilith:** In the Null Field, she is not just defenseless—she is *free*. The collar's binding patterns would go dormant, and with them, the guarantee of her obedience. But freedom in the presence of a Null Mantis is a death sentence; she has no weapon, no training, and no way to escape a predator that can move faster than she can react. Worse, the mantis instinctively targets the most magically active creature in its field first—which would be John, leaving Lilith to watch as the scythes carve through him before turning to her.

**Why John treats it as an inconvenience:** He discovered during his second encounter with a Null Mantis that his crystals—the ones he grows, with their hollow-carved channels—are not affected by the Null Field. The field disrupts *active* magic, but the hollows are physical structures, not ongoing spells. His gun fires just fine. He has learned to shoot the mantis in the joint between its second and third legs, which collapses its stance and brings its head down to a height where he can put a bullet through the central eye. He considers the mantis's chitin useful for knife handles and its eyes interesting to study under magnification.
