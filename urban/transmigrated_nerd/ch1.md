
# The Transmigrated Husband's Quiet Counterattack
## Prologue: The Imprint

The first thing I felt was the weight of paper in my hands.
Thick, expensive paper. The kind that practically screams money. My fingers were resting on a paragraph I hadn't read, halfway down a page filled with dense legal text and looping signatures I didn't recognize.
I blinked. The room was too big. High ceilings, cream-colored walls, furniture that probably cost more than my entire apartment in my previous life. Chandeliers. Actual chandeliers. And people — maybe fifteen of them — standing around in a loose semicircle, watching me.
No. Not watching me. Waiting.
A man I didn't know stood near the door in a suit that fit him like a second skin. A woman beside him clutched a handkerchief, her expression caught between expectation and irritation. Servants lined the walls, eyes politely averted but ears very much present.
And facing me, not three feet away, sat the most beautiful woman I had ever seen.
She was all sharp lines and cold grace. High cheekbones. Dark hair pulled back with mathematical precision. Eyes the color of winter skies, watching me with the flat patience of someone who had long ago mastered the art of waiting through unpleasantness. Her posture was perfect. Her hands rested motionless on her lap. She looked like a marble statue someone had dressed in couture.
Then it hit me. Not recognition — *knowledge*. A flood of it, crashing into my skull like someone had hooked a firehose directly to my brain.
*John Carter. Supporting male lead. Forced marriage. Lilith Smith. Ice queen. Tragedy. Disinheritance. Divorce. Regret.*
Names. Dates. Scenes. Entire chapters of a novel I'd never read but now remembered with perfect, terrifying clarity. The original John storming out of this very meeting. The years of humiliation. The clubs, the models, the security teams dragging him back. His eventual obsession with the female lead. The younger brother who would replace him the moment he turned eighteen. The final, pathetic image of a man who had lost everything, staring at a life he'd thrown away.
And now I was him.
"John."
The voice cut through the static in my head. The woman with the handkerchief — my mother, apparently, in this reality — was staring at me with barely concealed exasperation.
"Why won't you answer? Lady Lilith asked you a direct question."
*The novel's content finished imprinting. The original scene was clear: stand up, scream, throw the contract, storm out, and set in motion years of self-destruction.*
*No thanks.*
I took a breath and set the document down on the coffee table between us. The movement was slow. Deliberate. Nothing like the explosive reaction everyone was clearly bracing for.
"I need to talk to Lilith alone," I said. My voice came out low. Flat. Final. "Everyone leave."
The silence that followed was almost funny. Someone in the back let out a sharp breath. My new father's face went through three different shades of disapproval in under a second.
"John, you will compose yourself—"
"How dare you speak to us that way, after everything—"
"You ungrateful little—"
The voices layered over each other, a chorus of manufactured outrage. I ignored all of them. My eyes stayed locked on Lilith. She hadn't moved an inch. Her expression hadn't changed. But something flickered behind those winter-grey eyes. Interest, maybe. Or calculation.
I let the corner of my mouth twitch upward. Just slightly. Just for her.
She saw it.
And then, moving with the unhurried confidence of someone who was used to commanding rooms twice this size, she raised one elegant hand.
The voices stopped.
"This is a marriage between myself and John," she said. Her voice was quieter than I expected. Measured. "If he wishes to speak privately, I see no reason to refuse."
My family exchanged looks. The patriarch — my new father — opened his mouth, thought better of it, and snapped it shut. His jaw worked silently for a moment.
"Security will remain stationed outside," he finally said, directing the words at Lilith rather than me. "If he becomes... agitated..."
He trailed off. The implication hung in the air: *if he attacks you, we're ready.*
I almost laughed. They thought I was going to beat her. The original John must have been a real piece of work.
The family filed out with theatrical reluctance, the servants trailing behind them like a flock of well-dressed geese. The heavy double doors closed with a resonant thud that echoed through the cavernous room.
And then there were two.

The silence stretched. Lilith sat perfectly still, her hands folded, her face a mask of porcelain composure. But I noticed her throat move — a small, tight swallow. She was bracing herself.
For curses, probably. Insults. The tantrum everyone had been expecting since I picked up that contract.
I didn't give her any of that.
"Allow me to summarize the situation as I understand it," I said, settling back into my chair. "You need to marry a son of the Carter family because of your grandfather's dying wish. I am currently the only eligible option — my brother is twelve, so that'll be a while. You have no romantic or physical interest in me, which is fine. And to maintain public appearances, both families will ensure I can't... wander. Correct?"
Lilith's composure cracked. Just a hair. Her lips parted slightly, and I watched her mentally recalculate everything she thought she knew about this conversation.
"That is... accurate," she said.
"I thought so." I nodded. "Then I won't sign this agreement."
Her fist clenched against her skirt. So small I almost missed it. Here it comes, she was thinking. The calm before the hurricane.
I lifted my hand and extended one finger.
"First. Three percent of the monthly profit from *your* companies — not the family holdings, but the ones you build and control yourself — deposited directly into my private account."
I extended a second finger.
"Second. I will establish my own company, with exclusive ownership. You will provide the initial capital. Seven hundred thousand dollars."
A third finger.
"Third. My movements are not monitored or controlled by your security personnel, so long as I maintain public discretion. I am your husband, not your inmate."
A fourth.
"Fourth. I will hold a nominal Vice Chairman position at one of your subsidiaries. No operational duties, no interference. Purely for appearances and public standing."
I paused before extending my thumb. Let the silence hang for just a beat longer.
"Fifth. Since you have no interest in intimacy and I have no desire to create public scandal, I will agree in writing to remain faithful — but only if you, Lilith, agree to the same. Neither of us will embarrass the other. Parity, not leash."
The words landed like stones in still water.
Lilith's face had gone pale — not the pale of fear, but of genuine, staggering shock. Her composure was still there, technically, but it was the composure of someone who had just watched the sun rise in the west and was trying very hard to process it. The fifth point in particular had done something to her. Her eyes kept flicking to my face, searching, as if trying to find the trick.
"One more thing," I added. "I remain the legally recognized spouse and sole inheritor of the marital alliance's benefits until a mutual dissolution is agreed upon — regardless of whether my brother reaches adulthood. Neither family may unilaterally substitute me for him."
I lowered my hand.
"If either of us violates this agreement, the other receives ninety percent of the offender's personal assets and a public statement crafted to present the wronged party in the best possible light."
Silence.
It stretched for ten seconds. Twenty.
Lilith was staring at me now without any pretense of detachment. Her mask had slipped entirely. Underneath it was something rawer — confusion, suspicion, and a third thing I couldn't quite name.
"You've changed so much," she murmured, barely audible.
I knew what she meant. This wasn't just unexpected behavior. This was *impossible* behavior. She had known John Carter since childhood. Year after year, event after event, she'd watched him grow into exactly the kind of man who would have been screaming obscenities at her by now. The playboy. The disaster.
And now here I sat, negotiating like a corporate lawyer and demanding a monogamy clause.
*She doesn't know the half of it.*
"I will agree," she said, her voice steadying. "But the breach penalties will be revised by my legal team. I want them airtight."
Right. The fidelity clause. She still didn't trust it — still saw the playboy, still expected the mask to slip the moment some actress batted her eyes at me. Fair enough. I could use that.
"You don't believe I'll hold up my end," I said. "Fine. Let's add a sixth point."
Her eyes narrowed.
"I will submit to a private quarterly audit — conducted by a mutually approved third party — to verify I haven't had any... extracurricular entanglements. If I fail, the penalty triggers. But the same standard applies to you, Lilith. If you breach, you pay. I'm not asking for a leash. I'm offering parity."
The color had not returned to her face. If anything, she looked a shade paler. But she nodded. Slowly. Deliberately.
"Who are you?"
The question came out steady, but there was something fragile underneath it. She wasn't just asking for my name.
I let out a short laugh. "Let's call a lawyer and draft the contract."
I stood up.
Lilith followed the motion, still visibly reeling, still trying to solve a puzzle with pieces that didn't fit. I met her eyes and let my expression soften — just slightly.
"Don't be shocked," I said. "Just play along."
Then I turned, picked up a heavy porcelain vase from the pedestal behind my chair, and hurled it to the floor.
The crash was magnificent. Shards skittered across the marble like startled insects. From the corridor beyond the double doors, I heard muffled exclamations — the Carter family, already on edge, now thoroughly alarmed. Someone rattled the door handle but didn't open it. Not yet.
I turned back to Lilith, completely calm. "Lift your hand and stop them when they come in. Not before."
She stared at me, her composure visibly fraying around the edges. But she gave a single nod and slowly raised her hand, palm outward, as if readying to halt an oncoming wave.
I walked to the doors, and by the third step I had become someone else. Shoulders hunched. Jaw corded. Breath coming in short, furious bursts. I wrenched the doors open and exploded into the hallway just as the patriarch reached for the handle.
"GET BACK!" I roared. "LAWYER! GET LILITH'S LAWYER! NOW!"
The Carters stumbled backward as one. Behind me, Lilith's raised hand was perfectly visible through the doorway — a silent, imperious command: *stay out, I have this.* The family froze, eyes darting between my fake rage and her real authority.
"The Smith legal team," Lilith added, her voice carrying over my shoulder with cool precision. "Summon my personal attorney. I believe he's waiting in the east wing."
The patriarch opened his mouth, then shut it. My new mother clutched her handkerchief as if it might shield her from the chaos. After a tense five seconds, someone was dispatched. I stood in the doorway, breathing hard, glaring at anyone who dared meet my eyes, until a tall man in a dark suit arrived — greying temples, sharp eyes, carrying a slim leather briefcase.
"INSIDE," I barked, stepping aside just enough to let him through.
I slammed the doors behind us.

The echo died.
I let my shoulders drop. My breathing evened out. I turned to the lawyer with a polite, almost apologetic nod.
"My apologies for the theatrics. The Carters are easier to manage when they think I'm unstable. I assume Ms. Smith has briefed you on the situation?"
The lawyer — Mr. Aldridge, according to the gold lettering on his briefcase — glanced at Lilith. She was lowering her hand, her expression equal parts wonder and wariness.
"He has not," Lilith said. "But you may speak freely. This man is not the John Carter I grew up with."
Aldridge's eyebrows rose a fraction of an inch, but he simply opened his briefcase and produced a tablet and stylus. "Then perhaps the two of you would care to enlighten me."
We did. Over the next hour, I laid out the six points, and Lilith refined them with the precision of a legal scalpel. Aldridge asked questions, suggested language, and occasionally gave me a look that suggested he was reconsidering everything he knew about human nature. When the final draft was ready, he sent it to a portable printer that materialized from a side compartment of his briefcase.
We read. We signed. I handed the pen to Lilith, and she added her signature beneath mine — crisp, elegant, unhurried.
Two copies. One for each of us.
Aldridge tucked his tablet away with the air of a man who had seen many strange things in his career and had long since stopped remarking on them aloud. "I'll file the necessary legal notices tomorrow morning. Is there anything else?"
"No," Lilith said. "Thank you, Aldridge. You may go."
He inclined his head and exited through a discreet side door I hadn't noticed before. Probably a servant's passage. Rich people.
The silence that followed was different from the earlier ones. Less tense. More expectant.
Lilith looked down at the signed contract in her hands, then back up at me.
"How much of your past was acting?" she asked. Her voice was quiet, but there was real weight behind it. "The parties. The scandals. The man my father was so reluctant to betroth me to. Was any of that real?"
I folded my copy of the contract and slid it into my jacket pocket.
"Enough," I said.
She waited for more. I didn't give it.
Instead I stood up, straightened my jacket, and asked, in the same calm tone I'd used for the negotiations: "Do you already have a marital home prepared? Address?"
Lilith blinked, caught off guard by the sudden shift to logistics. "Rosewood Estate. Number twelve, Crescent Lane. About thirty minutes from here."
"Good." I folded my contract and tucked it away. "I'll move in within a few hours. Tell your staff to expect me."
Her eyes narrowed slightly—trying to read me, trying to find the seam between the performance and the person. "You're leaving now?"
"The sooner I'm out of this house, the better." I glanced toward the double doors. "Speaking of which—I should probably give them something to remember."
I walked to the doors with measured steps, then paused with both hands on the handles. I looked back at her one last time. "You might want to step back. I'm about to break two more vases."
She didn't move. She just watched.
I wrenched the doors open with every ounce of force I could muster. They flew backward, slamming into the pedestals flanking the entrance. Two more porcelain vases—twins to the one I'd shattered earlier—exploded across the marble floor. The crash was deafening.
And then I was in the hallway, roaring.
"You sold out your son!" I bellowed, stabbing a finger toward the cluster of Carters. "Signed me over like cattle and called it a marriage! Don't think for a second I'll go down easy!"
My father's face purpled. My mother clutched her chest. The servants shrank against the walls.
"I hope the business deals were worth it!" I spat. "I hope they're worth your only son!"
That landed strangely. I saw confusion flicker across several faces—my brother existed, after all. They must have assumed I was disowning him in my rage, rejecting the very idea of siblings. I kept moving before anyone could question it.
I strode past them without slowing, shoes grinding on porcelain dust. No one tried to stop me. No one even spoke.
The grand foyer opened before me. I didn't look at the servants lined near the door. I didn't acknowledge Lilith's driver, who stood beside her vehicle wearing a carefully blank expression. I just cast one disinterested glance across the fleet of luxury cars parked in the curved driveway—then walked straight to the silver coupe I knew, from the novel's scattered details, belonged to the original John.
The engine purred to life when I pressed the ignition. Smooth. Expensive. Exactly what a playboy would drive.
I pulled away from the Carter mansion without looking back. In the rearview mirror, the grand columns and ivy-covered stone shrank into the distance, and the house that had been my prison for all of two hours faded behind a curtain of ancient oaks.
The gate opened automatically as I approached. I turned onto the public road, rolled the windows down, and let the cold evening air rush in.
For the first time since waking up in this novel, I was truly alone.
No family. No servants. No ice queen watching my every twitch with those calculating winter eyes.
I exhaled, long and slow, and checked the GPS. Rosewood Estate. Thirty minutes north. I'd be there before the streetlights came on.
Time to see where the rest of my life would begin.

Inside the mansion, the Carters stood in stunned silence among the porcelain wreckage. Lilith had not moved from her chair. The contract sat in her hands, ink still fresh.
"Is she alright?" someone whispered.
"I'm fine," Lilith said, not looking at them. Her eyes were fixed on the shattered vases, the splintered pedestals, the space where John Carter had just stood—a man who had negotiated like a corporate shark, lied like an actor, and screamed about being an only child despite very clearly having a brother.
None of it fit.
None of it.
She set the contract aside, folded her hands on her lap, and allowed herself the faintest ghost of a smile.
*Who are you, John Carter?*
She intended to find out.




















Rosewood Estate sat at the end of a winding private road, flanked by old oaks and a wrought-iron gate that opened automatically as my car approached. The villa itself was modest by the standards of people who thought "modest" meant anything under ten thousand square feet. Clean lines. Modern architecture. Floor-to-ceiling windows that would probably be hell to keep clean but looked impressive in the dying evening light.
A staff member met me at the door—middle-aged, efficient, utterly unflappable. She introduced herself as Mrs. Harlow, the house manager, and showed me to my quarters without a flicker of surprise at my arriving alone, unannounced, and still dusted with porcelain powder from the vases I'd shattered an hour ago.
"Miss Smith's quarters are in the east wing," she said, gesturing down a long, art-lined corridor. "Yours are in the west. The kitchen is staffed until ten. Is there anything else, Mr. Carter?"
"No. Thank you."
She departed with the quiet click of sensible shoes on hardwood. I stood alone in a bedroom roughly twice the size of my previous life's apartment, staring at a bed that could have slept four people comfortably.
The silence was immense. No family. No servants hovering. No security teams lurking in the corridors—at least not visibly.
*First night of the rest of my life*, I thought, and unpacked my single bag.

I woke at six the next morning and got to work.
The first order of business was finding office space. By noon, I'd signed a lease on the entire third floor of a nondescript commercial building about ten minutes from Rosewood. It was the kind of place that housed dental practices and mid-tier insurance firms—exactly the sort of location no one would ever associate with a Carter heir. Perfect.
The second order of business was furnishing it. I bought desks. Chairs. An unreasonable number of whiteboards. And workstations. Fourteen high-end machines with the kind of specifications that made my programmer soul sing: top-tier CPUs, professional-grade GPUs, enough RAM to hold entire worlds in memory. I might have lingered a little too long over the specs, but honestly, after the day I'd had, I deserved it.
By evening, the space was ready. Desks and chairs stood in neat rows, whiteboards waited for battle, and the small kitchenette—which had been nothing but a sink and an empty counter—now boasted a commercial-grade coffee machine that could produce anything from espresso to cold brew with the pull of a lever. One of the metal drawers had been stuffed to the brim with chocolate bars, sour gummies, and the kind of high-octane snacks that kept late-night debugging sessions alive. Along the entire far wall, I'd lined up ten different varieties of bottled beverages—sparkling water, energy drinks, imported sodas, cold-brew coffees—three cases deep, like a miniature beverage fortress.
It was excessive. It was exactly what a team of sleep-deprived engine programmers would need.
Now I just needed the programmers.

The interviews started the next day.
I'd posted listings on every platform that serious engine programmers might browse. The requirements were specific: strong C++ fundamentals, systems-level experience, and—most importantly—genuine enthusiasm for the hard, unglamorous work of building game engine architecture from scratch.
The first candidate showed up at nine sharp. He had an impressive resume from a major studio and spent the first five minutes telling me about his leadership experience and his vision for team culture. I thanked him for his time and showed him out.
The second candidate's resume listed seventeen programming languages. When I asked him to describe a time he'd debugged a memory alignment issue, he pivoted smoothly to talking about his passion for agile methodology. His interview lasted twelve minutes.
The third candidate walked in, sat down, and immediately asked if I'd considered using lock-free ring buffers for the core messaging system. We talked for two hours. I hired him on the spot.
By the end of the first week, I had six people. By the end of the second, ten. They came from different backgrounds—some from games, some from fintech, two from a defunct robotics startup that had given them an almost pathological attention to crash safety. What united them was simple: they cared. Genuinely, obsessively cared. About cache lines. About allocator design. About the precise overhead of virtual function dispatch on different compilers.
I didn't care if they had degrees. I didn't care if they had industry connections. I cared if their eyes lit up when I mentioned writing a custom profiler from scratch. If they did, they were in.

Lilith watched from a distance. She was careful about it—she always was—but I knew the reports were coming in. The security team she'd promised to withdraw was still technically in place during the transition period, and she was clearly reading every update.
What she read, I could only imagine:
*"Target rented office space. No exterior signage. No receptionist."*
*"Target conducted eighteen interviews this week. Six candidates were rejected within ten minutes. Two were reportedly 'visibly attractive women' who were not called back."*
*"Target's team currently consists of ten men and two women. No administrative staff. No marketing personnel. No sales department."*
I found out later that she'd been suspicious enough to intervene personally. Two of the female candidates who'd crossed my desk in those first two weeks hadn't applied through the listings at all—they'd been sent by Lilith, junior employees plucked from her own companies, briefed to test me.
The first one spent her ten-minute slot asking about "company culture" and whether I'd be available for "personal mentorship" outside office hours. I asked her to describe the difference between a mutex and a semaphore. She couldn't. I ended the interview.
The second one complimented my jacket, leaned forward during a question about thread synchronization, and suggested we continue the discussion over dinner. I told her the position had been filled and thanked her for her time.
Both reported back to Lilith within the hour. According to the security notes I would only see much later, the first one described me as "cold," and the second called me "completely impervious to signals." Lilith apparently read both reports twice.
And then, quietly, she redirected her security team to pull back to the villa perimeter only. The monitoring stopped. The tests stopped.
The suspicion, however, had transformed into something else entirely.

The office fell into a rhythm.
Twelve people—we'd made our final hire, a brilliant systems programmer named Dana who'd spent six years writing device drivers and could spot a race condition at twenty paces—arrived each morning around eight and often didn't leave until well past midnight. The whiteboards filled with diagrams: job system topologies, memory allocator designs, the endless debate over whether to roll our own windowing abstraction or integrate an existing library.
"So we fork GLFW and strip out everything we don't need," Chen argued one evening, marker in hand. "Why rebuild window handling from scratch when the hard work is already done?"
"Because then we own every line," Marcus countered. He was our lead architect, a man who had once rewritten an entire rendering backend over a month because he could. "We're building an engine. If we depend on third-party code at the foundation level, we're inheriting their bugs, their design decisions, their—"
"Their license compliance headaches," I finished. "Marcus is right. We write our own windowing layer. Minimal, portable, nothing we don't explicitly need."
Chen groaned but started sketching out API designs. The debate shifted to the logging system—lock-free SPSC queue per thread versus a simpler synchronized ring buffer—and by the time I looked up from the whiteboard, it was nearly eleven at night.
"Alright," I said, capping my marker. "Someone order food. We're going to be here a while."
No one objected. No one even looked up from their monitors. This was exactly where they wanted to be.
So was I.

The crash handler bug showed up around week three.
It was one of those maddening, subtle things—a stack frame misreport on Linux, only under specific optimization levels, only when certain combinations of template instantiation happened in a particular order. The kind of bug that made you question your career choices.
I was still at the office at two in the morning when I finally cracked it. A single misplaced pragma directive in the debug symbol generation pass. I fixed it, committed the change, and sat back in my chair, the glow of the monitor the only light in the dark office.
Outside, the city was asleep. Inside, the build server hummed quietly in the corner, compiling the latest changes. The CI pipeline—which we'd built ourselves, of course, because why use Jenkins when you can spend a week writing a custom build orchestrator—churned through unit tests.
I should have been exhausted. Instead, I felt something I hadn't felt in years. Not since my previous life, when programming had been a passion rather than a profession.
Pure, uncomplicated joy.

Lilith read the morning report: *"Target at office until 2:14 a.m. Working alone. Crash handler debugging. No visitors."*
She set the tablet down and stared out the window of her downtown office. The city sprawled beneath her, twenty floors below, but she wasn't seeing it.
She was seeing a man who had been famous for never spending a night alone, voluntarily debugging a stack trace in an empty office.
"How much of your past was acting?" she'd asked him.
*Enough*, he'd said.
It wasn't an answer. But the more she watched, the more she began to suspect it was the only one she was going to get—and perhaps the only one that mattered.

Mike heard about the engagement from a mutual acquaintance at a charity function. The news was delivered with the usual socialite enthusiasm—*"John Carter, can you believe it? Settling down! With Lilith Smith, no less! I heard the contract meeting was absolutely dramatic..."*
Mike smiled his golden smile and said all the right things. *"Wonderful news. They'll be good for each other. John's always been... spirited."*
Inside, he was replaying memories.
In his first life, John had exploded at that meeting. Stormed out. Called the marriage a farce. Started the long, slow spiral that Mike had so carefully, so patiently, helped along. The clubs. The models. The anonymous tips to Lilith's security. The whispered rumors in the right ears. Every piece placed precisely, every trap sprung perfectly.
It had been beautiful.
But this time, John hadn't exploded. There'd been some kind of scene—a shattered vase, some yelling—but in the end, the contract was signed. The engagement was proceeding. John Carter was apparently a fiancé in good standing.
Mike told himself it didn't matter. The old urges were still there. The playboy couldn't stay caged forever. He just needed the right opportunity.
He started making calls.
Within a week, an invitation arrived at the Carter family's address—forwarded to Rosewood, then forwarded again to my office by Mrs. Harlow. The grand opening of an exclusive nightclub downtown. Champagne. VIP access. A guest list packed with models and socialites.
Mike had timed it perfectly. A 1 a.m. anonymous tip was already drafted, ready to land in Lilith's security inbox just when the party would be at its peak.
He settled back to wait for the inevitable scandal.

I found the invitation in a stack of mail I'd been ignoring for three days. I opened it, read it, and set it aside without a second thought.
That night, the team was deep in a debate about the logging architecture. Chen had prototyped the lock-free SPSC queue approach, but Marcus had concerns about cache line contention under heavy thread contention. The whiteboard was now a battlefield of latency diagrams, throughput calculations, and increasingly heated annotations.
"Look," I said, picking up a fresh marker, "if we're going to support hundreds of worker threads all screaming into the logging system at sixty frames per second, the synchronized ring buffer is going to bottleneck. We need per-thread queues that flush independently."
"But then we lose total ordering," Dana pointed out. "Debug logs without temporal coherence are just noise."
"High-frequency logs can be ordered post-hoc using the high-resolution timestamp counter. We're already integrating it for the profiler anyway."
The debate continued until nearly midnight. When we finally broke, I ordered pizza for the night crew and sat down at my workstation to prototype the queue implementation myself.
At 1 a.m., I was debugging a subtle alignment issue in the SPSC queue's header. At 1:30, I fixed it. At 2:14, I finally stood up, stretched, and headed home. No clubs. No champagne. No models.
The anonymous tip Lilith never received because it was automatically archived by her new harassment filter protocols would have been entirely useless anyway.
Mike waited.
No scandal came.
He checked the club's guest list. John Carter's name wasn't on it. He checked the gossip sites. Nothing. He checked with his contacts in Lilith's household. The security team had nothing to report except late nights and pizza deliveries.
*He must have missed the invitation*, Mike told himself. *Unfortunate timing. There will be other chances.*
He didn't know it yet, but he had already fired his first shot. And it had landed exactly nowhere.

By the six-week mark, Lilith had stopped reading the security reports with suspicion. She read them instead with a quiet, growing fascination.
*"Target arrived at office 7:42 a.m. Morning stand-up meeting with team. Primary topic: profiler instrumentation overhead. Target proposed sampling-based approach with minimal stack walking. Team consensus positive."*
*"Target left office 11:18 p.m. Dinner with team (Thai delivery). Conversation topics: custom memory allocator design, per-thread arenas, fragmentation mitigation strategies."*
*"Target spent Saturday at office. Alone. Working on asset pipeline prototype. No phone calls. No visitors. Ordered lunch from deli downstairs."*
She set the tablet down one evening and realized she was smiling. Not the practiced, public smile she wore at board meetings and galas. A real one. Small. Private. Entirely unbidden.
This man was not the John Carter she'd grown up dreading.
This man was something else entirely.

Our first weekly dinner happened by accident.
Lilith had been traveling for business—closing a logistics acquisition in Singapore—and by the time she returned, I'd been living at Rosewood for nearly two weeks. We'd exchanged perhaps twenty words total. Her east wing, my west wing. The vast shared living spaces between us occupied mostly by Mrs. Harlow and the occasional house staff.
I was in the kitchen one Wednesday evening, reheating leftovers from the office—someone had brought in Thai, and Dana had insisted I take the remaining green curry—when Lilith walked in. She was still in her business attire, a tablet tucked under one arm, her dark hair slightly disheveled in a way that suggested a long day.
She stopped when she saw me. I stopped when I saw her.
"The kitchen staff—" she started.
"Gave them the night off," I said. "Long day for everyone. Mrs. Harlow said you'd be back. I didn't know if you'd eaten."
The silence stretched for an awkward beat.
"There's enough curry for two," I added. "It's not fancy, but it's edible."
Lilith stood in the doorway for what felt like a full ten seconds. Then she set her tablet down on the counter and took a seat at the kitchen island.
"Green curry?"
"It was Dana's pick."
"Dana?"
"One of my programmers. She has strong opinions about Thai food and even stronger opinions about move semantics."
Lilith nodded slowly. "And what are her opinions on move semantics?"
I launched into an explanation—something about rvalue references and copy elision and a particularly elegant approach Dana had suggested for the asset transfer pipeline. Halfway through, I realized I was rambling. I stopped, slightly embarrassed.
"Sorry. That's probably not what you were asking."
"No," Lilith said, and there was something in her voice I hadn't heard before. "It's exactly what I was asking. Go on."
So I went on.
And she listened. Genuinely listened. Asked questions about the memory allocator. Wanted to understand why I'd chosen per-thread arenas instead of a global heap. When I explained the cache locality benefits, she nodded thoughtfully and said, "That's not so different from supply chain routing. Decentralized nodes with local optimization."
I blinked. "That's... actually a good analogy."
"I know," she said, and for just a moment, I thought I saw the corner of her mouth twitch.
