


# Chapter 1: The Wrong Room
Dying at twenty-nine from overwork isn't the flex people think it is.
One moment I'm face-down on a keyboard, the fluorescent office lights buzzing their funeral dirge, and the next I'm staring at a ceiling I don't recognise, in a body that feels wrong—too light, too young, too *not-aching*. It took about three days of confused bumbling to piece together what happened. Transmigration. Isekai. The whole cliché package, except instead of a fantasy kingdom with dragons, I landed in a world that looks almost identical to my old one.
Almost.
The first clue was the news broadcast playing in the kitMiller while I pretended to know who "my" mother was. A panel of nine female politicians and one very uncomfortable-looking man were debating marriage reform. The chyron read: "Bureau of Male Welfare proposes extending bachelor tax to eighteen-year-olds."
The second clue was walking outside.
I counted. Thirty-seven women. One man. And the man was being escorted by what appeared to be a personal security detail while carrying a tiny dog and wearing more jewellery than I'd ever seen outside a rap video.
"Yeah," I muttered to myself, tugging at my collar. "This is going to be a problem."
---
The mandatory marriage law hit my inbox—well, my physical mailbox, because apparently this world's tech is weirdly inconsistent—three weeks after I arrived. I had until my twentieth birthday to find a wife, or the government would find one for me. The pamphlet was aggressively cheerful about it. "Congratulations on your upcoming eligibility! Remember: a bonded man is a safe man!"
I stared at that pamphlet for a long time.
On one hand, the idea of being *assigned* a spouse made my skin crawl.
On the other hand...
I looked out the window. A man about my age was lounging in a hammock strung between two trees in the park across the street. Two women were fanning him with oversized palm leaves while a third fed him what looked like chocolate-covered strawberries. He wasn't even handsome. He was aggressively average. His superpower was simply existing while male.
*That could be me*, whispered a treacherous voice in the back of my mind. *No more deadlines. No more overtime. No more collapsing at your desk because Dave from accounting "really needed those spreadsheets by morning."*
I shook my head. I was getting ahead of myself. First, I needed to actually meet someone. And that meant blind dates. Lots of them, probably. My new mother—a terrifyingly energetic woman who had somehow produced me in this reality—had already started matchmaking.
"The Ashford girl is lovely," she'd said that morning, pressing a folded paper into my hand. "Her family's in textiles. Very stable. Very respectable. She's expecting you at the Celestial Garden restaurant, private room, at two o'clock."
"I don't remember agreeing to this."
"You agreed when you were born male and turned eighteen." She patted my cheek. "Don't slouch. And try not to say anything strange about your fantasies about a *past life* People will think you're eccentric."
She said "eccentric" the way someone might say "contagious."
Fair enough.
---
I decided to walk to the restaurant. It was only twenty minutes, and I wanted more time to observe this world before being thrust into the performative nightmare that is a blind date.
The streets were... surreal.
Women everywhere. Walking, talking, working construction, driving delivery trucks, arguing over parking spaces. The few men I spotted were either elderly couples shuffling along together or young guys my age being *attended to* like rare orchids. I passed a café where a man was having his tea stirred for him. *Stirred*. By a woman in a business suit who was also on her phone closing what sounded like a multi-million-dollar deal.
"What's the ratio on the street right now?" I murmured, counting under my breath. "One to thirty? Maybe one to thirty-five?"
No wonder men didn't work. Why would you, when the entire economic system seemed designed to wrap you in silk and feed you grapes?
I caught my reflection in a shop window and paused. The new face was... fine. Better than fine, honestly. My old body had been a monument to sedentary office life—pale, soft, with the perpetually hunted look of a man who knows his inbox is never actually empty. This version of me was eighteen, with clear skin, decent cheekbones, and the kind of lean build that suggested I'd been fed well but not excessively. Dark hair. Dark eyes. The sort of face that might make someone look twice if they weren't distracted by the sheer statistical improbability of my gender.
I was so busy admiring myself that I didn't notice the stares at first.
But they were there.
Women pausing mid-stride. Heads turning. Whispers behind hands. A girl in a school uniform dropped her phone and didn't pick it up, just kept staring at me like I'd materialised from a daydream.
*Right*, I thought, heat creeping up my neck. *I'm the one-in-thirty today.*
I walked faster.
---
The Celestial Garden was exactly the kind of restaurant I'd never been able to afford in my old life. Crystal chandeliers. White tablecloths. Waitstaff who moved like ballet dancers and probably earned more than I ever did. The maître d' took one look at me—young, male, alone—and immediately produced a smile that was two parts welcome and one part hunger.
"Sir! Are you meeting someone? Let me escort you to—"
"I've got the room number," I said, waving the paper my mother had given me. My palms were slightly sweaty, which was annoying. I was supposed to be *confident*. Arrogant, even. That was the plan.
Ah, yes. The plan.
I'd spent the entire walk cooking it up. Here's the thing about blind dates: they're exhausting. All that posturing. All that trying to impress. I didn't want to spend the next two years cycling through awkward small talk with strangers who'd judge my earning potential or my family connections or whatever metric this world used to rank its precious men.
So instead, I was going to be insufferable.
Deliberately, strategically insufferable.
I'd walk in, make outrageous demands, watch her face cycle through shock and horror, and then—just when she was about to flee—I'd drop the act and reveal it was all a test of character. A joke! A bit! If she laughed, she was a keeper. If she threw her drink in my face, well, at least I'd have a story.
It was foolproof.
The paper read: **909**.
I squinted at my mother's handwriting. She had the penmanship of a doctor having a seizure, but the numbers were clear enough. Nine. Zero. Nine. Ninth floor, room nine. Made sense.
I rode the elevator alone, practicing my arrogant face in the mirrored walls. Shoulders back. Chin up. The trick was to project the kind of casual entitlement that said *I'm doing you a favour just by existing*. I'd seen it on half the men in this world already. How hard could it be?
The elevator dinged.
I found room 909 at the end of a quiet hallway, the door closed, soft light spilling from the gap at the bottom. My heart hammered against my ribs. Annoying. I'd died from overwork. I'd survived (sort of) transmigration. One girl in a private dining room shouldn't make me nervous.
But she wasn't just *one girl*, was she? She was my first real interaction with the bizarre dating culture of this world. My first test of whether I could actually hack it here, or whether I'd end up government-assigned to someone who'd treat me like a collectible figurine.
*Stop thinking. Start performing.*
I rolled my shoulders. Puffed out my chest. Stuffed my hands in my pockets and let a cocky smirk settle onto my face—the expression of a man who has never doubted himself for a single second in his entire life.
Then I kicked the door open.
---
The room was elegant in a restrained way. Soft gold lighting. A table for two set with crystal glasses and a single rose in a silver vase. And at the table, half-turned toward the door with a teacup suspended mid-air, was the most beautiful woman I had ever seen.
I'm not being poetic. I'm being literal.
She was about my age—eighteen, nineteen—with hair the colour of dark honey falling in waves past her shoulders. Her eyes were a shade of green that made me think of forests in fairy tales, the kind where you wander in and never quite find your way out. Her features were delicate but sharp, the bone structure of someone who'd never had to compromise. And her clothes...
Even I, with my limited fashion knowledge, could tell they cost more than my previous life's annual salary. Her dress was deep blue, simple in cut but shimmering faintly as she moved, and the jewellery at her throat and wrists caught the light in ways that screamed *serious money*.
For a half-second, I forgot my own plan.
Then I remembered.
*Arrogant. Insufferable. Go.*
"Yo," I said, striding into the room like I owned it. I didn't bother closing the door properly—let it swing shut on its own. "You must be my blind date."
She set down her teacup with a soft clink. Her expression didn't change. "And you must be the gentleman my mother arranged for me to meet."
"That's right." I circled the table, making a show of looking her up and down. "Name's John. John Doe. Yeah, laugh it up. I've heard all the jokes."
"I wasn't planning to laugh."
"Good. You're already doing better than most." I stopped behind the empty chair across from her, but I didn't sit. Sitting felt too deferential. Instead, I leaned against the back of the chair, arms crossed, smirk still in place. "What's your name?"
"Lilith."
I blinked. Lilith. It suited her—elegant, slightly dangerous, the kind of name that belonged to women who started wars or ended them. For a moment, something in my expression must have slipped, because her lips twitched.
"You like it," she said. Not a question.
"It's fine," I lied. "Now, before we waste each other's time, let me be clear about what I'm looking for."
I pushed off the chair and began to walk around her. My shoes made soft sounds on the carpet. She tracked me with her eyes but didn't turn her head, which was both unnerving and, I had to admit, impressive.
"First," I said, holding up a finger, "I want one million dollars. As a marriage price. Upfront."
"One million," she repeated, her tone perfectly neutral.
"Second, a car. Something nice. You figure it out. Third—" I gestured vaguely at the walls, the restaurant, the concept of property in general. "—a house. Private. Good neighbourhood. None of that apartment complex nonsense."
"Of course."
Her calm was throwing me off. Most people would have at least *frowned* by now. But Lilith just watched me with those forest-green eyes, her expression mild, almost... amused?
I was imagining it. Had to be.
"And finally," I said, leaning in slightly for dramatic effect, "let's get one thing straight. I don't work. Not now, not ever. I'm not going to earn money, I'm not going to manage money, I'm not even going to *think* about money. You'll handle all of that. You'll provide whatever I want, whenever I want it, and I won't lift a finger. Got it?"

Then she smiled. A real smile, the kind that crinkled the corners of her eyes and made her look less like a CEO's daughter and more like a girl who'd just been given exactly what she wanted for her birthday.
"OK," she said.
My brain made a sound like a record scratch.
"...OK?"
"OK." She reached for a small bell beside her plate and rang it once. A panel in the wall slid open—I hadn't even noticed it was there—and a woman in a sharp black suit stepped through, carrying a tablet.
"Madam Lilith?"
"Ms. Miller, I need a marriage contract drafted. The terms are as follows." Lilith began ticking off points on her fingers, her voice businesslike. "One million dollar marriage price, transferred immediately. A private vehicle of the groom's choosing. A residential property, specifications to be determined. And a clause stating that the groom will not engage in any paid employment or financial management, and that all material needs will be provided for by the bride."
Ms. Miller didn't even blink. "Understood. I'll have the document ready in ten minutes."
The panel slid shut. Lilith turned back to me, her smile still in place, and I realised with dawning horror that my brain had completely stopped working.
"But—" I started.
"Would you like some tea while we wait? It's jasmine. Quite good."
I stared at her. She gazed back, serene as a cat with cream on its whiskers. Something had gone terribly, catastrophically wrong with my plan, and I couldn't figure out what.
*She's supposed to be offended*, my internal monologue screamed. *She's supposed to storm out. You're supposed to chase after her laughing, explain the joke, and bond over the absurdity. Why is she saying OK?! Who says OK to this?!*
"Ready," Ms. Miller announced, stepping back into the room with a freshly printed document. She set it on the table between us, along with two pens. "Please review the terms before signing."
The contract was a monument to my own stupidity. Every ludicrous demand, neatly typed. I could feel a cold sweat beading on my forehead. This had to stop. Screw the bit.
"Wait—wait" I stammered, holding my hands up as if the paper were a weapon. "What do you mean, OK? None of that was serious! It was a joke! A terrible, poorly-conceived prank to break the ice. I was trying to make you leave. I'm not actually a human-shaped financial disaster!"
Lilith’s expression didn't change, but Ms. Miller’s did. She clicked her tongue, a dry, legalistic sound.
"A joke?" Ms. Miller said, her voice flat. "Sir, under the National Marital Preliminaries Act, Section 4, Subsection B, any verbal exchange of terms during a state-registered blind date constitutes a formal negotiation. Once both parties verbally agree to the stated terms—you by proposing them, and Madam Lilith by accepting them—a binding preliminary contract is formed."
My mouth opened and closed. A small, gold-embossed plaque on the wall suddenly seemed to scream its message: "By law, this room is under constant audio-visual surveillance for the security and contractual validity of all dates held within."
Ms. Miller continued, mercilessly tapping her tablet. "Withdrawal from a mutually agreed-upon contract is, of course, possible. The penalty for withdrawal without legitimate cause, as determined by the state's Relationship Arbitration Board, is compensation equivalent to five times the total stated value of the agreement. With one million dollars, a vehicle, a property, and ongoing lifetime maintenance... the compensation would easily be in the eight-figure range. Will your family be providing the surety, or will you be taking out a bond?"
I looked from her to Lilith. Lilith took a delicate sip of her tea, her eyes sparkling with something that was absolutely, terrifyingly not amusement. It was victory.
"That's... that's extortion," I whispered.
Ms. Miller offered a thin, professional smile that didn't reach her eyes. "It's the law, Mr. Doe."
John, trapped by his own unfunny joke, is now legally bound to a woman who views his laziness as the ultimate asset.















The door banged open.
I spun around. A tall young man stood in the doorway, his face twisted into an expression of barely contained fury. He was handsome in a sharp, polished way, but his cufflinks were bejewelled and his collar was so stiff it looked like it was strangling him.
"What," he said, biting off each word, "is the meaning of this? I was told a private suite would be prepared for my interview with Lady Lilith. Who is this... person?"
I looked at him. I looked at Lilith. I looked at the contract.
"Interview?" I said weakly. "I thought this was a blind date."
The man drew himself up to his full height. "I am Damian Ashford, third son of the Ashford Conglomerate. I don't *do* blind dates. I grant marriage *audiences*." His gaze swept over me like I was something stuck to his shoe. "And I certainly do not share them with strays. Lilith, explain."
Lilith didn't rise. She stirred her tea, the picture of calm. "Damian. You're late by seven minutes. I've already made my choice." She nodded toward me. "This gentleman will be my husband."
Damian laughed. It was an ugly sound, all edges and spite. "You can't be serious. Look at him. What could he possibly offer that I don't?" He spread his arms wide, a performer gesturing at his own magnificence. "I came prepared to be generous. I'll take only thirty percent of the voting shares of your largest company as a wedding token. You may keep the rest—on paper. I'll require a private estate on Azure Hill, a monthly allowance of two million credits, and your board seat. I call that more than reasonable for a man of my pedigree."
"That's..." The words slipped out before I could stop them. "That's not generous at all."
Damian rounded on me, his face reddening. "Silence! A nobody like you could never comprehend the responsibilities of a high-value husband!" He turned back to Lilith. "Get rid of him. I'm prepared to sign a prenuptial that lets you pay my family a loyalty fee. You won't find a more accommodating man."
Lilith set her cup down. The clink was delicate. Final.
"Accommodating?" Her voice was soft, but something in it made Damian flinch. "You just asked for controlling interest in my grandfather's company, a house worth more than a small country, and a salary that could fund a war. You've called yourself 'high-value' while arriving late to a meeting you demanded." She rose to her feet, and I realised with a start that she was taller than I'd thought. Not physically—we were about the same height—but her presence expanded to fill every corner of the room. "Damian, I turned down a duke's son last week who only wanted a castle. You are a downgrade."
Damian sputtered. "You—I'll report this insult to the Male Protection Bureau! You can't treat a man this way!"
"Try." Lilith's smile was sharp enough to draw blood. "The Bureau prefers men who honour appointments. Now leave, or I'll have my legal team audit your family's tax filings for entertainment."
The colour drained from Damian's face. He threw one last venomous glare at me—I could practically hear the promises of future revenge—and stormed out, slamming the door hard enough to rattle the chandelier.
Silence descended.
I stared at the door. I stared at the contract. I stared at Lilith, who was smoothing her dress and sitting back down as though nothing had happened.
"He was going to report *you*?" I heard myself say. "What kind of world is this?"
Lilith looked up at me, and her eyes were glinting with something that might have been triumph. "One where you just promised to let me handle all the money and never interfere with my work." She slid a pen across the table toward me. "You're a unicorn, John Doe. Now—sign."
"Wait." I held up both hands, backpedalling so fast I nearly tripped over my own feet. "Wait, wait, wait. I need to explain. That whole speech—the demands, the attitude—it wasn't real. It was a test. A joke. I was going to see how you'd react and then reveal the punchline, and then we'd both laugh about it, and—"
"Your voice stated clear, actionable terms," Lilith interrupted. "You requested I, and I quote"—she glanced at Ms. Miller, who tapped her tablet—"obediently provide whatever you want. I find that adorable."
*Adorable?!*
"If you withdraw now," Lilith continued, her tone perfectly pleasant, "I could sue for emotional distress, breach of oral contract, and—since my lawyer recorded our entire conversation—fraudulent inducement."
I looked at Ms. Miller. Ms. Miller nodded, her expression helpful and utterly terrifying.
"Or," Lilith said, tapping the contract with one manicured finger, "you could sign, and walk out of here a very wealthy, very free man who never has to work again. Your choice."
Never.
Has to work.
Again.
I looked at the pen. Such a small thing. Such a simple thing. A signature, and the life I'd dreamed about—the life I'd died *not* having—would be mine. No more sixteen-hour days. No more stress headaches. No more staring at spreadsheets until my eyes burned and my soul shrivelled into a tiny, exhausted prune.
I thought about my death. The sudden chest pain. The way the office lights flickered as I collapsed. The last thought I'd had before everything went dark: *I never even took a vacation.*
I looked at Lilith. Beautiful, terrifying, inexplicable Lilith, who held my "unreasonable" dream in the palm of her hand and was threatening to sue me into compliance.
What kind of man would I be if I walked away from this?
A stupid one, probably.
I picked up the pen.
"One condition," I said, my voice steadier than I felt.
Lilith raised an elegant eyebrow. "Name it."
"All the fancy snacks I want. Unlimited budget. No questions asked."
For the first time since I'd kicked in her door, Lilith's composure cracked. A laugh escaped her—surprised, genuine, chased with something warm. "Done. I'll add a snack clause."
Then I signed.
The pen scratched across the paper, and just like that, I became a kept man.
Lilith picked up her copy of the contract and examined my signature with the same satisfaction I'd seen on collectors acquiring rare art. "Excellent," she said, her smile turning soft and predatory at the edges. "The car will be at your mother's house in an hour. Pack light—you won't need anything I haven't already arranged."
She stood, offered me her hand. I shook it. Her grip was firm, her skin warm, and she held on just a beat longer than professional courtesy required.
"Welcome to your new life," she said. "*Husband.*"
I had a feeling—deep in my bones, deep in whatever remained of my overworked, transmigrated soul—that nothing was ever going to be the same.
And honestly?
I was starting to think that might not be a bad thing.



















# Chapter 2: Moving Day
The car arrived exactly one hour later.
I know because I was standing at the window, watching the street like a meerkat who'd just been told a hawk was in the neighbourhood but wasn't sure if that was good news or bad. My mother—this world's version of her, anyway—was in the kitMiller humming something aggressively cheerful about "finally" and "a good match" and "I knew my boy would land someone important."
The car pulled up, and I stopped breathing for a second.
It wasn't a car. It was a *vehicle*. A statement. A sleek silver thing with curves that looked like they'd been designed by someone who thought aerodynamics was a personality trait and windows tinted dark enough to hide a celebrity scandal. The hood ornament alone probably cost more than my previous life's annual rent.
"Mother," I called, my voice weirdly calm. "There's a spaceship in our driveway."
She bustled over, drying her hands on a dish towel, and looked out the window.
Then she made a sound like a kettle reaching boiling point, swayed dramatically, and collapsed onto the sofa in what I can only describe as a choreographed faint.
"Oh!" she cried, one hand pressed to her forehead, the other groping for the armrest. "Oh, my son! My brilliant, beautiful son! I knew it! I knew from the moment you were born that you were destined for greatness!"
"You're faking."
"I am *overcome with maternal pride*." She cracked an eye open. "Is the car still there?"
"Yes."
"Then I'm still overcome." She sat up with the speed of someone who'd never actually lost consciousness, beaming at me with an intensity that was frankly alarming. "A Rolls-Silverton Spectre. Do you know how much one of those costs? Do you know who *makes* those?"
"I'm going to guess 'Lilith's company' based on the way this day is going."
"Don't be silly. Lilith doesn't make cars. Lilith's *family* makes cars. Among other things." She grabbed my shoulders and squeezed. "John. Johnny. My little boy. You have somehow—and I genuinely don't understand how, given you spent last week trying to make toast in the refrigerator—"
"It looked like a toaster."
"—you have somehow landed the single most eligible woman in the entire city. Possibly the country. And I am taking full credit for this."
"You set up a blind date. I went to the wrong room."
She paused. "What?"
"Nothing. Long story. I'll explain later. Or never."
A polite knock sounded at the door. My mother released me, smoothed her apron, and glided to answer it with the dignity of a queen receiving foreign dignitaries.
The woman standing on our doorstep was dressed in a crisp black uniform that probably cost more than my entire wardrobe. She inclined her head precisely fifteen degrees.
"Master John Doe? I am Margaret, head of household operations for Madam Lilith Vance. I've been sent to collect you and your belongings." Her gaze swept the modest living room behind me with no visible judgment, which somehow felt like more judgment than if she'd openly sneered. "The car is waiting."
"I—yes. Right." I looked down at myself. Same clothes I'd worn to the restaurant. "Should I change? Pack? Do I need—"
"Madam Lilith has prepared everything you'll require. However, personal effects are, of course, welcome." Margaret paused. "She suggested you might want to bring any 'favourite snacks.' Her words."
I felt my face do something complicated. Half embarrassment, half grudging appreciation. The woman had known me for approximately twenty minutes and had already identified my priorities.
"I'll just... grab a bag."
---
Packing was surreal.
I stood in my bedroom—was it still mine? had it ever been?—and stared at my possessions. A few changes of clothes. A second-hand tablet. A half-eaten bag of chips. The accumulated detritus of three weeks of confused existence in a body and world that weren't quite mine.
What do you pack for moving into a billionaire's mansion?
I threw in clothes. The chips. A notebook I'd been using to document the differences between this world and my old one. A pen that looked vaguely like one I'd owned before I died.
That was it. That was my whole life.
I paused at the door, looking back at the room. The bed where I'd lain awake, staring at the ceiling, trying to process reincarnation. The window where I'd watched a man being hand-fed grapes and decided, *yes, that's the dream*.
"John?" My mother's voice drifted up the stairs. "The nice terrifying woman is looking at her watch."
"Coming."
---
The car's interior was like being swallowed by a very comfortable cloud. Leather seats that seemed to adjust themselves to my body. Ambient lighting that shifted to a warm gold when I sat down. A console that offered me "travelling refreshments" in a soothing voice.
I accepted a sparkling water and tried not to hyperventilate.
*This is fine*, I told myself. *You signed a contract. You're married now. Technically. Probably there's paperwork and ceremonies and things, but effectively—you're someone's husband. Someone who has a head of household operations and a car with opinions about lighting.*
"Master John?" Margaret's voice came through a speaker I couldn't locate. "We'll arrive in approximately twenty minutes. Madam Lilith has asked me to relay that you should feel free to nap if you wish."
Nap. In a moving vehicle. Because I was the kind of person who did that now.
"Thanks," I said to no visible person. "I'm good. Just... processing."
"Of course, sir."
The city slid past the tinted windows. We left the modest neighbourhoods behind and entered districts I hadn't seen before—wider streets, larger buildings, more green space. Then even those gave way to something else entirely.
Gates. Walls. Mansions set so far back from the road they looked like paintings in the distance.
And then we turned onto a private drive, and I realised all those other mansions had been the *warm-up act*.
---
Lilith's estate wasn't a house. It was a small country that had decided to cosplay as a building.
The main structure sprawled across what had to be several acres, all pale stone and modern glass and architectural lines that managed to be both aggressive and elegant. Gardens surrounded it in carefully curated waves of green and colour. A fountain—no, *fountains*, plural—marked the circular driveway. And beyond the main building, I could see additional wings stretching into the distance like the arms of a very wealthy octopus.
"Master John?" Margaret had materialised at my door, which was now open. I hadn't noticed it opening. "Welcome to Vance Estate."
I got out of the car on legs that felt slightly disconnected from my body. "This is... a lot."
"Madam Lilith's family has owned the land for six generations. The current main house was completed four years ago. It includes five residential wings, three formal gardens, a private lake, a helipad, and—"
"A lake?" My voice might have cracked. "She has a lake?"
"Stocked with koi. The ornamental bridge is quite lovely in spring."
"Of course it is. Why wouldn't there be a bridge?"
Margaret's expression didn't change, but I got the distinct impression she was amused. "If you'll follow me, I'll show you to your quarters."
---
My quarters.
The phrase alone was absurd. I'd grown up—well, I'd grown up in two different realities now, and in neither of them had I ever had *quarters*. Rooms. Corners. The space under a desk where I'd once hidden from my boss for twenty blessed minutes. Those were my historical benchmarks.
The wing I was led to was called the East Wing, and it had clearly been recently prepared. Fresh flowers in crystal vases. Art on the walls that looked original and expensive and possibly stolen from a museum. Carpets thick enough to sleep on.
"This is your private suite," Margaret said, opening a door into a living room larger than my mother's entire house. "Bedroom through there. Bathroom en suite. The wardrobe has been stocked with your measurements—Madam Lilith accessed your family's clothing registry."
"She what?"
"The walk-in closet is climate-controlled. Touch panel on the wall adjusts the lighting. Your personal chef is available from six AM to midnight via the intercom. Any dietary preferences or restrictions?"
I opened my mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. "I... like chips?"
"Noted. Chef Maria has already been briefed on common comfort foods for young men in transition. She'll check in with you directly." Margaret produced a tablet and swiped through screens with practised efficiency. "Now. Your schedule."
"Schedule?" My heart, which had been doing so well, performed a small anxious flip. "I don't—she said I didn't have to work."
"You don't." Margaret turned the tablet to show me. The screen displayed a calendar. Tomorrow's date had exactly three entries:
*7:00 AM – 9:00 AM: SLEEP (Do Not Disturb)*
*9:00 AM – 10:00 AM: Breakfast (Chef Maria will prepare to preference)*
*10:00 AM – 12:00 PM: RELAX (see attached relaxation options)*
I stared at the screen. Margaret swiped to the next page, which was titled "Relaxation Options" and included a numbered list with forty-seven items. Forty-seven. Item number twelve was "Cloud watching (blankets and grass pad provided upon request)." Item thirty-one was "Nap (assorted locations available)."
"Madam Lilith was very specific about the first week," Margaret said. "She wants you to 'settle in' and 'decompress from whatever was making you look so hunted at the restaurant.' Her words, sir."
I sat down on the nearest chair. It was softer than any chair had a right to be.
"This is a prank," I said. "This is an elaborate prank, and there are cameras, and someone is going to jump out and tell me I've been—"
"It's not a prank, Master John."
"No one expects me to do anything? No chores? No obligations? No 'since you're here, could you just—'?"
Margaret's expression softened by the tiniest fraction. "Sir. You are a man in a bonded marriage to one of the wealthiest women in the nation. No one expects you to do anything except exist and be content. If you're content, Madam Lilith is content. If Madam Lilith is content, the household is content." She paused. "Would you like me to request a relaxation consultation? We have a specialist who can help you identify your preferred leisure activities."
A relaxation consultation. A *relaxation consultation*.
"I think," I said faintly, "I need to lie down."
"The bedroom is through that door. I'll have Chef Maria prepare a light snack tray."
She vanished, presumably to do exactly that, because nothing in this house was going to make sense ever again.
---
I lay on the bed—king-sized, ridiculous thread count, pillows that felt like they were filled with angel feathers—and stared at the ceiling.
No work. No expectations. Nobody who needed spreadsheets or reports or "just one more thing before the weekend." Just... this. A schedule that told me to sleep in and then relax. A personal chef who'd been briefed on comfort foods. A relaxation specialist who could, apparently, be summoned like a genie.
I should have been ecstatic.
Instead, I was suspicious.
It wasn't natural. People didn't just *give* you things. There was always a catch, always a price, always a moment where the other shoe dropped. I'd learned that lesson in two lifetimes. You didn't get something for nothing.
But what was the catch here? What did Lilith actually want from me?
She'd agreed to my absurd demands. She'd threatened to sue me if I *didn't* sign the contract. She'd sent a luxury car and prepared an entire wing and put "RELAX" on my calendar in capital letters.
A knock at the door made me jolt upright. A young woman in a chef's coat entered, carrying a tray that smelled like heaven had decided to cater a small event.
"Master John? I'm Maria. Margaret said you might want something to eat." She set the tray on a side table and lifted the covers one by one. "We have truffle fries, sliders, fresh fruit, and a chocolate mousse that I'm particularly proud of. Also some chips—I wasn't sure what kind you preferred, so I brought four options."
Four bags of chips. Arranged on a silver platter. Like they were fine chocolates.
"I don't understand anything about this world," I said.
Maria smiled, warm and genuine and not at all like someone who thought this was weird. "Most new husbands say that, sir. It passes. Try the mousse first—it's got a hint of sea salt."
She left me alone with my snack tray and my confusion.
I ate the mousse. It was, objectively, the best thing I'd ever tasted.
And somewhere between the third truffle fry and the second bag of chips, I felt something shift in my chest. A loosening. A quiet voice whispering: *What if this is real? What if there isn't a catch? What if you actually get to just... be happy?*
I didn't trust that voice.
But I was too tired, too full of chocolate mousse, and too enveloped in the softest bed in human history to argue with it.
Tomorrow, I decided, I would investigate. Ask questions. Figure out what Lilith's angle was. Prepare for the inevitable reveal.
Tonight, I was going to sleep better than I had in either of my lives.
---
Somewhere in the west wing of the estate, Lilith Vance reviewed the day's security footage on her tablet. She watched John eat the mousse, watched him investigate the walk-in closet with the expression of a man discovering buried treasure, watched him bounce experimentally on the bed and then freeze, as if expecting someone to scold him.
She smiled.
"Margaret," she said, not looking up.
"Madam?"
"Triple the snack budget."
"It's already quite generous, Madam."
"Then quadruple it. And arrange for the relaxation consultant to visit tomorrow. He looked confused when you mentioned it. I want him to understand he's allowed to enjoy this."
"Understood." Margaret made a note. "Shall I schedule your first newlywed dinner?"
"Give him three days. Let him settle." Lilith set down the tablet, her smile curving into something more complex. "He's going to be interesting, Margaret. I can feel it."
"That's what you said about the last potential match, Madam. He tried to reorganise your board of directors and install his cousin as CFO."
"This one's different." Lilith's eyes drifted to the window, toward the east wing where the lights were dimming. "This one asked for unlimited snacks and looked terrified when I said yes. He has no idea what he's worth." She paused. "He has no idea what he's worth to *me*."
Margaret wisely said nothing.
Outside, the koi in the private lake swam lazy circles beneath the ornamental bridge, and somewhere in the vast house, John Doe fell asleep fully clothed, a half-eaten bag of chips still clutched in one hand, dreaming of absolutely nothing at all.























# Chapter 3: The Clauses and the Snack Budget
I woke up to sunlight and the distant sound of someone humming.
For approximately four seconds, I was at peace. Then my brain caught up with my circumstances, and I sat bolt upright in a bed that wasn't mine, in a room that was larger than my previous apartment, wearing yesterday's clothes and still clutching an empty bag of chips like a security blanket.
"Right," I said to the empty room. "That happened."
The ceiling offered no commentary. It was a very nice ceiling—painted a soft cream, with moulding that looked hand-carved—and even it seemed to be politely waiting for me to have my existential crisis in private.
I peeled myself out of bed and investigated the en suite bathroom. It had a rainfall showerhead, heated floors, and more towel options than I'd ever considered necessary. *Hand towel. Bath towel. Body sheet. Face cloth. Spa wrap.* I spent a full minute just opening drawers and closing them again, like a raccoon who'd broken into a spa.
When I finally emerged—showered, dressed in clothes that had mysteriously appeared in the wardrobe overnight, feeling more human but no less confused—there was a note on my bedside table.
*Breakfast in the conservatory. 9 AM. —L*
The handwriting was elegant and decisive. I stared at it for too long, then checked the time. Eight forty-five. The schedule in my room still said *SLEEP* until nine, but apparently Lilith had other plans.
I found my way to the conservatory through a combination of following subtle signage, backtracking twice, and eventually being rescued by a passing staff member who guided me with the gentle patience of someone used to directing lost husbands.
The conservatory was a glass-walled room filled with plants that looked expensive enough to have their own insurance policies. Morning light poured through the panes, and at a small table set for two, Lilith was reading something on a tablet, a cup of tea cooling beside her.
She looked up as I entered, and her expression shifted into something I was starting to recognise—amusement wrapped in composure, like a cat watching a mouse it had decided not to eat.
"Good morning, husband. Did you sleep well?"
"Too well. I think the mattress has some kind of sedative technology." I hovered by the chair across from her. "Is this seat...?"
"It's yours. Sit. Maria's been experimenting since six AM." She gestured to the table, which was laden with enough food for a small dinner party. "She wanted to make a good impression."
I sat. I stared at the spread—pastries, fresh fruit, eggs prepared three ways, something that looked like pancakes but smelled like vanilla heaven—and felt my stomach make a sound that was somewhere between gratitude and fear.
"There's enough here for ten people."
"Maria gets nervous. She had fewer options yesterday and felt it reflected poorly on her." Lilith took a sip of tea. "You'll learn that the staff takes your satisfaction very seriously."
"Why?"
"Because I do."
She said it so simply, so matter-of-fact, that it took me a moment to process. Then my face heated, and I busied myself with loading a plate to avoid having to respond. The pancakes—if that's what they were—melted on my tongue. I made a sound I wasn't proud of.
Lilith's lips twitched. "Good?"
"Mm."
"I'm glad." She set down her tablet and folded her hands on the table. "Now. We should discuss the contract."
I stopped chewing. "The contract."
"The full version. What you signed at the restaurant was a preliminary summary. The actual document was delivered this morning." She produced a leather-bound folder from the chair beside her and placed it on the table between us. It was thick enough to stop a bullet. "I thought we'd go through it together."
I swallowed my pancake. It went down with slightly more difficulty than before.
"Full version," I repeated.
"Standard marital contract. Two hundred and forty-seven pages."
"Two hundred and—" I choked. "Forty-seven?"
"My legal team is thorough." She opened the folder with the casual grace of someone who'd read every page and probably memorised the good parts. "Don't worry. I'll summarise. Most of it is boilerplate about asset allocation, inheritance protocols, and what happens if either party decides to open a competing business in the same sector."
"That's... specific."
"We live in a litigious society."
I set down my fork. The food was still fantastic, but my stomach had tightened into a knot. Here it was. Here was the catch I'd been waiting for. Hidden in the fine print was something I'd missed, something that would turn my lazy paradise into indentured servitude, something—
"Shall I start with the notable clauses?" Lilith said, flipping to a flagged page. "Clause Twelve: Spousal Employment Restrictions. You are forbidden from accepting any position of employment, paid or voluntary, without my express written consent."
I blinked. "You already said I didn't have to work."
"This makes it legally binding. In both directions." She looked at me over the top of the folder. "It means no one can pressure you into a job you don't want. It also means no rival company can attempt to hire you as a way to access my business interests."
"Would that actually happen?"
"It's happened to other husbands. Industrial espionage is less cloak-and-dagger when you can just offer a bored spouse a 'fun consulting opportunity' and wait for them to mention the wrong thing at dinner." She turned a page. "You're a shielded asset now. My board of directors is very pleased."
I wasn't sure how to feel about being an "asset," but the protective logic was hard to argue with. "So it's not just about making me lazy?"
"It's about making you safe." She tilted her head. "The laziness is a bonus."
"Right. Okay." I picked up my fork again, then put it down. "What else?"
"Clause Thirty-Four: Social Obligations. You are required to attend a minimum of one formal social event per month as my spouse."
I felt my face fall. "One per month?"
"Minimum. Maximum is at your discretion, though I'd advise against more than weekly. The press becomes tiresome."
"One per month," I said again, trying out the words. "Like, a party? A gala? One of those things where people stand around making small talk and judging each other's outfits?"
"Precisely."
"And I have to go?"
"You have to go." Lilith's expression softened almost imperceptibly. "I'll be there with you. It's not as terrible as you're imagining."
"I'm imagining a lot of things."
"Then imagine me in a ballgown, verbally dismantling anyone who tries to make you uncomfortable."
That was... actually a nice image. I filed it away for later examination and gestured for her to continue.
"And finally," Lilith said, and I didn't miss the slight pause, the way her eyes brightened with suppressed mirth, "Clause Eighty-Nine: the Snack Budget."
The snack budget. The *snack budget*. It had its own clause.
"How absurdly high is it?" I asked, because I'd already started learning how her mind worked.
Lilith turned the folder around and slid it toward me, pointing to a figure near the bottom of the page. Her nail polish was perfect. The number underneath it was not.
I leaned forward. I read the number. I read it again.
"That's—" My voice came out strangled. "That's a monthly figure?"
"Annual."
"Oh, annual. Well, that's reasonable—"
"Six figures annual. Specifically allocated to snacks."
Silence.
"Lilith."
"Yes, John?"
"That's a snack budget that could buy a house."
"Two houses, depending on the neighbourhood." She was definitely enjoying this. "You asked for unlimited snacks with no questions asked. My lawyers took you at your word."
"You can't—" I sputtered. "This is—nobody needs this much money for *snacks*. This is ridiculous. It's excessive. It's—"
"Adorable," Lilith said.
I stopped mid-word. "What?"
"Adorable." She reached across the table and, with all the casualness in the world, placed her hand on top of my head and gave it a gentle pat. "You're arguing *down* your own budget. Do you know how many men would be arguing up?"
My brain had short-circuited. The most powerful woman I'd ever met was patting my head like I was a puppy who'd done a clever trick, and somewhere in the back of my mind, I was aware that I should probably be offended.
Instead, I was frozen.
"You're adorable," she repeated, withdrawing her hand—reluctantly, I thought? Wishful thinking?—and closing the folder. "The budget stays. Consider it a stress test for the accounting department."
"This isn't—" I swallowed. "Nobody just gives someone unlimited snacks and a head pat. There has to be a catch."
"John." Her voice gentled. "Did you mean what you said at the restaurant? That you don't want to work? That you don't want anything to do with money or management?"
"I—yes. I meant that." I fumbled for words. "But that was supposed to be a joke. An act. I was testing you."
"I know."
"You know?"
"From the moment you walked in. You were nervous. You stumbled mid-sentence twice. Your arrogant face was deeply unconvincing." She ticked off the points like a diagnosis. "Also, your hands were in your pockets the entire time, which is terrible for posture but excellent for hiding shaking fingers."
I stared at her. "You knew I was faking."
"And I watched you commit to the bit with absolute sincerity. It was the funniest thing I'd seen in months." She picked up her tea. "But the demands themselves—those were real, weren't they? Maybe exaggerated, but the core of them."
I thought about my death. The exhaustion. The way I'd worked until my body gave out. The desperate, shameful fantasy I'd nursed in the office bathroom: someone taking care of everything so I could just... stop.
"Yes," I said quietly. "The core was real."
"Then this contract isn't a cage." She said it with absolute certainty. "It's armour. I have a husband who will never try to steal my company, never demand a board seat, never use my resources to fund his own ambitions. Do you understand how rare that is? How *valuable*?"
I understood. Sitting there in the glass-walled conservatory, with morning light catching the gold in her hair and a contract worth millions sitting between us, I understood.
"I'm a shield," I said slowly. "For you. The no-job clause means no one can use me against your companies. The social events mean I'm visible enough to satisfy expectations but controlled enough to prevent... what happened with Damian."
Lilith's eyes glinted. "You're smarter than your snack demands suggest."
"That's not hard. My snack demands are very unintelligent."
She laughed—a real one, brief but warm. "The point stands. This works for both of us. You get to never work again. I get a husband who doesn't view me as a stepping stone." She paused. "And you get to eat truffle fries at three in the morning if that's what you want."
"You're going to spoil me."
"Yes." She said it like a promise. "I am."
The weird thing was, it didn't feel like a trap anymore. It felt like... an arrangement. A partnership. Two people who'd both been looking for something they couldn't find, accidentally stumbling into each other because of a wrong room number and a misread piece of paper.
I picked up my fork again. The pancakes were still warm. Maria's doing, probably—some kind of heating technology in the serving dishes.
"I have a condition," I said.
"You already have a contract."
"A verbal one. Non-legal."
Lilith raised an eyebrow. "Go on."
"If we're doing this—this marriage thing, this partnership—I want you to be honest with me. No hidden clauses. No surprises. If there's something I need to know, you tell me. I've had enough of people making decisions about my life without my input."
Something flickered across her face—surprise, maybe, or respect. "That's not a condition. That's basic decency."
"You'd be surprised how often basic decency gets skipped."
"I wouldn't." She extended her hand across the table. "I accept your non-legal condition. Honesty. No hidden clauses. I'll tell you things directly, and I expect the same from you."
I shook her hand. Her grip was warm and firm, and she didn't let go immediately.
"Good," she said. "Now eat your breakfast. Maria will be devastated if the eggs go cold."
I ate. The eggs were perfect—creamy, seasoned with something I couldn't identify, served on toast that crunched exactly right. Lilith returned to her tablet, but every so often her gaze flicked to me, and there was something in her expression I couldn't quite read.
Satisfaction, maybe. Or anticipation.
Or maybe just the quiet pleasure of someone who'd gambled on a stranger and was starting to think she'd won.
---
After breakfast, I wandered back to the East Wing and found my schedule had been updated. Tomorrow's entries now included a consultation with the relaxation specialist—I was going to have to actually process that concept at some point—and a note at the bottom in elegant handwriting:
*Snack budget effective immediately. Don't make me regret it. —L*
I laughed. I couldn't help it.
Then I called Maria and asked if she could make those truffle fries again. Just to test the system. Just to see if it was real.
Twenty minutes later, a tray arrived with truffle fries, three dipping sauces, and a small handwritten card that said *Welcome home, Master John*.
I stared at the card for a long time.
*Home*, I thought.
Maybe, impossibly, that's what this was becoming.














# Chapter 4: Public Debut Gala
The first social event arrived faster than I'd hoped and slower than I'd feared.
Two weeks into my new life, I had settled into a routine that was frankly embarrassing in its comfort. Wake up whenever. Breakfast prepared to my preferences. Mornings spent exploring the estate grounds—I'd found a hedge maze, three separate gazebos, and a greenhouse full of orchids that someone clearly doted on. Afternoons devoted to what Margaret called "personal enrichment" and what I called "napping in progressively more exotic locations." Evenings with Lilith, when her schedule permitted, which was about half the time.
She was busy. I'd known she was a CEO, but the reality of three companies meant she often worked fourteen-hour days. I saw her at breakfast sometimes, and at dinner occasionally, and once at two in the morning when I'd wandered to the kitMiller for a snack and found her there, still in her business clothes, drinking tea and staring at spreadsheets with the thousand-yard stare of someone who'd been fighting with quarterly projections for six hours.
I'd made her a snack. She'd looked at the plate of cheese and crackers like I'd handed her the moon.
"You didn't have to do this," she'd said.
"You're working too hard. Eat a cracker."
She'd eaten the cracker. She'd eaten four. Then she'd patted my head again—the head pats were becoming a thing, and I still didn't know how to react to them—and told me to go back to bed.
So our relationship was... something. Not quite friends yet. Definitely not lovers, the contract notwithstanding. Two people orbiting each other, learning the gravity of each other's presence.
And now we were going to attend a gala together.
---
The invitation arrived on heavy cream cardstock, embossed with gold lettering:
*The Van Derholt Foundation Cordially Invites*
*Lilith Vance and Spouse*
*To the Annual Benefactors' Gala*
*Formal Attire Required*
"Our first public appearance," Lilith said at breakfast, watching me read it. "Are you ready?"
"No," I said. "I'm not ready. I've never been to a gala. I don't know how to gala."
"It's not a verb."
"It should be. It's active. It's an action. One does not simply *attend* a gala. One galas."
Her lips twitched. "You'll be fine. Stay close to me, smile when people introduce themselves, and if anyone asks you uncomfortable questions, refer them to me."
"That's the whole strategy?"
"That's the whole strategy. Also, the food will be excellent. The Van Derholts use the same caterer I do."
I perked up. "Maria's people?"
"A sister company. Their miniature beef wellingtons are legendary."
"I'm suddenly more interested in this event."
"I thought you might be." She set down her teacup. "We'll arrive at seven. Margaret has arranged your wardrobe. Try not to look too surprised by anything you see—the press will be watching."
"Why would I be surprised?"
Lilith smiled, enigmatic and slightly dangerous. "It's your first time in high society as a kept man. You'll see."
---
She wasn't wrong.
The gala was held at some kind of converted palace—marble floors, chandeliers the size of small vehicles, a ballroom that could have hosted a professional sporting event. Women swirled in gowns that cost more than my mother's house. Men drifted through the crowd in clusters of two or three, wearing tailored suits and expressions that ranged from contented to predatory.
And Lilith.
Lilith stepped out of the car in a gown of deep emerald that made her eyes look like forest fire, and I temporarily forgot how breathing worked. Her hair was swept up in something elegant, showcasing the line of her throat, where a single emerald pendant rested. She took my arm, and the cameras flashed.
"Smile," she murmured.
I smiled. It felt like a grimace. The cameras didn't seem to care.
"Good," she said. "Now walk. Slow, measured. Like we have all the time in the world."
"What about the wellingtons?"
"They'll wait."
We made our way inside, and I was immediately struck by two things. First, the sheer number of people who wanted to talk to Lilith—businesswomen in sharp suits and sharper jewellery who materialised from every direction, offering congratulations and veiled business propositions and what I was pretty sure was a merger inquiry disguised as a compliment about her dress.
Second, the way they all looked at me.
Not *at* me, exactly. More like *through* me, assessing my value with the clinical efficiency of appraisers examining a disputed artwork. I was the new acquisition. The unknown variable. The man who'd somehow landed Lilith Vance without anyone in society ever having heard of him before.
"John, this is Helena Cross, CEO of Meridian Shipping," Lilith said, as another woman materialised.
"Charmed," Helena said, offering her hand. She didn't look charmed. She looked like she was trying to figure out what I was worth. "And where did you say your family was from, Mr. Vance?"
"Actually, it's Doe. I kept my name."
Surprise flickered across her face. "How... modern. And the Doe family is...?"
"Small," I said. "Very small. Microscopic, really."
Lilith's hand tightened on my arm—suppressing laughter, I thought—and smoothly redirected the conversation to shipping routes. I stood there, smiling vaguely, and tried to look like I belonged.
The wellingtons couldn't come fast enough.
---
I escaped to the hors d'oeuvres table the moment Lilith was pulled into what sounded like an intense discussion about trade tariffs.
The spread was, as promised, magnificent. Miniature beef wellingtons. Smoked salmon on toast points. Something wrapped in prosciutto that tasted like heaven's own charcuterie. I loaded a plate with the focused determination of a man who'd been promised good food and was finally reaping the reward.
"You're Lilith's new husband, aren't you?"
The voice came from beside me. I turned to find a young man about my age, blond and carefully tousled, wearing a suit that fit him like it had been painted on. He was holding a single glass of champagne and a single grape. He looked like he'd been holding that grape for hours.
"I am," I said. "John Doe."
"Sebastian Moore." He offered a hand that had never seen manual labour. "Husband of Victoria Moore. Three years now."
"Nice to meet you." I shook. His grip was soft. "How's the... husband life?"
"Tedious." He popped the grape into his mouth with the precision of someone who'd practiced. "But comfortable. Victoria's in textiles. I spend most of my time at the spa." He looked me up and down. "You're the one who made the Ashford boy lose his audience. Everyone's talking about it."
"Damian?"
"Dreadful man. Terrible manners. But his mother owns half the media outlets, so he's been... talking."
A chill went down my spine. "Talking?"
"Rumours. Whispers." Sebastian's expression was sympathetic in a detached, gossipy way. "That you're a commoner with no family connections. That Lilith was somehow tricked. That you're a gold-digger who showed up in her private room and refused to leave until she signed a contract."
"I didn't—she was the one who—" I stopped. Took a breath. "Those are not accurate rumours."
"I'm sure they're not. But Damian's been spreading them since the day you were... acquired." He said the word delicately, like it was a questionable vintage. "Watch yourself tonight. Some of the husbands here take pedigree very seriously."
He drifted away, leaving me with my plate of wellingtons and a growing unease.
So Damian was talking. Of course he was. A man like that, publicly humiliated, wasn't going to just vanish. He was going to try to make my life difficult.
But what could I actually do about it? Deny the rumours? I was a commoner. I did have no family connections. The only thing that was wrong was the idea that Lilith had been tricked—and honestly, sometimes I still wondered if it was the other way around.
I ate three wellingtons in rapid succession and felt marginally better.
---
The husbands' section of the ballroom was a distinct ecosystem.
I found it by accident while looking for a bathroom. A cluster of fifteen or twenty men, ranging from my age to late forties, all dressed immaculately, all wearing identical expressions of cultivated boredom. They occupied a corner of the room like it had been reserved for them, which it probably had. Couches. Champagne. A servant circulating with some kind of exotic canapés.
"You're new," one of them said as I passed. Older, silver at the temples, sharp-eyed. "Lilith's. The mysterious John Doe."
"That's me. Mysterious and John Doe-ish."
"Damian Ashford says you forced your way into a private meeting and strong-armed her into a marriage contract."
"Damian Ashford says a lot of things."
The man smiled, slow and not entirely friendly. "We look after our own here. If you've done something untoward..."
"I haven't." I held his gaze. "I showed up. She proposed the contract. I signed. That's the whole story."
"Someone like you? Landing someone like Lilith Vance?" He shook his head. "That doesn't happen."
"I'm aware. I was there. It was very confusing." I spotted an escape route—a servant with a fresh tray emerging from the kitMiller. "Now if you'll excuse me, I need to investigate those canapés."
I retreated before he could respond, feeling their eyes on my back. The husbands' ecosystem, I was learning, had its own hierarchy and politics. And I was the newest, lowest-ranking member, suspected of cheating my way in.
Perfect. Absolutely perfect.
---
I found a quiet alcove near the back of the ballroom, partially hidden by an enormous potted fern, and set up camp with my third plate of appetizers.
From here, I could observe without being observed—much. Lilith was across the room, deep in conversation with a grey-haired woman who was gesturing emphatically about something. A trade deal, maybe, or a hostile takeover. Lilith's expression was calm and focused, the same look she'd worn when she'd threatened to sue Damian.
She was good at this. The power thing. The navigating thing.
I was good at eating miniature quiches in a fern alcove.
Well. Everyone had their talents.
"Young man."
I nearly dropped my plate. An elderly woman had appeared beside me, leaning on a silver-topped cane. She was tiny, wrinkled, and dressed in a gown of midnight blue with a necklace that contained more sapphires than I'd ever seen in one place.
"Sorry," I said, straightening up. "I didn't see you."
"Obviously. You were very focused on that quiche." Her eyes sparkled. "You're the Vance husband."
"John Doe. Yes."
"I am Ambassador Zhou." She extended a hand, gnarled but steady. "I've been watching you all evening. You're the only person here who seems honestly interested in the food."
"I was promised excellent catering."
"And has it delivered?"
"The wellingtons alone were worth attending."
She laughed, a dry rustle of a sound. "Honest and hungry. A rare combination in this room. Tell me, Mr. Doe, what do you do when you're not hiding in fern alcoves?"
The question caught me off guard. In my old life, that question had always meant *what's your job*. Here, no one had asked me that. No one had needed to.
"I... don't do anything, actually. Professionally."
"Nothing at all?"
"I'm a professional relaxer. It's a demanding field. Very competitive."
Ambassador Zhou's eyes narrowed, and for a moment I thought I'd offended her. Then she smiled again, wider this time. "You quote Zhuangzi at a gala. Unexpected."
"I'm sorry, I quote who?"
"Zhuangzi. The Daoist philosopher. 'The man who does nothing is the most valuable of all.' Or in your phrasing, a professional relaxer." She tapped her cane on the floor. "I didn't expect to find a student of ancient Earth philosophy at a Van Derholt gala."
I blinked. Earth philosophy. Daoism. Zhuangzi. These were things from my world—things I'd read about in a half-remembered college elective, things I'd spouted without thinking because "professional relaxer" sounded like a good joke.
But in this world, apparently, they were still things. Ancient Earth things. And I'd just quoted one to a foreign dignitary.
"I'm not really a student," I said carefully. "More of a... casual enthusiast."
"A casual enthusiast who can quote Zhuangzi in casual conversation. What else do you know?"
What followed was one of the strangest conversations of either of my lives. Ambassador Zhou, it turned out, was a scholar of pre-modern Earth philosophy—a niche interest in this world, where apparently Earth was some kind of historical reference point that academics argued about but few people actually studied. She peppered me with questions. I answered as best I could, drawing on half-remembered lectures and the kind of general knowledge that came from growing up in the actual Earth.
The fact that this world shared some historical and cultural touchpoints with my old one was something I'd noticed but not fully explored. The languages were similar. Some classics existed in both places. But the scholarship here was fragmentary, incomplete. When I mentioned a detail about Laozi that I vaguely remembered from a YouTube video, Ambassador Zhou looked at me like I'd revealed the secrets of the universe.
"Remarkable," she breathed. "Absolutely remarkable. The Vance family has acquired an intellectual treasure."
"I really wouldn't go that—"
"I'll be speaking to Lilith about supporting a research fellowship. Your knowledge shouldn't go to waste."
"Please don't. I'm very busy with the relaxing."
But Ambassador Zhou was already hobbling away, her cane clicking with renewed purpose, and I was left holding an empty plate and the sinking feeling that I'd accidentally done something impressive again.
---
Lilith found me an hour later. I'd migrated from the fern alcove to a balcony, where I was getting some fresh air and trying to process the fact that a foreign ambassador now thought I was some kind of Earth philosophy savant.
"Ambassador Zhou just asked if I'd loan you to her university for a lecture series," she said, stepping out onto the balcony. The night air caught the loose strands of her hair, and the city lights spread out behind her like scattered jewels. "What did you say to her?"
"I quoted a philosopher without realising it was a philosopher. Then I apparently filled in some academic gaps she's been researching for twenty years."
"Accidentally."
"Very accidentally. I was trying to make a joke."
Lilith stared at me for a long moment. Then she leaned against the balcony railing and started laughing—not the composed, professional laugh she deployed in business settings, but something genuine and unguarded.
"You," she said, when she could breathe again, "are the most chaotic person I have ever met. You accidentally quote ancient philosophers at ambassadors. You accidentally land marriage contracts with billionaires. You accidentally impress everyone while actively trying to do nothing."
"It's a gift."
"It's something." She caught her breath, still smiling. "The gala's almost over. You survived."
"I survived. Your business associates are terrifying."
"They're vultures. But they're useful vultures." She studied me. "I heard Damian's been spreading rumours."
"You heard that?"
"I hear everything. It's my job." Her expression turned serious. "I'll handle him."
"How?"
"Legally, preferably. But I'm flexible." She said it with the calm of someone who had absolutely ruined people before and would do it again if necessary. "Did anyone give you trouble?"
"The husbands' corner. They think I cheated my way into this."
"Didn't you?"
"Accidentally. Through the wrong door."
She smiled at that, soft around the edges. "Then let them think what they want. We know the truth. And tomorrow, after Ambassador Zhou's endorsement circulates, they'll also know you're not just some random commoner."
"What am I, then?"
"A puzzle." She reached up and, unexpectedly, smoothed a wrinkle on my collar. "A frustrating, fascinating puzzle that I'm increasingly certain I was right to acquire."
The touch was brief, but it left a warmth that the night air couldn't dispel.
---
The car ride home was quiet. I was tired in a way that had nothing to do with physical exertion—the exhaustion of performing, of being watched, of navigating a social landscape I didn't fully understand.
Lilith sat across from me, scrolling through her tablet, and I found myself watching the streetlights slide across her face.
"Can I ask you something?" I said.
"Of course."
"The paper my mother gave me. For the blind date. It had the wrong room number on it."
Lilith looked up. "What do you mean?"
"I mean—" I paused, the pieces clicking together for the first time since that day. "She wrote '606.' But I read it upside down or something, I thought it said '909.' Your room."
"909 was my room. I was supposed to be meeting Damian."
"Right. And I was supposed to be meeting someone in 606. Some girl my mother set me up with." I rubbed my forehead, a new dread creeping in. "Someone who was waiting in a private room that whole time. Someone I never showed up for."
Lilith was very still. "You left a blind date waiting."
"I didn't mean to. I genuinely thought 909 was the right room. I kicked in your door instead."
For a long moment, she said nothing. Then, quietly: "Do you know who it was? The girl in 606?"
"No. Mother just said 'the Ashford girl.' I assumed it was some textile family connection. I didn't think—" I stopped, because Lilith's expression had shifted into something complicated. "What?"
"Textiles. Ashford. That would be Mira Ashford. Damian's cousin."
My stomach dropped. "Damian's cousin."
"The Ashford family has a textile branch and a finance branch. They don't get along. Mira's from the textile side—she's supposed to be sweet, actually. Runs a bakery now, I think."
"A bakery. I stood up a baker. A sweet baker named Mira. Because I couldn't read my own mother's handwriting."
The car fell silent. I thought about a girl in room 606, sitting alone at a table for two, waiting for a blind date who never came. Wondering what she'd done wrong. Maybe thinking it was her fault.
"The Ashford family," Lilith said slowly, "probably assumed you stood her up deliberately. Another slight against them, on top of what happened with Damian."
"I didn't even know there was a Damian until he burst into your room."
"But they don't know that. And Mira..." Lilith's brow furrowed. "She's one of the few Ashfords with a decent reputation. If she was hurt by this..."
I leaned back in my seat, staring at the car's ceiling.
"I have to fix it," I said. "Somehow. I have to find her and explain."
Lilith was quiet for a moment. Then she reached over and took my hand—a brief, warm pressure.
"We'll figure it out," she said. "Together. That's what the contract's for, remember?"
I remembered. The shield. The partnership.
And somewhere across the city, I imagined a girl closing up her bakery for the night, not knowing that the stranger who'd stood her up was about to crash back into her life with an apology and a very, very strange story.

















# Chapter 5: The Girl in Room 606
Three days after the gala, my face appeared in the society pages.
I'd been blissfully unaware of this until Margaret materialised at my elbow during my mid-morning cloud-watching session—item thirty-one on the relaxation options list, and one I'd become particularly devoted to—and placed a tablet in my hands with the quiet gravity of someone delivering a classified document.
"You're in the papers, Master John."
I squinted at the screen. There I was, frozen mid-bite of a miniature wellington, looking like a squirrel that had been caught raiding a bird feeder. Beside me, Lilith was radiant and composed, the emerald gown catching the light. The contrast was almost comical.
The headline read: *Vance Heiress Debuts Mysterious New Husband at Van Derholt Gala.*
Below that, a smaller line: *Ambassador Zhou Calls Young Mr. Doe 'An Intellectual Treasure.'*
"That's... a photo," I said.
"There are three more inside. The society bloggers are very taken with you."
"Why? I spent most of the night hiding in a fern alcove."
"Apparently, that's 'refreshingly authentic.'" Margaret's expression suggested she found this as baffling as I did. "You've been trending on several lifestyle feeds. 'The Reluctant Trophy Husband,' they're calling you."
I groaned and handed back the tablet. "Is this going to be a problem?"
"Unclear. But I'd expect the public to take an interest for at least a news cycle or two."
She vanished, leaving me to contemplate the horrifying reality that I had somehow become internet-famous for eating hors d'oeuvres and quoting a philosopher by accident. This world was strange. I was strange. Everything was strange.
I resumed cloud-watching with slightly less tranquillity than before.
---
The letter arrived the next morning.
It wasn't delivered through normal channels. No postmark, no courier stamp. One of the groundskeepers found it tucked into the gatehouse's outer door—an envelope of pale cream paper, addressed simply: *John Doe, c/o Vance Estate.*
Margaret brought it to me at breakfast, her demeanour radiating professional suspicion. "This was not delivered through any approved channel, sir. Shall I have security screen it?"
I turned the envelope over in my hands. It was light. Hand-addressed in neat, rounded handwriting. It smelled faintly of vanilla.
"No," I said, surprising myself. "I think... I think I know who it might be from."
I opened it carefully. Inside was a single sheet of paper and a business card.
*Dear Mr. Doe,*
*You don't know me, but I was supposed to meet you three weeks ago at the Celestial Garden Restaurant. Room 606. I waited for an hour, but you never came. I assumed you'd changed your mind, and I tried not to take it personally—blind dates are awkward for everyone, and maybe your mother hadn't told you much about me.*
*But then I saw your picture in the paper, and I recognised your name from the reservation. It seems you found someone else that day. I'm happy for you, truly. Lady Lilith is a remarkable woman.*
*I'm writing this because... well, I suppose I want to know what happened. Was it something I did? Something my family said? I'd rather know than keep wondering. If you're willing to talk, even just for a moment, I'd be grateful.*
*My bakery is called Flour & Sugar. I'm there most days. No pressure. No expectations. Just—closure, I suppose.*
*Wishing you well,*
*Mira Ashford*
I read the letter twice. Then a third time.
Each reading made it worse.
The careful, measured words. The complete absence of accusation. The way she apologised for *herself*, as if she might have been the problem. And at the bottom, a small hand-drawn doodle of a cupcake with a smiling face.
This girl had sat alone in room 606 for an hour. Had seen my photo in the papers and realised I'd married someone else on the same day. Had written me a letter so gentle it practically apologised for existing.
And I had done this to her.
"John?" Lilith's voice came from the doorway. She was dressed for work, tablet in hand, already half-distracted by the day ahead. Then she saw my face. "What's wrong?"
I held out the letter without speaking.
She read it. Her expression shifted through several stages I couldn't quite track.
"Mira Ashford," she said quietly.
"She thinks it was her fault."
"Of course she does. That's how Mira works." Lilith handed the letter back. "I told you—she's the only Ashford with a decent reputation. This is why."
"I have to go see her."
"Yes." Lilith paused. "Do you want me to come with you?"
The offer caught me off guard. Lilith had three companies to run. A schedule that was probably packed with meetings and decisions and whatever CEOs did all day. And she was offering to drop it all to accompany me on an apology mission.
"I think," I said slowly, "this is something I need to do myself."
She nodded, something flickering in her expression—approval, maybe, or a testing of her own instincts. "Then go. Take the smaller car—it draws less attention. And John?"
"Yeah?"
"Tell her the truth. All of it. She deserves that much."
---
Flour & Sugar was tucked into a corner of the city I'd never visited before—a neighbourhood of small shops and cobbled streets, where the buildings were old but well-kept, and every storefront seemed to have its own personality.
The bakery itself was charming in a way that felt genuine rather than curated. Window boxes full of herbs. A hand-painted sign. The smell of fresh bread drifting out every time the door opened. Through the glass, I could see a small interior with mismatched tables and a display case full of pastries.
I stood outside for approximately forty-five seconds, thoroughly failing to compose myself.
*Hi, I'm the guy who stood you up because I can't read my mother's handwriting and then accidentally married a billionaire. Also, I'm from another world. Also, I'm very sorry. Also—*
This was going to go terribly.
I pushed open the door. A bell chimed.
The woman behind the counter looked up, and our eyes met.
Mira Ashford was not what I'd expected. Given her family—Damian's sneering face came to mind—I'd braced for someone sharp-edged, polished, maybe cold. Instead, the girl before me was soft in every sense. Round cheeks dusted with flour. Warm brown eyes. Hair pulled back in a practical ponytail, with wisps escaping around her temples. She wore a simple apron over a comfortable sweater, and her hands were busy arranging a tray of croissants.
She froze when she saw me.
"Oh," she said. "You came."
"I'm John." The words tumbled out, ungraceful. "John Doe. I'm—I'm the one who—you wrote me a letter. I got your letter."
"I know. I wrote it." A flush crept up her cheeks. "I wasn't sure you'd actually... I mean, I didn't know if you'd want to..."
"I owed you an explanation." I hovered awkwardly by the counter. "Actually, I owe you a lot more than that. An apology, to start with. A big one. The biggest."
Mira set down the croissant she'd been holding. Her expression was guarded, but not hostile. Curious, maybe. And something else—relief? That I'd shown up at all?
"Would you like some tea?" she asked. "I find that big apologies go better with tea."
"That would be... yes. Thank you."
She gestured to one of the small tables, then disappeared into the back. I sat. The chair was wooden and slightly rickety, and somehow that made me feel more grounded. This was a real place. A real person. Not another society mask.
Mira returned with two cups of something fragrant and golden, and a plate of small cookies shaped like flowers.
"Lavender shortbread," she said, settling into the chair across from me. "I was testing a new recipe. Tell me if they're too sweet."
I took a cookie. It melted on my tongue—buttery, floral, perfect. "These are incredible."
"Thank you." She wrapped her hands around her teacup. "So. The apology."
Right. The apology.
I took a deep breath. "I went to the wrong room."
Mira blinked. "What?"
"The Celestial Garden. My mother wrote '606' on a piece of paper. I read it upside down or sideways or something—her handwriting is genuinely terrible—and I thought it said '909.' So I went to room 909." I paused. "Where Lilith was waiting for someone else. And I kicked in the door and started making outrageous demands because I thought it would be funny to pretend to be an arrogant jerk as a test."
"You... kicked in the door?"
"It was a whole performance. I was trying to be insufferable. I demanded a million dollars and a car and a house and to never work again." The memory was surreal even now. "And then Lilith said 'OK' and called in a lawyer and made me sign a marriage contract."
Mira stared at me.
"Then her actual date showed up," I continued, "and he was furious, and she threw him out, and I was standing there holding a pen and a legally binding document, and I'd completely forgotten that somewhere on the sixth floor, there was a girl in room 606 wondering where her blind date was."
Silence.
Then, unexpectedly, Mira laughed.
It wasn't a bitter laugh or a cruel one. It was surprised and genuine and slightly incredulous, like she couldn't quite believe what she was hearing.
"You're telling me," she said, "that all of this—the marriage, the gala, the society pages, Damian's tantrum—all of it happened because you read a room number wrong?"
"Yes."
"And because your mother has terrible handwriting."
"The worst. It's like she writes with her feet."
Mira covered her mouth with her hand, her shoulders shaking. "I thought... I spent weeks thinking I'd done something wrong. That maybe my family's reputation had scared you off, or you'd heard something about me, or..."
"No. God, no. It was just me being spectacularly incompetent."
"You stood me up by *accident*."
"A complete accident. I didn't even know you existed until after the gala. And then Lilith told me about the Ashford family connection, and I realised—" I stopped, the guilt washing back in. "I realised I'd hurt someone without even knowing it. And that's not... I don't want to be the kind of person who hurts people through carelessness."
Mira's laughter faded into something softer. She studied me across the table, her brown eyes thoughtful.
"You came here," she said. "You read my letter and you came. Most people wouldn't have."
"Most people aren't responsible for leaving you alone in a restaurant for an hour."
"It was actually an hour and fifteen minutes. I counted."
I winced. "I'm so sorry."
"I know." She picked up a lavender cookie and examined it. "I believe you. Which is strange, because the story you just told is completely absurd."
"It really is."
"But somehow it makes sense." She bit into the cookie, chewed thoughtfully. "You don't seem like the kind of person who'd deliberately stand someone up. You seem like the kind of person who falls into things backwards and then tries very hard to make them right."
I wasn't sure if that was a compliment or an observation, but I'd take it. "Can I... make this right? Somehow? I know I can't undo the blind date, but—"
"You could help me eat these cookies. I made too many." She pushed the plate toward me. "And you could tell me the rest of the story, because I'm dying to know what happened after you kicked in the door."
So I told her. The whole ridiculous chain of events—the demands, the contract, Damian's entrance, Lilith's threats, the car arriving at my mother's house. Mira listened with the expression of someone watching a particularly entertaining drama, gasping at the right moments and laughing at the rest.
By the time I finished, the plate of cookies was empty and my tea had gone cold.
"Your life is insane," Mira said.
"I'm aware."
"Lilith Vance. You accidentally married Lilith Vance." She shook her head. "You know, Damian was supposed to meet her that day. He spent weeks preparing. New suit, new cufflinks, some speech he'd rehearsed about his 'value as a high-status male.' When he came back from that meeting, he was white as a sheet and wouldn't talk to anyone."
"I was there for that part."
"You were the reason." A smile tugged at her mouth. "You know what? I'm not even upset anymore. Anyone who can make Damian Ashford lose his composure in public has my gratitude."
"Damian's your cousin."
"Cousin. Not friend." Her voice was matter-of-fact. "The finance Ashfords and the textile Ashfords don't get along. They think we're beneath them. Damian especially."
"Is that why you run a bakery instead of... whatever Ashfords usually do?"
"That's part of it." She glanced around the shop with obvious affection. "This place was my grandmother's. She left it to me when she passed. My family wanted me to sell it and 'invest properly,' but I couldn't. Baking makes me happy."
I thought about that. A girl who'd chosen happiness over family expectations. Who ran a small bakery in a quiet neighbourhood and wrote letters to the strangers who'd wronged her, asking politely for closure.
"You're a good person, Mira."
She flushed. "I'm just... me."
"That's what I said." I leaned back in my chair. "The good ones always say that."
---
I ended up staying for another hour.
Somewhere between the second pot of tea and Mira showing me how she made her signature sourdough, I had an idea. Actually, I had several ideas—fragments from my old life, things I'd seen in coffee shops and bakeries in a world that no longer existed.
"You should do loyalty cards," I said, watching her knead dough with practised efficiency.
"Loyalty cards?"
"Punch cards. Buy ten pastries, get one free. It keeps customers coming back."
Mira paused mid-knead. "That's... actually clever."
"And you could name things more creatively. Like, instead of 'chocolate croissant,' call it 'Chaos Croissant' or something. Something people remember."
"'Chaos Croissant'?"
"It worked in—" I almost said *in my world*, caught myself, "—in a bakery I used to go to. They had a whole menu of ridiculous names. People loved it. They'd bring friends just to show them the menu."
Mira's eyes lit up. "I could do pun names. Baking puns."
"Exactly."
"'Pain au Chocolat' becomes 'Pain au Choc-o-lot-of-Love.'"
"Perfect. Terrible. People will talk about it."
She grabbed a notebook from beside the register and started scribbling. I fed her more ideas—seasonal specials, social media posts (adapted for whatever this world's equivalent was), a "mystery box" of day-old pastries at a discount. Things I'd absorbed through cultural osmosis in a life where bakeries were a dime a dozen.
"This is amazing," she said, pages filling with her neat handwriting. "Where did you learn all this?"
"A previous life," I said, which was technically true.
She looked up, caught my expression, and laughed. "You're strange, John Doe."
"Thank you."
"It's a compliment."
---
I arrived back at the estate to find Lilith in her study, surrounded by reports and projections. She looked up when I entered, her expression carefully neutral.
"How did it go?"
"Better than I expected." I dropped into the chair across from her desk. "She's... really nice, actually. Sweet. Runs a bakery. We talked for hours."
"I know."
"You know?"
"Margaret keeps me informed." Lilith set down her pen. "I also know you spent two hours in that bakery and came out looking like you'd made a new friend."
There was something in her tone—not accusation, exactly, but something adjacent to it. A guardedness.
"Are you... worried?" I asked.
"Should I be?"
"No." I said it firmly. "Mira's not—that's not what it was. She just wanted to know why she got stood up. And once she heard the story, she was fine. Better than fine. She's one of the least threatening people I've ever met."
Lilith was quiet for a moment. Then she stood and walked to the window, looking out at the gardens.
"I'm not used to sharing," she said, her voice quieter than usual. "Things that are mine."
"Is that what I am? A thing?"
"A person." She turned to face me. "A person I acquired through an accident and a legally binding contract. A person who, I'm realising, has his own life and his own connections separate from me." She paused. "I don't know how to be jealous. I've never had anything worth being jealous over."
I stood up, crossing to the window. "Lilith. You're a terrifying CEO who could buy and sell half the people at that gala. You threatened to audit Damian's family for *entertainment*. You put 'SLEEP' on my calendar as an official activity. I'm not going to run off with a baker just because she's nice."
"Then why did you stay there so long?"
"Because I felt guilty. Because she deserved better than what I did to her. And because—" I hesitated, then pushed forward, "—because she reminded me a little of myself. Someone who's just trying to do what makes her happy, even when the world expects something different."
Lilith studied me. Her green eyes were unreadable in the fading light.
"I don't want to be possessive," she said finally. "I've seen what that does to marriages in our circles. Husbands treated like property. Wives who monitor every interaction. It's toxic."
"But?"
"But I've never had anyone who was... mine. Not in a way that mattered. And I don't quite know what to do with the feeling."
I reached out—awkwardly, uncertain—and took her hand. She let me.
"Maybe start with trusting me," I said. "I signed the contract. I'm not going anywhere. But I'm also not going to be the kind of husband who sits in a gilded cage and never talks to anyone else. That's not healthy for either of us."
"You're right." She exhaled, some tension leaving her shoulders. "You're right, and I hate that you're right."
"Terrible feeling, isn't it?"
"Awful." She squeezed my hand once, then let go. "Mira Ashford. The baker. Is she as genuinely nice as everyone says?"
"Nicer. She sends cookies home with her customers."
Lilith considered this. "Then maybe... I should meet her properly. If she's going to be your friend, she should at least know I'm not the monster Damian probably told her I am."
"I think she already figured that out. She seems pretty happy for us."
"Us." Lilith tested the word. "I like that."
"Me too," I said, and found that I meant it.
---
Later that night, I found a note on my pillow in elegant handwriting:
*Thank you for being honest with me. I'm not good at this—feelings, trust, sharing. But I'm trying. And I'm glad you stayed.*
*—L*
*P.S. I've doubled the snack budget again. Don't argue.*
I laughed, folded the note carefully, and placed it in the drawer of my bedside table.
Maybe this marriage had started as an accident. Maybe we were both still figuring out what it meant.
But for the first time, I thought we might actually figure it out together.















# Chapter 6: The Misleading Secret
One month into my marriage, I had achieved perfect laziness.
This was not a small accomplishment. True laziness, I was discovering, required discipline. Anyone could be idle for a day or two—sleep in, skip chores, binge whatever passed for television in this world. But sustained, professional-grade indolence? That demanded structure. Routine. The kind of dedication that turned relaxation from a hobby into an art form.
I had my schedule memorised. Wake naturally (no alarms, a clause I'd negotiated with Margaret after a traumatic encounter with a chiming clock). Breakfast with Lilith when her calendar permitted. Morning walk through the gardens, stopping at the koi pond to watch the fish do their lazy circles. Midday snack consultation with Maria. Afternoon nap in the hammock that had been installed in the sunroom at my request. Evening cloud-watching. Late-night snack, because why not.
It was, by any measure, paradise.
I was losing my mind.
"You're fidgeting," Lilith observed at breakfast, approximately five weeks in. She was reviewing quarterly reports between bites of toast, her tablet propped against the fruit bowl. "You never fidget."
"I'm not fidgeting."
"Your leg has been bouncing for ten minutes. I timed it."
I looked down. My leg was, indeed, bouncing like a jackhammer. I made it stop through force of will. "That's just... excess energy."
"Excess energy." She set down her tablet. "John, you nap four hours a day. You have cloud-watching scheduled as a formal activity. What excess energy?"
"I don't know." I pushed my eggs around my plate. "I'm just... restless, I guess."
"Restless." She said the word like she was testing a foreign language. "Do you need more activities? I can have Margaret add something to the schedule. Water aerobics? Falconry? The estate used to have a falconer, I'm sure we could—"
"I don't need falconry."
"Competitive napping? There must be leagues for that."
"I'm being serious."
"So am I." But her expression softened. "John, if something's wrong, tell me."
How could I explain it? That I'd spent my entire previous life working myself to death, dreaming of exactly this freedom—and now that I had it, some traitorous part of my brain was looking for problems to solve? That the absence of stress had left a vacuum that relaxation couldn't fill?
"Nothing's wrong," I said. "I just need a hobby. Something to do with my hands."
Lilith considered this. "The estate has a woodworking shop. A pottery studio. A music room with seventeen instruments you've never touched."
"I'll figure something out."
"See that you do. Your leg is bouncing again."
---
The discovery happened three days later, in the manor's library.
I'd wandered in looking for something to read—a novel, maybe, or a history of this world that might help me understand it better. The library was two storeys tall, with rolling ladders and leather armchairs and the particular quiet that only accumulates in rooms full of old books.
I wasn't reading.
I was staring at a maritime magazine I'd found on a side table. Someone on staff must have left it there. The cover showed a sleek vessel cutting through blue water, all gleaming hull and modern lines, the kind of boat that screamed wealth and speed and absolutely nothing about actual sailing.
I flipped through it. Page after page of motor yachts. Power cruisers. Hydrofoils. Luxury catamarans with engines the size of small apartments. All of it designed for people who wanted to go fast while burning fuel and making noise.
No sailing yachts. None.
I went deeper into the library's collection. Maritime history. Naval architecture. Shipbuilding traditions from a dozen different cultures. The books were dusty and clearly underused, but they were there—and they told a story I hadn't expected.
This world had boats. It had ships. It had maritime trade routes and naval warfare and fishing fleets that had operated for centuries. What it didn't have was recreational sailing. The concept of a yacht powered purely by wind, designed for pleasure rather than transport—it had never developed here. Something about the technological path this world had taken, some divergence in priorities or materials or cultural preferences, had left a sailboat-shaped hole in their collective imagination.
I sat in the leather armchair with books piled around me, my heart beating faster.
In my previous life, I had been obsessed with sailboat design.
It was the one thing that had kept me sane through years of spreadsheet hell—the dream of one day building my own boat, of understanding the marriage between wind and water and hull that had fascinated humans for millennia. I'd read everything I could find. I'd sketched designs during lunch breaks. I'd taught myself naval architecture from online courses and library books and forums where obsessive hobbyists argued about keel ratios and sail plans.
I'd never built anything. Never had the money, never had the time. Just the dreams, folded away in notebooks that probably got thrown out when I died.
But now...
Now I had time. I had resources. I had a wife who'd quadrupled my snack budget without blinking.
I closed the book in my lap with a thump that echoed through the quiet library.
I was going to design a sailboat.
---
The guest suite in the east wing became my studio before I'd consciously decided to claim it.
It was the perfect space—unused, out of the way, with good northern light and enough floor space to spread out large sheets of paper. I commandeered it without asking permission, which was technically within my rights as husband of the estate but still felt vaguely illicit.
The first day, I just sketched. Nothing serious. Doodles of hull shapes, sail configurations, the elegant curve of a bowsprit. Muscle memory from a lifetime ago, reawakening slowly. My lines were shaky at first, the proportions slightly off, but as the hours passed, something clicked into place.
By the third day, I'd requisitioned proper drafting supplies. Paper. Pencils. A T-square, a set of curves. A drafting table that I'd found in a storage room and dragged across the wing myself, nearly throwing out my back in the process.
By the fifth day, I was lost.
I'd start sketching after breakfast and look up to find it was dark outside. Maria's carefully prepared lunches sat uneaten by the door. Notes from Margaret went unread. My schedule—that beautiful, structured monument to indolence—fell completely apart.
And I was happy. Actually, genuinely happy, in a way that the endless napping and cloud-watching had never managed. The problem-solving part of my brain, starved for weeks, was feasting.
But I was also secretive.
I didn't mean to be. At first, I just didn't mention it. A hobby, I told myself. Nothing worth announcing. Then the sketches started getting good—really good—and something in me recoiled from showing them. What if Lilith thought they were stupid? What if she didn't understand? What if she offered to help, and suddenly this private thing became another obligation, another expectation, another thing I had to perform?
So I locked the door.
---
"Master John has been spending a great deal of time in the east guest suite," Margaret reported, her tone carefully neutral. "He's requested that it not be disturbed."
Lilith looked up from her desk. "Disturbed?"
"The door is locked, Madam. He's been taking his meals there. When staff approach, he becomes... flustered."
"Flustered how?"
"Yesterday, one of the housemaids asked if he needed fresh linens. He said 'no thank you, everything's fine, please don't come in,' and then dropped a pencil through the gap under the door. He asked her to pass it back without opening it."
Lilith set down her pen. "He passed a pencil under the door."
"Yes, Madam."
"For a week now?"
"Nine days."
Nine days. Lilith had noticed, of course, that John had been absent from their usual routines—fewer breakfasts together, no appearances in the gardens, the hammock conspicuously empty. She'd assumed he'd found some new entertainment. The estate was vast; it was easy for two people to go hours without crossing paths.
But locked doors. Nervous deflection. Requests for privacy that bordered on paranoia.
In her experience—and her experience was extensive—men only hid things for one reason.
"Pull the security logs for that wing," she said, her voice very calm. "I want to know everyone who's entered or exited in the past two weeks. Any deliveries. Any visitors." She paused. "Any communications."
Margaret hesitated. "Madam, if I may—"
"You may not."
"Understood, Madam."
Margaret withdrew, and Lilith sat alone in her study, staring at nothing.
She'd known this might happen. Had braced for it, in some quiet corner of her mind. Men got bored. Men got restless. Men who were kept—no matter how comfortably—eventually looked for something more. A mistress. An affair. A secret that wasn't so secret.
But John? Her strange, sweet, accidentally-acquired husband, who'd argued down his own snack budget and quoted philosophers at ambassadors and befriended the girl he'd accidentally stood up? John, who looked at her sometimes with such unguarded warmth that she forgot how to be calculating?

But right now, in this moment, I had everything I'd ever wanted. A purpose that didn't feel like work. A wife who looked at my obsessive sketches and saw a future instead of a mess.
And a thirty-five-metre sailing yacht waiting to be born, with my terrible handwriting all over its soul.

I looked up. Lilith stood in the doorway, still in her business clothes—a charcoal suit that probably cost more than my first car—but her jacket was unbuttoned and her expression was softer than it had been this morning.
"I was told you fled the studio," she said, walking toward me. "Elena said, and I quote, 'the Chief Designer has retreated to resupply.' She seemed pleased."
"She called me Chief Designer."
"I heard."
"Multiple times. In front of other people."
"I heard that too." Lilith reached the counter and stood across from me. "They also called your keel design 'revolutionary' and your rigging configuration 'unprecedented.' I have Margaret monitoring the studio audio feed."
"You're monitoring—why am I surprised? Of course you're monitoring."
"For security purposes."
"For control purposes."
"For both purposes." She didn't deny it. "John, do you understand what happened in that room today?"
"I presented my boat to actual engineers and they didn't laugh at me?"
"You presented a completely original naval architecture to the foremost experts in the field, and within four hours, they had reorganised themselves around your vision. Elena Vasquez, who has turned down offers from every major shipyard on the continent, just messaged me to say she's 'profoundly glad' she accepted this contract. Saskia Voss, who once told a government minister his yacht design was 'a floating insult to hydrodynamics,' spent twenty minutes praising your sail plan."
"It's a good sail plan."
"It's a brilliant sail plan. And it's yours." Lilith reached across the counter and took my hand. "You've been terrified all week that you were an amateur playing at design. You're not. You're a designer who happens to lack formal credentials. The credentials are just paperwork. The talent—" She squeezed my fingers. "—the talent is real."
I stared at our joined hands. Her nails were perfect, as always. My fingers were still smudged with marker ink.
"How did you find them?" I asked. "Elena. The team. You said you assembled them quietly."
"I have a very efficient recruitment team. I also have access to every major engineering publication and a database of dissatisfied talent. Elena was working for a cargo shipping company that had rejected her canting keel research three times. Priya was at a composites firm that refused to let her experiment with new laminate schedules. Saskia had been sidelined at a yacht builder that only wanted motor vessels. Yuna was teaching introductory fluid dynamics at a university that didn't appreciate her research." Lilith's smile turned sharp. "I offered them the chance to build something that's never been built before, with an unlimited materials budget and a designer who actually understands their work. They accepted within twenty-four hours."
"You poached the best engineers in the world."
"I recruited the best engineers in the world for my husband's boat."
"Is there?"
"Legally, yes. I checked."
I laughed, sudden and surprised. "You're impossible."
"I'm thorough." She released my hand, but only to move around the counter and sit on the stool beside me. "Now. What's the existential crisis about?"
"I don't know. I just... this morning I was a guy who drew boats in a locked room. Now I'm Chief Designer of a project with a team and a physics engine and a composites specialist who looked at my material specs like they were Christmas morning." I ran a hand through my hair. "I'm supposed to be retired. I'm supposed to be relaxing. That was the whole point of the contract."
"And are you not relaxed?"
"I'm... I don't know what I am. Excited, I think. Terrified. Happy. All at once."
Lilith considered this. "When you were in that room, explaining the keel design, were you thinking about deadlines? Budgets? Performance reviews?"
"No. I was thinking about boats."
"Then it's not work." She said it with the absolute certainty of someone who had spent her life distinguishing between obligation and passion. "Work is what you do when you'd rather be doing something else. You designed a revolutionary sailing yacht while locked in a guest room with no external pressure. That's not work. That's a calling."
"A calling I didn't know I had."
"Those are usually the best kind." She stood, brushing imaginary dust from her skirt. "I should let you get back. Elena mentioned something about a spar configuration review after lunch."
"Stay for a minute?"
The words came out before I could stop them. Lilith paused, then settled back onto the stool.
"Of course," she said. "What do you need?"
I didn't know what I needed. Reassurance, maybe. Or just presence—the steady, certain presence of the woman who had taken my absurd demands and turned them into a contract, my chaotic sketches and turned them into a studio, my private obsession and turned it into a team.
"I just wanted to say thank you," I said. "For all of this. The studio. The team. Believing in the boat before anyone else did."
"You showed me the blueprints, John. I believed in the boat because you believed in the boat. I just... facilitated."
"You facilitated the best naval engineering team on the continent."
"On the continent, yes." She smiled, small and private. "The best in the world are still employed by the government. They have security clearances that make poaching difficult."
"There's a joke in there about me being the second-best option."
"There's no joke. You're exactly who I want." She said it simply, without fanfare, and the words settled somewhere in my chest like a stone dropped into still water. "Now. Finish your truffle fries. Your team is waiting."
My team. I had a team.
And apparently, I was their Chief Designer. A title I had no credentials for, no training for, and—I was starting to realise—every qualification that actually mattered.
I finished the truffle fries and went back to the studio.





















# Chapter 8: The Physics Problem
The problem announced itself on a Tuesday.
I arrived at the studio at my usual time—which is to say, late morning, after the sleep schedule had been satisfied and Maria's breakfast had been properly appreciated—to find the engineering team clustered around Yuna's laptop like mourners at a wake. The mood in the room was distinctly funereal.
"Good morning," I said, stopping in the doorway. "Why does everyone look like someone cancelled sailing forever?"
Elena looked up. Her expression, usually so controlled, was strained at the edges. "We have a problem."
"I gathered. Is it a snack problem? Because I can solve snack problems."
"It's a computational problem." Yuna turned her laptop to face me. On the screen, a simulation was running—or trying to. A simplified hull model was being fed water conditions, and the resulting animation looked like a boat having a seizure. Numbers scrolled on a sidebar, most of them red. "Every existing simulation platform we've tried fails when we input your design parameters."
"Sailing yacht hydrodynamics," Saskia added, her scarred hands wrapped around a coffee mug, "aren't like motor yacht hydrodynamics. A motor yacht pushes through water. The forces are predictable. Linear. A sailing yacht harnesses wind and water simultaneously—the hull isn't just being pushed, it's being leveraged against by the rig. The physics couples in ways that commercial software doesn't model."
"A canting keel makes it worse," Elena said. "The keel isn't static. It moves. The righting moment changes continuously. Standard computational fluid dynamics packages assume fixed geometries. When we try to model a moving appendage that's simultaneously generating lift and redirecting the centre of mass, the equations don't converge. The software literally gives up."
I walked to the laptop and watched the simulation sputter. Error messages bloomed like ugly flowers.
"Okay," I said. "So we need different software."
"There isn't different software." Yuna's voice was flat. "I've checked. I've contacted every computational hydrodynamics lab on the continent. The military has custom tools for submarine modelling, but they're classified and wouldn't help us anyway—submarines don't have sails. The commercial yacht industry has entirely standardised around motor vessel physics. No one has ever needed to simulate a thirty-five-metre sailing yacht with a canting keel and a tandem foil configuration because no one has ever built one."
"We're in uncharted territory," Priya said. She didn't sound upset about it. She sounded like someone acknowledging interesting weather. "Without proper simulations, we can't verify the hull design. Without hull verification, we can't lay up the first prototype. Without a prototype—"
"We can't build the boat," I finished.
Silence settled over the studio.
I stared at the failing simulation. The hull model juddered and froze. Error dialogue: *Solution diverged at timestep 1,847. Instability in coupled aero-hydrodynamic solver.*
In my previous life, I'd spent years building financial models. But at night, I'd haunted different corners of the internet. Open-source fluid dynamics projects. Finite element analysis forums. Papers about wake turbulence and boundary layer transition. I'd been fascinated—obsessed—with the idea that you could build a mathematical model of reality itself.
I'd never built one. But I'd read the source code of people who had.
"What about a custom engine?" I said.
Yuna looked up. "I mentioned that last week as a possibility. But building a physics engine from scratch takes years. Teams. Funding."
"We have funding." I gestured at the studio around us. "We have a team. And we don't need years. We need one specific engine for one specific set of problems."
"It's not that simple—"
"I know it's not simple." My voice was sharper than I intended. I took a breath. "Sorry. I just... I've been working on something. Since before you all arrived. When I was doing the initial calculations by hand, I kept hitting walls because I couldn't model the dynamic interactions. So I started sketching what a solver would need to look like."
I crossed to my drafting cabinet and pulled out the folder—"COMPUTATIONAL STUFF (INCOMPLETE)"—and handed it to Yuna. She opened it. Her expression shifted as she turned the pages.
"This isn't half an architecture," she said after a long moment. "This is a fully specified computational framework. You've defined the discretisation scheme, the turbulence model, the free-surface tracking algorithm, the fluid-structure coupling—" She stopped on a page near the back. "What's this section?"
"A problem I couldn't solve. The solver works for steady-state conditions, but when you introduce gusting wind and wave action simultaneously, the time-stepping goes unstable. I think it's a numerical damping problem, but I couldn't find the right coefficient."
Yuna closed the folder. Removed her glasses. Cleaned them on her shirt. Replaced them.
"I need a software team," she said to Elena. "Four people minimum. Fluid dynamics background, parallel computing experience. With this architecture as a starting point, we could have a prototype in six weeks."
"Do it," Elena said. "Whatever you need."
---
The software team arrived within three days. Four women and one deeply sleep-deprived man who looked like he'd been dragged from a research lab and hadn't seen daylight in months. Yuna commandeered a corner of the studio and filled it with servers, monitors, and coffee-making equipment.
I tried to help. I sat in on planning meetings. I explained the physics. I sketched diagrams of what the solver needed to do at each timestep. But the actual coding—the implementation—was beyond me. I knew the math, not the programming, and after the second day of mostly getting in the way, I retreated to my side of the studio.
But I couldn't stop thinking about the boat.
The engine was the bottleneck. Without simulations, we couldn't verify the hull. Without hull verification, we couldn't build. And somewhere in Yuna's team's code, there were errors—small ones, subtle ones—that made the early outputs not quite match what my intuition said they should be.
I told myself I was just going to check a few things. Just look at the free-surface boundary condition. Just verify one equation.
---
Thirty-six hours later, I was still at the whiteboard.
"You missed dinner," said a voice from the doorway. "And breakfast. And lunch."
I didn't turn around. "The free-surface model is wrong."
"It's six in the evening."
"Is it?" I squinted at the windows. The light was wrong for morning. "Huh. Anyway, standard free-surface models assume deep-water conditions. But a sailing yacht operates at the interface—you're getting wave reflection off the hull, which creates local surface deformation that the standard model doesn't capture. I've been working on a modified boundary condition—"
Hands settled on my shoulders. Warm. Firm.
"John."
Something in her voice made me stop. I turned. Lilith stood behind me, still in her business clothes—she must have come straight from work—and her expression was the particular mix of concern and steel that I'd learned to recognise as her "I'm about to manage you" face.
"Hi," I said. "When did you get here?"
"Just now. Margaret said you've been in here since yesterday morning."
"Has it been that long? I napped a bit. On the couch."
"The couch is not a bed."
"It's a very comfortable couch."
Lilith's hands tightened on my shoulders. "You're coming with me. You're going to eat dinner. You're going to sleep."
"I can't. The free-surface model—"
"Will still be broken tomorrow."
"But I'm close. I can feel it. There's something wrong with the boundary condition, and once I fix that, the centre of lateral resistance will line up properly, and then—"
"John." Lilith stepped closer, her eyes searching my face. "You're swaying."
"I'm fine."
"You have ink on your forehead and you've been wearing the same shirt for two days. You're not fine."
"I've worked longer hours than this before. In my old life, I once did sixty hours straight for a quarterly close." The memory surfaced, unwelcome—fluorescent lights and cold coffee and the steady thrum of a headache that never quite went away. "This is different. This matters."
"Your health matters."
"The boat matters."
Lilith's jaw tightened. For a long moment, she just looked at me. Then she released my shoulders and stepped back.
"Fine," she said. "Show me."
"Show you?"
"Show me what you're working on. Explain it to me." She pulled over a stool and sat down, crossing her legs with the poise of someone who had all the time in the world. "If you won't leave, I'll stay."
I knew what she was doing. She was trying to slow me down. Engage a different part of my brain. Make me realise how tired I was.
But I also couldn't resist the chance to explain.
So I did. I walked her through the free-surface model, the boundary conditions, the wave-making resistance calculations. She asked questions—not technical ones, but smart ones, the kind that forced me to step back and think about the big picture instead of the details I'd been drowning in. By the time I'd finished explaining, an hour had passed and my voice had gone hoarse.
"Thank you," Lilith said. "That was fascinating."
"You're just trying to make me tired."
"Is it working?"
"Yes."
"Good." She stood up. "Come to bed."
"I can't. I really am close—"
"John." Her voice sharpened. "You're going to collapse. I'm not asking."
"You're not the boss of me."
The words came out before I could stop them—childish, stubborn, the verbal equivalent of crossing my arms. Lilith's eyes widened, then narrowed.
"Technically," she said, "I am. Contract. Clause Twelve."
"That's for employment. This isn't employment."
"This is self-destruction. Which falls under the unwritten clauses."
"There are unwritten clauses?"
"Several." She stepped closer. "I'm adding one now. 'The husband shall not work himself to unconsciousness.' Margaret will draft the language tomorrow."
"You can't just—"
"Watch me." She was very close now, close enough that I could smell her perfume—something subtle and expensive that I'd come to associate with safety and head pats and the particular way she smiled when I said something ridiculous. "John. Please."
The "please" nearly broke me. Lilith didn't say please. Lilith commanded and delegated and occasionally threatened legal action. She didn't stand in doorways with worry in her eyes and say *please*.
"I'll sleep when the simulation works," I said. It came out weaker than I intended.
Lilith stared at me for a long moment. Then something in her expression shifted—from concern to resolution to something I couldn't quite read.
"Fine," she said again. But this time, the word sounded different. Less like surrender, more like a decision. "I'll check on you tomorrow."
She kissed my forehead—a brief press of lips that left a warm spot—and walked out.
I turned back to the whiteboard. My hand was shaking slightly. From exhaustion, I told myself.
Not from the way she'd looked at me.
---
The second day blurred into the third.
I didn't sleep. I tried—once, around four in the morning—but every time I closed my eyes, equations scrolled across the backs of my eyelids. The turbulence model needed recalibration. The gust response was throwing everything off because the coupled aero-hydro solver wasn't accounting for the mast's wind shadow. The righting moment at thirty degrees of heel was off by four percent, and I couldn't figure out why.
The team came and went. Yuna brought me coffee that I forgot to drink. Priya left a sandwich that I ate without tasting. Elena stood in the doorway for a long moment, watching me work, then left without saying anything.
At some point, Saskia tried to intervene. "John. You need to rest."
"Almost there."
"You said that six hours ago."
"I mean it this time."
She exchanged a look with Yuna that I pretended not to see.
The world had narrowed to this: the whiteboard, the equations, the beautiful terrible complexity of wind and water and hull. I was so close. I could feel the solution waiting just beyond my grasp, like a word on the tip of my tongue. If I could just think clearly for ten more minutes. Five more minutes. One more—
The studio door opened.
This time, the footsteps were different. Heavier. More deliberate. I heard low voices—Elena's, then Yuna's—and then the sound of people gathering their things and leaving. The subtle click of equipment being powered down. The rustle of papers being neatly stacked.
I didn't turn around. "I'm fine, Lilith."
"I didn't say anything."
"Your presence is loud."
"My presence is quiet. You're just attuned to it." She had reached my side. I felt her warmth before I saw her, standing just behind my shoulder. "When did you last sleep?"
"Define sleep."
"Unconsciousness in a horizontal position."
"Then... Monday night? I think. It's hard to keep track."
"It's Thursday afternoon."
I finally turned. Lilith was wearing a dark green dress, simple and elegant, but her expression was anything but simple. She looked at me with an intensity that made me want to step back—but also, somehow, step forward.
"Thursday," I repeated. "That's... a while."
"It's sixty hours. Give or take." She reached out and carefully removed the marker from my hand. I let her. My fingers were too tired to protest. "I asked you to sleep. Two days ago."
"I remember."
"You said you would."
"I said I'd sleep when the simulation worked. It doesn't work yet. The free-surface model is correct now, but the turbulence scheme—"
"I'm invoking an intervention."
"What kind of intervention?"
I truned around but only then registered that we were alone.
"You cleared my team," I said. My voice sounded strange. Hollow.
"I cleared your team because they were too professional to tell you to stop, and you were too stubborn to listen." Lilith set the marker on the nearest table. "Now. We're going to talk."
"About what?"
"About you. About this." She gestured at the whiteboard, the papers, the chaos of three days of obsession. "About why you think the boat matters more than your health."
"The boat will still exist tomorrow. You might not, if you keep going like this." Her voice cracked on the last word—just slightly, just for a moment. "I can't watch you do this, John. I can't watch you destroy yourself over a project, even a beautiful one. Especially a beautiful one."
"I'm not destroying myself. I'm working."
"You worked yourself to death in your previous life."
The words hit me like a physical blow. I stared at her. "How did you—"
"You talk in your sleep sometimes. Brief things. Names. Numbers." She stepped closer. "I also pay attention. I notice the way you flinch at certain phrases. The way you talk about your old life like it was a prison. 'Spreadsheet hell.' 'Quarterly close.' 'Sixty hours straight.' You told me you died from overwork, John. You told me that yourself."
"I didn't mean—"
"I know you didn't mean to tell me. But I know. And I refuse—" Her voice strengthened. "I refuse to watch you do it again. Not in my house. Not on my watch. Not when I—"
She stopped. Swallowed. When she spoke again, her voice was quieter.
"You matter. You, the person, not the designs, not the boats. I didn't sign a contract with your keel calculations. I signed it with you."
I opened my mouth. No words came out.
"Come here," Lilith said.
It wasn't a request.
She took my hand and led me away from the whiteboard, away from the papers, toward the leather sofa in the corner—the one that Margaret had insisted on because "every design studio needs a thinking couch." I followed because I was too tired to resist and too confused to argue and too something-else to want to pull away.
"Sit," she said.
I sat. The leather was cool and soft. When had I last sat on something that wasn't a drafting stool?
"I want to talk about the simulation," Lilith said, settling beside me.
"I thought you wanted me to stop thinking about the simulation."
"I want you to stop obsessing." She turned to face me, tucking her legs beneath her. "Tell me about the free-surface model. The one you finally fixed."
"I... wait. Really?"
"Really."
So I told her. Haltingly at first, then with more energy as the technical details surfaced. The wave reflection problem. The modified boundary condition. The way the centre of lateral resistance had snapped into alignment once I'd corrected for the hull's own wave-making interference.
Lilith listened. She asked questions—good ones, as always—and her attention never wavered. But as I talked, I noticed she was moving closer. Shifting her weight. Her hand had come to rest on the back of the sofa, inches from my shoulder.
"And the turbulence model?" she asked. Her voice was lower than it had been.
"That's the problem. It's still not right. The gust response keeps diverging because the solver isn't—"
I didn't finish the sentence.
Because Lilith had placed her hand on my chest and pushed. Gently. Firmly. Inexorably.
My back met the sofa cushions. I stared up at her, my brain making a sound like a skipped record.
"What are you—"
"You weren't going to stop talking." She swung one leg over me, positioning herself above me with the careful deliberation of someone who had planned this—or at least, someone who was very good at improvisation. "You were going to keep explaining turbulence models until you collapsed. So I'm making you stop."
Her hair had fallen forward, creating a curtain around her face, and her green eyes were absolutely focused on mine. I could feel the warmth of her through my shirt, the subtle weight of her balanced above me.
All thoughts of the simulation vanished. The free-surface model. The turbulence scheme. The righting moment. Gone. Wiped clean. There was nothing in my head except Lilith.
"You've been trying to make me sleep for three days," I managed.
"I've been trying to make you stop for three days. The sleeping is secondary." She braced one hand beside my head. "You were running on fumes and stubbornness, John. Reasoning didn't work. Asking didn't work. Pleading didn't work." Her lips curved into a smile that was half fond, half predatory. "I decided to try something more effective."
Then she kissed me.
It was different from our first kiss. That one had been gentle—a question, an offering, a first step. This was a declaration. Deep and deliberate, her mouth fitting against mine with the confidence of someone who had decided exactly what she wanted and wasn't going to pretend otherwise.
My hands found her waist—reflex, instinct, desperate need for something to hold onto. Her free hand slid up my chest to my jaw, tilting my face up, and the kiss deepened.
I don't know how long it lasted. Long enough for my heart to reset its rhythm from "spreadsheet anxiety" to "Lilith." Long enough for every tense muscle in my body to unlock one by one. Long enough for the constant hum of calculations in the back of my mind to finally, mercifully, go quiet.
When she pulled back, we were both breathing harder. Her cheeks were flushed, her composure—that legendary Vance composure—was nowhere to be seen, and she was looking at me like I was something precious and infuriating and entirely hers.
"There," she said, slightly breathless. "Now you're thinking about something else."
"I'm not thinking about anything at all," I said honestly. "My brain stopped working."
"Good. That was the goal." She lowered herself down, settling against my side on the sofa—which was definitely not designed for two people, but she made it work through pure determination. Her head found the hollow of my shoulder. Her arm draped across my chest. "Don't move."
"I couldn't if I wanted to."
"Even better."
We lay there in silence. The studio was darkening around us, evening light fading to twilight through the big northern windows. Somewhere far away, I heard the sounds of the estate settling into its nightly rhythm—doors closing, staff retiring, the distant chime of a clock.
"Lilith?"
"Mm."
"I'm sorry I didn't listen. Before."
"You're stubborn. I knew that when I married you." Her fingers traced idle patterns on my chest. "You're also passionate about things you care about. I knew that too. I just needed to find the right way to reach you."
"This was the right way."
"I thought it might be." I could hear the smile in her voice. "You've been running on anxiety and obsession for three days, John. Your brain wouldn't turn off. I needed to give it something bigger than the boat."
"You're bigger than the boat."
"I should hope so. The boat is only thirty-five metres."
I laughed—a weak, exhausted sound that turned into something suspiciously like a sob halfway through. She felt it. Her arm tightened around me.
"Sleep," she said. "I'll be here."
"The simulation—"
"Will still be broken when you wake up. Or it won't be. Yuna and her team are very good, and you've given them everything they need. You don't have to solve everything yourself."
"That's... a hard habit to break."
"I know." She pressed a kiss to my jaw. "We have time."
The words settled over me like a blanket. We have time. Such a simple thing. Such an impossible luxury. In my previous life, I'd never felt like I had time—always another deadline, another quarter, another emergency that only I could fix. But here, in the quiet studio, with Lilith's weight warm against my side and the battle finally draining out of me, I had time.
"Thank you," I murmured. The words were getting heavy. "For dragging me. Literally."
"You needed dragging."
"Still. Thank you."
She didn't answer. Or maybe she did, and I was already too far gone to hear.
The last thing I felt before sleep took me was her hand finding mine, her fingers lacing through my ink-stained ones, and her voice—soft, private, barely a whisper:
"I've got you."
---
I slept for sixteen hours.
When I woke, the studio was bright with mid-morning sun. Someone had draped a blanket over me—two blankets, actually, one of which smelled faintly of Lilith's perfume. The sofa was too short, my neck was stiff, and I was still wearing the same clothes I'd been wearing for three days straight.
I had never felt better in my life.
Voices drifted from the other side of the studio. Yuna's, rapid and excited. Elena's, measured but pleased. Saskia's laugh—a sound I hadn't heard before. I sat up, rubbing my eyes, and saw the entire team clustered around a monitor. On the screen, a sleek hull cut through digital waves. Numbers scrolled in a sidebar.
Almost all of them were green.
"It works," Yuna was saying. "The simulation ran clean. Fourteen point two knots in twenty knots of wind. Righting moment stable. Flooding survivable. Drag polar matching the theoretical curve within two percent." She looked up and saw me. "John! It worked. The modifications you left—the free-surface corrections, the turbulence recalibration—we implemented them last night and ran the full suite this morning. She's perfect."
I stood up, the blankets falling away. My legs were unsteady but my head was clear—clearer than it had been in days.
"Fourteen point two," I said. "I calculated fourteen point five."
"That's within the uncertainty margin of the turbulence model. We can probably tune it further, but for all practical purposes—" Yuna broke into a grin. "She's fast, John. Really fast."
I walked to the monitor. The *Indolence* sailed across the screen, elegant and effortless, exactly the way I'd imagined her in those first sketches in the locked guest room. The simulation showed everything I'd hoped for. Righting moment within safety margins. Bulkhead placements correct. Flooding scenarios survivable. Maximum speed: 14.2 knots.
"Where's Lilith?" I asked.
"Madam Lilith left approximately two hours ago," Elena said. "She had an early meeting. She asked us to let you sleep." A pause. "She also asked us to inform you that she's 'glad the intervention was successful.' Her words."
I felt my face heat. The team was politely not looking at me with the knowing expressions they were definitely wearing. Priya was suddenly very interested in her material samples. Saskia had turned to study the monitor with intense focus.
"Right," I said. "The intervention. That was—"
"We saw nothing," Yuna said. "We heard nothing. We arrived this morning and found you asleep and the simulation ready to run."
"The door was locked when we got here," Saskia added helpfully. "We used our keycards."
"Madam Lilith sent a message that the studio was off-limits until eight AM," Elena said, her voice absolutely neutral. "We followed instructions."
I looked at them. Four of the world's top engineers, all of whom had clearly spent the morning running the most important simulation of their careers, and all of whom were studiously pretending they hadn't spent the previous evening being evacuated so their employer could physically force her husband to sleep.
"Thank you," I said. "For... the simulation. And the discretion."
"You're welcome, Chief Designer," Elena said. And then—just for a moment—she smiled. "Now. Shall we review the results? There are a few areas where we think further optimisation is possible."
I pulled up a chair. Someone put coffee in my hand. The simulation played again, and this time, I watched it with a clear head and a full night's sleep and the lingering warmth of Lilith's kiss still on my lips.
She was fast. She was beautiful. And she was real—or close enough to real that I could taste the salt spray.
"Let's review," I said. "Show me the drag polar again."
The team leaned in. The numbers scrolled. And somewhere in the corner of my mind, I filed away the memory of Lilith's voice in the darkness: *I've got you.*
She did.
And somehow, impossibly, that made everything else possible too.

















# Chapter 9: Lilith's Possessive Side
The rumours started, as rumours do, at the dockyard.
Port Cartwright had been a sleepy coastal town before we arrived. Its primary industries were fishing, mild tourism, and the kind of comfortable stagnation that came from being too small to attract attention. The arrival of Vance-Doe Marine—with its security gates, its covered slipways, and its steady stream of engineers who spoke in technical jargon and drank alarming amounts of coffee—had been the most exciting thing to happen in decades.
The locals talked. The contractors talked. The suppliers talked. And somewhere in the chain of talk, word reached the wider maritime industry that something unusual was being built in Port Cartwright. Something that didn't use engines. Something that had computational tools no one else possessed.
Which was how Helion Maritime found us.
---
The delegation arrived on a Thursday, unannounced.
I was in the shipyard's design office—a converted warehouse loft with good light and a view of the slipway—reviewing the latest laminate schedules with Priya when Margaret's voice came through the intercom.
"Master John, there are visitors at the main gate. A delegation from Helion Maritime. They say they have a proposal for you."
I looked up from the carbon fibre specifications. "For me? Not for Lilith?"
"They specifically requested you, sir. A Mr. Marcus Webb is leading the delegation."
"Marcus Webb," Priya said, her voice carefully neutral. "Helion's vice president of strategic partnerships. He's known for being... persuasive."
"Is that code for something?"
"Code for 'has poached three of our industry's top designers in the past two years.' He's very charming. Very handsome. Very good at making people feel like they're the most important person in the room." She set down her laminate sample. "I'd recommend calling Madam Lilith."
"It's just a meeting. I can handle a meeting."
"Of course, Chief Designer."
She said it the same way someone might say "of course, the cliff looks perfectly stable."
---
Marcus Webb was, unfortunately, exactly as advertised.
He was tall, with the kind of jawline that belonged on magazine covers and the kind of smile that had probably closed a hundred business deals. His suit was impeccably tailored, his handshake was firm and warm, and his eyes—a striking shade of blue—lit up when he saw me like I was exactly the person he'd been hoping to meet.
"John Doe. The visionary behind the sailing yacht." He held my hand a beat longer than necessary. "I've been following your project with great interest. The rumours don't do it justice."
"Thank you," I said, retrieving my hand. "This is... a surprise visit."
"I apologise for the lack of notice. When I heard what you were building, I couldn't wait for formal channels." He gestured to the two women behind him—both in crisp business attire, both carrying tablets. "My associates, Ms. Miller and Ms. Okonkwo. We represent Helion Maritime's advanced projects division."
"How did you hear about the project?"
"Trade publications have been speculating about the Vance-Doe shipyard for weeks. A new computational approach to hull design? A custom physics engine?" His smile widened. "The maritime world is smaller than you'd think, Mr. Doe. Word travels."
I led them to the shipyard's conference room—a modest space with a view of the slipway where the *Indolence*'s hull jig was taking shape. I noticed Marcus's eyes tracking the covered construction bay with undisguised interest.
"We'd like to offer a partnership," he said, settling into a chair with the easy grace of someone accustomed to being welcomed. "Helion has resources that a single-family shipyard can't match. Manufacturing facilities. Distribution networks. Marketing reach. You have the designs. Together—"
"I'm not looking for partners."
"Every artist needs a patron." He leaned forward. "What you're building here—a sailing yacht, in this day and age—it's more than a vessel. It's a statement. A revolution. Helion wants to help you bring that revolution to the world."
"It's a private project."
"Private projects have a way of becoming public obsessions." He smiled again, and this time it was self-deprecating, almost shy. "I'll be honest with you, John—may I call you John?—I came here expecting to find an interesting technical exercise. What I found instead was the most innovative hull design I've seen in fifteen years, backed by computational tools that don't exist anywhere else. You're not just building a boat. You're reinventing an industry."
The flattery was expert. Warm and specific and calibrated to make me feel seen. I could see why he'd successfully poached those designers.
"I appreciate the interest," I said. "But the project isn't for sale. It's personal."
"Everything's personal until it becomes profitable." Marcus reached across the table and, casually, placed his hand on my shoulder. "A visionary like you deserves proper backing. Why limit yourself to one company? One—" he paused, letting the word hang, "—partner?"
I opened my mouth to respond.
The conference room door opened.
Lilith stood in the doorway.
She was wearing charcoal grey, her hair swept up in a style that meant she'd come straight from a board meeting. Her expression was perfectly composed. Her posture was perfectly relaxed. She looked, in every way, like a woman who had simply decided to check on her husband's meeting.
I knew her well enough by now to see the danger.
"Darling," she said, her voice warm and absolutely controlled. "When Margaret told me we had visitors, I had to come see. Helion Maritime. What an unexpected pleasure."
Marcus's hand dropped from my shoulder. He rose to his feet, his professional smile sliding back into place. "Lady Lilith. I was just discussing a potential collaboration with your husband."
"So I heard." Lilith crossed the room with the measured grace of a predator who knew it had all the time in the world. She didn't sit across from Marcus. She sat beside me—close beside me, her thigh pressing against mine—and took my hand in hers.
It was a gesture of affection. It was also a territorial claim so blatant that I saw Ms. Miller's eyebrows rise a millimetre.
"A partnership," Lilith continued, her thumb tracing small circles on the back of my hand. "How intriguing. What terms were you considering?"
Marcus, to his credit, recovered quickly. "Helion would provide manufacturing and distribution infrastructure. We'd handle the commercial side entirely—sales, marketing, customer relations. Your husband would continue as lead designer, with a dedicated studio at our headquarters and a significant equity stake in the resulting product line." He leaned back, projecting confidence. "We believe his work could revolutionise the recreational maritime sector."
"Revolutionise," Lilith repeated. "That's quite a word."
"I don't use it lightly."
"Nor should you." She shifted slightly, her shoulder brushing mine. "Let me make sure I understand. You want my husband—who, I should mention, is contractually prohibited from accepting outside employment—to design boats for your company. You want access to the computational tools our team spent months developing. You want to commercialise a project that was conceived as a private vessel." Her smile didn't waver. "And in exchange, you're offering an equity stake in a product line that doesn't exist yet, plus the privilege of working from your headquarters instead of his own studio."
"An extremely generous equity stake—"
"The Vance Group has a market capitalisation of approximately forty-seven billion credits," Lilith said. "Helion Maritime's market capitalisation is approximately three point two billion. You're offering my husband a percentage of something significantly smaller than what he already has access to."
Marcus's smile tightened. "Resources aren't only about market cap."
"No, they're not. They're also about control." Lilith set her tablet on the table—she'd been carrying it this whole time, I realised, and had clearly prepared. "Shall we discuss the intellectual property situation? The *Indolence* design is protected by seventeen patent applications filed jointly under my husband's name and Vance-Doe Marine. The simulation engine, Zephyr, has an additional eight patents pending. Any outside collaboration would require navigating those protections, plus the comprehensive non-compete clauses in my husband's contract." She tilted her head. "Clauses that I would enforce to the fullest extent of the law. Not out of spite. Simply out of... diligence."
She said "diligence" the way someone else might say "nuclear option."
The silence in the room was absolute.
Ms. Miller had stopped taking notes. Ms. Okonkwo was studying the ceiling with the expression of someone who'd just watched a building get demolished and was grateful not to have been inside.
Marcus Webb, to his considerable credit, managed to maintain his smile. It had become somewhat fixed.
"I see," he said. "The legal landscape is more complex than I'd anticipated."
"Legal landscapes usually are." Lilith released my hand—finally—but only to reach across and adjust my collar with casual intimacy. "I'm sure you understand. My husband is a brilliant designer, but he's also a protected asset. I take that protection seriously."
"Clearly." Marcus stood. "Well. I appreciate your candour, Lady Lilith. Mr. Doe." He nodded to me. "If circumstances should change—"
"They won't."
"—please do keep Helion in mind."
He left with his associates trailing behind him, their retreat so swift it was nearly a tactical withdrawal. The door clicked shut.
I turned to Lilith. She was still sitting beside me, the picture of composed satisfaction.
"That," I said, "was terrifying."
"Yes."
"You dismantled him like a first-year intern."
"He touched your shoulder."
"He handed me a business card."
"Same thing." She stood, smoothing her skirt. "Helion has been trying to infiltrate my supply chain for three years. Sending their most photogenic executive to flatter my husband was a new tactic. I'll give them points for creativity."
"You knew he was coming."
"Margaret alerted me the moment they arrived at the gate. I had the legal team prepare a brief on the way over." She glanced at me. "You handled yourself well, by the way."
"I was about to tell him no."
"I know. I just wanted to make sure he understood why no meant no." She paused. "Also, I wanted to watch his face when he realised he'd made a tactical error."
"Which tactical error?"
"Thinking you were the easier target just because you're the one who designs boats instead of the one who runs companies." Her smile was sharp and fond. "You're not easier, John. You're just different. And you're mine."
The word hung in the air between us. *Mine.*
In my previous life, that would have bothered me. Ownership. Possession. The implication that I was something to be kept.
But Lilith didn't say it like I was property. She said it like I was something precious. Something she'd chosen and would defend without hesitation. Something she couldn't bear to lose.
"That's the second time you've territorial-marked me in front of business rivals," I said.
"The first time was Damian. That was different."
"Was it?"
"Damian was an annoyance. Marcus was a threat." She turned to face me fully. "Damian had nothing I wanted. Marcus wanted you."
"He wanted my designs."
"He wanted your attention. Your collaboration. Your time." Her voice had gone quieter, more serious. "I don't share those things, John. Not with rival companies. Not with photogenic vice presidents. And not with anyone who looks at you the way he did."
"How did he look at me?"
"Like you were an asset to be acquired." She stepped closer. "You're not an asset. You're my husband."
I thought about Priestley's warning at the gala—the way the husbands' corner assessed each other, the hierarchy of value and status. In that world, I was a gold-digging commoner who'd landed above his station. But to Lilith, I was something else entirely. Something worth defending with legal briefs and territorial displays and the quiet, fierce certainty of *you're mine*.
"It's not the same," I said slowly, "but I think I'd do the same thing. If someone tried to take you away."
Lilith's eyebrows rose. "Would you?"
"I don't have legal briefs. But I'd probably say something awkward and then eat their hors d'oeuvres while glaring."
"That's more intimidating than you think. Your glare is surprisingly effective when you stop looking like a confused puppy."
"I don't look like a confused puppy."
"You absolutely do. It's one of the things I like about you." She reached up and smoothed my collar—the same gesture she'd used during the meeting, but this time it felt less performative. "I've added a security detail to the shipyard. And I'm having 'Doe Yachts' trademarked."
"Doe Yachts? That's not a thing."
"It will be. Eventually."
"I don't want to run a company."
"You won't. You design boats. I handle the predators." She patted my chest. "Now. Priya said something about laminate schedules before we were interrupted?"
---
Over the next week, I noticed changes.
A private security detail appeared at the shipyard gates—two women in dark suits who checked credentials with professional thoroughness. The design office had new locks installed, keyed to the team's individual access cards. Margaret informed me that all external meeting requests would now be pre-screened by Lilith's office.
"Those are new," I said on Friday, pointing at the security cameras that had sprouted near the slipway entrance.
"Madam Lilith's orders," Margaret said. "She wants all construction bays monitored."
"She thinks someone might steal the boat?"
"She thinks someone might try to gather intelligence on the boat. The hull design is now classified as proprietary trade information."
"Classified by whom?"
"By Madam Lilith. Approximately six hours after the Helion delegation departed."
I should have found it suffocating. The security. The surveillance. The way Lilith had essentially wrapped my project in legal and physical armour. In my old life, I'd chafed at any kind of control—the micromanaging bosses, the invasive productivity tracking, the sense of being watched and measured at every moment.
But this wasn't control. This was protection.
Lilith wasn't trying to limit what I could do. She was trying to make sure no one else could take what I'd built. She was standing guard at the gates of my project so I didn't have to.
I found it, against all logic, oddly flattering.
---
"You're wearing a different shirt," Lilith observed at breakfast on Saturday.
I looked down at myself. It was a nice shirt—dark blue, better fabric than my usual, a cut that actually fit properly. I'd found it in my wardrobe earlier in the week, one of several that had mysteriously appeared after Margaret had done a "seasonal refresh" of my clothing.
"Just felt like a change," I said.
"It suits you."
"Thank you."
She returned to her tablet, but I caught the tiny smile at the corner of her mouth. The one that meant she'd noticed something and filed it away.
The next day, I wore another new shirt. Dark green this time—not quite her signature colour, but close. When I walked into the conservatory, Lilith looked up, and her expression flickered through something that was definitely appreciation before she controlled it.
"That's a nice colour," she said.
"I thought so too."
"Any particular reason for the wardrobe upgrade?"
"Just experimenting." I sat down across from her, trying to project casualness. "You seemed to like the blue one."
"I did."
"This one?"
"Also good." Her eyes lingered on my collar for a fraction of a second longer than necessary. "You're aware that I notice when you do things, John."
"That's the idea."
"Ah." She set down her tablet. "So this is deliberate."
"Maybe."
"You're trying to get a reaction."
"Is it working?"
Lilith stood up, walked around the table, and leaned down to press a kiss to my forehead. It lingered—just barely—before she pulled back.
"It's working," she said. "But you should know that I'd notice you in a burlap sack. The shirts are just a bonus."
She returned to her seat with the serene expression of someone who'd won the exchange. I sat there, forehead tingling, and tried to remember why I'd thought I could out-manoeuvre Lilith Vance at anything.
"Noted," I said.
"See that you do." She picked up her tablet. "I believe you have a keel assembly to review this morning. Don't let me keep you."
I left for the shipyard with undignified warmth in my chest. The security guards at the gate nodded as I passed. The new cameras tracked my progress toward the design office. And somewhere in the back of my mind, I was already thinking about the next shirt.
Lilith's possessiveness, I was learning, wasn't a cage. It was a choice—fierce and deliberate and completely unwavering. She'd chosen me. She'd defend that choice against charming vice presidents and rival companies and anyone else who tried to interfere.
And if that meant I started wearing nicer clothes to watch her notice?
Well. That was my choice too.
