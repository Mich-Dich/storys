

The station groaned like it had a hundred thousand secrets it wasn’t willing to share, the walls of raw rock scarred with tunnels and scaffolds, neon lights sputtering against pools of smoke that hung thick in the corridors. Machinery rattled and hissed in the distance, a constant industrial heartbeat, punctuated by the clang of metal against metal and the occasional explosion that shook the floor like a reminder someone, somewhere, was bleeding for profit. Shadows clung to the corners, trading whispers with thieves, drunks, and the occasional desperate soul who thought hiding behind a pillar might buy them a second of safety. Slaves moved in rigid lines, their shoulders bent under cargo, collars glinting under the artificial light, eyes trained downward, never daring to meet another’s gaze. Traders shouted over the din, hands waving with contract slips and credits, while the station guards drifted by like ghosts, indifferent, arms empty, minds elsewhere, letting fights, thefts, and worse slide without so much as a twitch of concern.

In the center of this chaos, John leaned back on a cracked bar stool, his elbows resting on the sticky laminate of the counter, a glass of amber liquor sliding lazily in circles as if tracing the orbit of a tiny moon he alone could see. The gamblers around him didn’t matter. The dice, the cards, the occasional fistfight—everything blurred into the background of a world he had already walked away from, a place where ambition was a bad joke and trust was currency no one could afford. He sipped, and the alcohol tasted like it always did: bitter, numbing, and just strong enough to make everything else irrelevant. His thoughts wandered, drifting like the station’s artificial winds: glory was a memory too fragile to bother with, love a trap that left him bleeding in empty hotel rooms on planets with names nobody remembered, and human connection—well, human connection was just another form of leverage, better avoided entirely.

He flicked the dice across the table, watching them spin and bounce with more interest than the man opposite him, who cursed as he lost another small pile of credits. John didn’t care. The numbers fascinated him, the way momentum, surface tension, and imperfect friction combined to decide the outcome of something everyone called luck. Somewhere in the back of his mind, he noted the alignment of the girders overhead, how a single miscalculation in the rotation of the scaffolding could shear a man’s leg in half, how the rusted joints of a cargo bot’s arm would eventually tear itself free under strain. It was absurd, all of it, the way the station held together on chance and brute force, and for a moment, a grin teased at the corner of his mouth, sharp and private, before dissolving into another sip. The bartender’s gaze flicked to him and then away, uninterested, as if he had already seen a hundred men like John walk in, spiral out, and vanish into the station’s bowels.

He tossed the dice again, rambling under his breath, half to himself and half to the smoke-thick room. "If the flux coil in that loader bot’s auxiliary drive is off by point zero zero three, it’s going to shear the mount like—like bending a bone you didn’t mean to touch…hah, but the energy transfer would be…wait, where’d the other die go?" His words trailed off, a mash of mechanical observations, drunken asides about thermodynamic inefficiency in gravity pumps, and the kind of casual brilliance nobody would recognize without context. A metal crate shifted somewhere above, squealing against its tracks, and his gaze flicked to it. That small moment of clarity—the way the cargo bot’s servos were misaligned, the slight imbalance of tension in its support cable—made him smirk, though he would forget it by the time the dice settled.

Somewhere in the haze of neon and smoke, the station itself seemed to pulse in rhythm with his pulse. The hum of the core underfoot, the high-pitched whine of ion drives docking, the occasional scream from a fight or a collapsed floor—it all combined into a symphony of entropy. He leaned forward, tracing the rim of his glass with a finger, murmuring calculations no one could follow, ideas about energy compression, magnetic flux, impossible engineering feats, and then letting them tumble into nonsense as the alcohol claimed more of his coordination. He had a ship, somewhere quiet, somewhere built from things far beyond what the station’s engineers could even imagine. It waited for him, a sleeping god of steel and circuitry, and for now, he was content to be nothing more than a body in the crowd, a single man dissolving into a universe that owed him nothing.

He threw the dice one more time, and they clattered against the table, bouncing into oblivion like his ambition, and John laughed, low, dark, and without warmth, leaning back as if gravity itself was conspiring to push him down. The chaos around him carried on, indifferent. Guards ignored a brawl starting in the corner, a trader shoved a slave into a wall, sparks flew from an exposed conduit. John let it all blur together, perfect background noise, the soundtrack of a station built on exploitation, desperation, and chance, the same way his thoughts blurred brilliance into apathy, and brilliance again.

The alcohol tugged at his eyelids, his limbs heavy, but his mind refused to fully sleep, twitching at random insights and absurd observations about the scaffolding above, the way a single misaligned conduit could cascade into a failure across half the station. Somewhere deep in that fog of intoxication, he smiled, a faint, private acknowledgment of the one thing nobody else could touch: understanding, if only for a few brief, fleeting moments, the absolute ridiculousness of everything and the way it somehow held together anyway.

John drifted through it all with the vague intention of going somewhere specific, though the idea of direction felt abstract, like trying to remember a dream that had already faded.

He clipped his shoulder against a passing worker without noticing, stumbled over a loose cable that sparked angrily at the contact, and continued forward with the kind of stubborn persistence that only existed in people too drunk to admit they had no idea what they were doing. The station shifted around him in disjointed fragments, pieces of machinery jutting out at odd angles, pipes vibrating with pressure that should have burst them hours ago, a distant grinding noise that suggested something large was either being built or coming apart. His mind latched onto it briefly, calculating tolerances, stress points, the exact moment of catastrophic failure, and then let it go just as quickly, because holding onto anything felt exhausting.

A moving platform slid into place in front of him with a hydraulic hiss, and he stepped onto it half a second too late, his foot catching the edge as it began to descend. The impact threw him sideways into a dented metal container that had no reason to be there except that someone had decided it was not worth moving. The container tipped, clattered loudly against the floor, and John followed it down in an ungraceful collapse, landing flat on his back with a dull, hollow thud that echoed slightly in the confined space.

For a moment he stayed there, staring up at the exposed cables and rusted beams above, watching a thin stream of sparks spit intermittently from a cracked junction. His chest rose and fell slowly, and then he let out a quiet, thoughtful breath, as if he had just reached a profound conclusion.

"I am officially not fit to navigate anything more complex than gravity," he muttered to no one in particular, his voice low and rough, the words slurring together just enough to make them feel detached from intent. "Ship probably disagrees, but the ship is…unreasonably optimistic about my capabilities under chemical influence."

A shadow passed over him as a cargo unit moved along the track above, something heavy dangling beneath it, swaying slightly with each mechanical jerk. He watched it for a second longer than necessary, eyes narrowing as if trying to solve a problem that no longer held his interest, then rolled onto his side and pushed himself up with visible effort. The world tilted in response, the corridor stretching and compressing in ways that felt deeply incorrect, but he compensated by leaning into it, letting imbalance become motion.

He staggered forward again, slower now, his steps uneven but determined in the vague way of someone who had decided that stopping was not an option even if the destination had been forgotten. The station did not care. It continued to grind and pulse around him, swallowing the sound of his footsteps, replacing it with the constant mechanical hum that seemed to seep into bone.

The slave market was not marked by any sign, because it did not need one. The density of bodies changed first, then the posture, then the silence. Lines of people stood or knelt behind low barriers, collars catching the light, expressions flattened into something that was not quite empty but close enough to pass for it. Traders moved between them with the same casual authority as someone sorting through spare parts, gesturing, negotiating, occasionally striking when a response was too slow or insufficient.

John slowed without fully realizing why, his gaze drifting across the rows with detached curiosity, as if he had wandered into a section of the station that operated on a different set of physical laws. His mind struggled to connect purpose to observation, but something simple and immediate surfaced through the haze, cutting cleanly through the rest of his thoughts.

He needed to get back to his ship, and he was in no condition to manage that alone.

The solution presented itself with the same blunt practicality as everything else in this place, and he accepted it without resistance, because thinking beyond the immediate felt unnecessary. He changed direction slightly, angling toward one of the stalls where a trader stood behind a makeshift counter, tapping idly at a handheld device while keeping half an eye on his inventory.

"I need one," John said, resting a hand on the edge of the counter to steady himself, his gaze unfocused but intent in the way of someone who had locked onto a single idea and refused to let it go. "Temporary function. Navigation assistance. Minimal conversation preferred, though not strictly required."

The trader looked up, taking in John’s state with a quick, practiced assessment, the kind that measured value and risk in the same glance. There was no confusion, no hesitation, only the immediate shift into transaction.

"Specifications narrow price range," the trader replied flatly, already scrolling through options. "Physical capability, language compatibility, obedience tier, durability—"

"Something that can walk," John interrupted, waving a hand vaguely as if the rest was irrelevant. "And preferably something that does not look like it belongs in a scrap heap, because I have to look at it while it gets me there."

The trader paused for a fraction of a second, then nodded slightly, turning and gesturing toward the line behind him. The movement was efficient, practiced, devoid of any acknowledgment that he was presenting people rather than objects.

John’s gaze drifted over them, unfocused at first, then sharpening just enough to discriminate between shapes and features. He was not searching with intent, not weighing qualities or considering implications, only following an instinct dulled but not entirely erased by alcohol. His eyes lingered where contrast existed, where something stood out against the uniformity of exhaustion and restraint.

Lilith stood still among the others, her posture straight but not rigid, her gaze lowered but not unfocused, every detail of her presence controlled with the precision of someone who understood that survival depended on it. The horns marked her immediately, thin and dark against her skin, the tail held low and still behind her, a detail that would have drawn attention even in a less crowded space.

John tilted his head slightly, as if aligning a mental image that refused to stay still.

"That one," he said, the decision forming and completing in the same instant, without analysis, without hesitation. "She will do."

The trader glanced at Lilith, then back at John, calculating again, adjusting numbers that only existed in his own interest. A price was stated, numbers exchanged, authorization confirmed with a brief flicker of light across the collar at her throat. The entire process unfolded with mechanical efficiency, each step following the last without pause, as impersonal as any other system on the station.

Lilith stepped forward when prompted, her movements smooth, controlled, her expression unchanged. Internally, the assessment began immediately, fast and precise, cataloging everything visible and audible about the man now designated as her owner. His posture indicated instability, his speech patterns suggested intoxication, his focus fluctuated unpredictably, and yet there were inconsistencies that did not align with typical profiles. His gaze, when it settled, carried a sharpness that did not match the rest of him, and the fragments of speech she had overheard hinted at a level of technical understanding far beyond the norm for someone operating in this environment.

Risk factors adjusted accordingly.

She did not expect kindness, because kindness was not part of the system she existed within. She did not expect cruelty either, not specifically, because cruelty was variable and therefore less predictable than indifference. What she expected was function, orders, compliance, and the consequences of failure. The collar at her throat remained an absolute, a constant boundary she had never been able to test.

She stopped in front of him, head slightly inclined, awaiting instruction, every muscle held in readiness without visible tension.

John looked at her for a second longer than necessary, as if confirming that the decision he had already made still made sense, then nodded once, more to himself than to her.

"Right," he murmured, turning unsteadily toward the corridor he had come from, already assuming she would follow. "You are now a navigation solution, which is a promotion of questionable merit, but we will accept it as progress."

Lilith followed immediately, because that was the only correct response, her steps measured and silent behind his uneven path, the distance between them calculated to allow reaction without overstepping. The trader had already turned away, the transaction complete, her existence in his world reduced to a completed exchange logged somewhere in a system that would never think of her again.







Lilith followed him down the corridor without a word, her boots clacking softly against the metal grates, her tail brushing lightly against the back of her legs in a movement so controlled it might have been mistaken for nothing at all, if one weren’t paying attention. The station around them continued its indifferent grind, sparks hissing from exposed conduits, crates shifting on overhead rails, the distant echo of a body hitting the floor somewhere behind them. John stumbled on a loose panel and caught himself against her shoulder, muttering something about gravity misalignment as though it were a personal affront, and she didn’t flinch, didn’t react, simply adjusted her stance to compensate, a small calculation executed without hesitation.

They reached the hover-bike rental booth—a narrow alcove crammed with buzzing electronics and the smell of ozone, grease, and half-baked lubricants—and John leaned on the counter like a man who had forgotten how to stand upright. The operator barely looked up, pressing a few buttons, gesturing toward the bike, and Lilith moved forward instinctively, swinging herself onto the seat with the smooth efficiency of muscle memory she hadn’t had reason to use until now. John climbed behind her, arms wrapped loosely around her waist, grip uncertain, balance nonexistent, and immediately began commenting on the slight sway of her horns in relation to his center of gravity as if it were a pressing technical flaw.

The hover-bike roared to life beneath them, a low hum that vibrated through metal and bone, and Lilith guided it into the open corridor with careful precision, eyes sweeping the space ahead for moving platforms, hanging scaffolds, and any cargo that might choose to drop at exactly the wrong moment. John leaned back, relaxed against her, and launched into a long, meandering monologue about energy flow in the station’s gravity systems, the inefficiency of the plumbing in the older sectors, the likelihood that the cooling ducts would collapse if they weren’t reinforced with additional struts. His words ran over themselves, tangling in half-finished observations, jokes about probability, and inappropriate comments that drifted from her horns to her posture to how absurdly clean her boots were compared to everything else on the station.

She didn’t respond, didn’t even blink in acknowledgement, but her mind was active in a way John’s was not. Every spinning scaffold, every worker rushing past with cargo or slung bodies, every exposed junction that might spray fire or electricity, every weak grating or worn handrail—she noted it all, mentally assigning hazard levels, calculating escape paths, memorizing the rhythm of the chaotic traffic through the tunnels. While John’s observations were scattered, absurd, often irrelevant, hers were precise, real-time, and quietly life-preserving.

The corridors twisted and turned like the inside of a wounded beast, the lights flickering over walls streaked with soot and sweat, cables swaying above them with a soft, threatening hiss. A crate swung loose from its chain, tipping as it brushed the edge of a platform, and John laughed, a high, jagged sound, pointing at it and declaring with no particular logic that it should have failed compression tests centuries ago. Lilith nudged the hover-bike to one side, accelerating smoothly past the hazard, and he flailed slightly in protest, muttering about Newton’s laws and how every misaligned pulley in the station was a personal insult.

She felt the pressure of him shift with each movement, listened to the rambling tangents that included physics, mechanics, and increasingly ridiculous conjectures about what the station itself would do if it were “sentient and bored,” and cataloged it all. There was a rhythm to his chaos, a pattern beneath the randomness, and she adjusted her own focus to maintain equilibrium for both of them, steering with calculated anticipation as though the vehicle were an extension of her own body rather than a borrowed machine.

The corridors became narrower, scaffolds spinning closer to the walls, random sparks leaping across gaps in overhead conduits, and John leaned forward suddenly, gesturing at a damaged pipe, explaining in an incoherent rush how the expansion valves were probably oscillating in some mathematically beautiful way he hadn’t had the energy to prove. Lilith shifted slightly, compensating for his weight, eyes scanning the next hundred meters for loose platforms, exposed wiring, or anything that could react to a misstep. She didn’t answer, didn’t correct him, but each subtle adjustment she made kept them alive, illustrating the quiet, ruthless efficiency of experience against the reckless, brilliant entropy of his mind.

Somewhere ahead, the station opened into a larger chamber where cargo lifts swung wildly and workers shouted over the roar of engines and sparks. John laughed and reached around to touch the base of her horns, remarking something about symmetry and aesthetic friction, entirely missing the danger of a loose support beam swinging just above their heads. Lilith moved with imperceptible precision, ducking under the beam, accelerating just enough to avoid collision, while calculating the path for every subsequent hazard in the immediate vicinity. Her silence was absolute, but the way she navigated the chaos spoke louder than any words could, and for John, it was just another absurd amusement, the world bending and colliding around them while he prattled on about tolerances, flow rates, and the unnecessary elegance of the station’s maintenance architecture.

The ride was a blur, spinning scaffolds and industrial scaffolds flashing past, neon reflections dancing in his half-closed eyes, her silhouette perfectly controlled in the seat ahead, and for a moment the station itself seemed to fold around them, chaos and precision locked in an unspoken, deadly ballet.








The station finally receded behind them, a kaleidoscope of sparks, scaffolds, and smoke dissolving into the distance as Lilith guided the hover-bike through the last stretch of tunnel toward the docking bay where John’s ship waited, low and unassuming among a row of industrial haulers. In his mind, it was simply unnecessary, intrusive, a device that had outlived its utility, nothing more. Lilith sat perfectly still for a moment, the space around her suddenly unbound, edges of her vision unmarked by the familiar restraint she had known her whole life. The collar’s neural functions were fully disabled—no pain, no shock, no automatic control—and yet she did not move. Her hands flexed once, twice, testing, cautious, expecting the invisible leash to snap her back. Nothing happened. The ship’s interior remained neutral, silent but precise, humming faintly like the heartbeat of a patient observer. She could move freely, but the freedom was a disorienting weight, terrifying in its novelty, and she paused, scanning the room, every vent, console, and shadow cataloged and assessed.

John slept, drifting between moments of clarity and deep oblivion, muttering softly about tolerances and the absurdity of gravity conduits, entirely unaware of the magnitude of what had just occurred. Lilith’s eyes followed him, calculating, measuring, noting flaws and intentions she could barely read, and somewhere in that stillness, a choice formed—not gratitude, not loyalty, but pure survival logic. Stay or leave. Every risk, every variable considered. The decision was hers, and for the first time, no one could override it.

She rose with the quiet grace of a trained body, testing her limbs, her boundaries, and the space around her, noting that the ship would not intervene, would not punish. And in that calm, almost sterile interior, amidst the hum of circuits and air, she took the first, careful steps into a freedom that had never existed before, every movement measured, deliberate, alive.







Lilith remained still for a long moment, letting the ship hum around her, her fingers brushing absentmindedly along the smooth surface of the corridor wall as if testing whether it would suddenly bite back, whether some invisible leash would snap her into obedience again.
