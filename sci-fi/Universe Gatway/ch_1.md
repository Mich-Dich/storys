Just another star system.
A pale yellow sun burns quietly in the distance, its light illuminating a vast region of empty space where no planets orbit and no asteroids drift.
But something interrupts the starlight.
Directly beneath us.
Lilith’s eyes move downward.
For a few seconds she does not understand what she is looking at.
Her mind searches automatically for familiar categories—planet, station, orbital ring, artificial habitat—anything that might explain the enormous structure occupying the inner system.
None of them fit.
Because the thing below us is too large.
The cross-section alone stretches across space like a fragment of a moon, a colossal wall of dark metallic structure and glowing energy channels that curves gently away from our position until it disappears into the distance.
Lilith slowly turns her head.
The structure continues.
And continues.
And continues.
Her gaze follows the arc of it across the system, but the curvature is so vast that she cannot see the full circle. The ring simply fades into the far horizon of the system, a continuous band of impossible engineering wrapping itself around the star like a boundary drawn by someone with a truly excessive sense of scale.
The inner surface of the structure glows faintly with slow-moving rivers of energy.
Gigantic stabilization pylons extend inward toward the star, each one the size of a planetary ring habitat, anchoring the structure against the gravitational pull of the system’s sun.
We hover above the ring’s outer surface.
Even from this distance the scale is overwhelming.
The structure beneath us is easily the size of a small moon in thickness, layered with kilometers of machinery, energy conduits, and gravitational field projectors that disappear into its depths.
And that mass continues outward…
Around the entire solar system.
Lilith says nothing.
She simply stares.
I let her.
For a long time we drift in silence while she processes the sight.
She has already accepted that my domain is absurd. I can see that in the quiet resignation behind her eyes. Her mind has stopped trying to reject the impossible and instead focuses on understanding it.
But even acceptance has limits.
And the ring below us pushes directly against those limits.
Her gaze travels slowly along the structure again.
“…that cannot be a shipyard,” she murmurs quietly.
“No.”
“…it is too large to be a transportation network.”
“Correct.”
“…and it does not resemble any known megastructure.”
I smile faintly.
“That’s because it isn’t finished yet.”
Her head turns toward me again.
The metallic strands in her dark hair catch the light as they drift slowly through the thin atmosphere around us.
“…John.”
There is a tone in her voice now that suggests she is attempting to remain calm through sheer professional discipline.
“Yes?”
“What exactly am I looking at?”
I fold my arms and glance proudly down at the structure.
For a moment I simply enjoy the view.
It took a long time to build.
Several centuries, technically speaking.
Multiple Dyson spheres feed power into it from surrounding systems, and the gravitational stabilizers alone required the mass of three dismantled gas giants.
It is, without question, the largest single device I have ever constructed.
And that is saying something.
Finally I answer.
“That,” I say with obvious satisfaction, “is the doorway.”
Lilith blinks.
“…the doorway.”
“Yes.”
I gesture toward the vast inner surface of the ring, where immense energy collectors and field generators form a continuous lattice along the circumference.
“When it’s active, the ring will generate a stabilized dimensional aperture across the entire central region of the system,” I explain, my voice carrying the faint enthusiasm of someone describing a project they are particularly proud of. “Essentially it punches a controlled hole in spacetime large enough for stellar-scale traffic.”
Her eyes widen slightly.
“A hole to where?”
Now I grin.
“A different universe.”
Silence.
The kind of silence that arrives when someone’s last remaining assumptions about reality quietly give up and sit down.
Lilith looks back at the ring.
Then at the distant star inside it.
Then back at me.
“…you are serious.”
“Very.”
I push away from our position and drift slowly along the edge of the structure, letting her see the endless lines of machinery stretching across the ring’s surface.
“Universes form naturally all the time,” I continue casually. “Vacuum fluctuations, inflation pockets, spontaneous spacetime bubbles—most of them separate from their parent universe immediately after forming, but with enough energy and the right gravitational conditions you can hold the connection open long enough to stabilize a passage.”
Lilith follows me silently.
Her eyes move across the gigantic mechanisms embedded in the ring.
“This one,” I say, nodding toward the structure beneath us, “is tuned to a very promising candidate. Newly formed universe, still early in its cosmological development.”
Her attention sharpens instantly.
I notice.
The scientist inside her has finally found something that matters more than the shock.
“What do you mean by early?” she asks quietly.
I glance back at her.
“Fresh,” I reply.
Her breathing slows slightly.
“Stars are still forming,” I continue. “Galaxies are just beginning to organize. The physical constants are slightly different from ours, but stable enough to work with.”
Lilith stops drifting.
Her glowing amber eyes lock onto me with sudden intensity.
“…has it been explored?”
I laugh softly.
“No.”
Her voice becomes almost a whisper.
“…mapped?”
“Not even a little.”
For the first time since she entered my domain, the disbelief in her expression is replaced by something else entirely.
Wonder.
Because now she understands.
An entire universe.
Untouched.
Unmapped.
A cartographer’s dream so vast that it barely fits inside the concept of possibility.
And suddenly the scale of everything she has seen begins to make sense.
The star movers.
The impossible ships.
The planetary mind.
The ring.
Lilith slowly turns back toward the colossal structure surrounding the system.
“…you are not just building a machine,” she says quietly.
I smile.
“No.”
Her glowing eyes return to me.
“You are leaving.”
I nod slightly.
“Eventually.”
She studies me for a moment longer.
Then she asks the question that finally matters.
“…and you built all of this,” she says slowly, “just so you could explore what’s on the other side.”
I grin widely.
“Exactly.”










For a long time we simply drift above the ring.
The star at the center of the system burns quietly beneath the immense circular structure, its light flowing across the endless machinery that wraps around it like a boundary drawn by a civilization that never learned the meaning of moderation. Vast conduits pulse slowly along the ring’s surface, carrying enough energy to disturb the geometry of spacetime itself once the system is finally brought online.
Lilith does not speak.
Her eyes move slowly along the curve of the structure, tracing the impossibly distant arc until it fades into the horizon of the system. Even now, after everything she has already seen, the scale still pushes against the limits of what her mind comfortably accepts.
I let the silence sit for a while.
Eventually she exhales.
“…how long,” she asks quietly, still looking at the ring, “have you been building this?”
“A while,” I answer.
“That is not a measurement.”
I shrug lightly.
“A few centuries, depending on how you count.”
Her head turns toward me.
The amber glow in her eyes flickers with the quiet recognition that the person floating next to her experiences time very differently from most living beings.
“…and when it is finished?”
“Then I leave.”
She nods slowly, as if confirming a thought she has already been forming.
Her gaze drifts across the stars surrounding the system.
“So everything you showed me…” she says thoughtfully, “…the ships, the shipyards, the planetary mind… all of it exists because you are preparing to go somewhere no one else can follow.”
“More or less.”
“And you are doing this alone.”
That one makes me smile faintly.
“I have been doing most things alone for quite a while.”
Lilith studies me for a moment.
Then she looks back toward the star inside the ring.
“You said the universe on the other side is unmapped.”
“Yes.”
Her voice grows quieter.
“…completely unmapped.”
“Completely.”
A long silence stretches between us again.
I can almost see the thoughts forming behind her eyes.
Cartographers spend their entire lives exploring the edges of known space, pushing slowly outward from one charted region to the next, always hoping to be the first person to place a new star on a map.
But the edges of the galaxy are still… part of the same map.
This would be something else entirely.
I decide it is time.
“Lilith,” I say gently.
She turns her head.
“Yes?”
I float a little closer, resting my hands casually in the pockets of my cargo shorts while the immense ring rotates slowly beneath us.
“I want to ask you something.”
Her expression sharpens slightly.
“What?”
I glance once more at the structure surrounding the system, then back at her.
“…would you like to come with me?”
The question settles into the quiet air between us.
Lilith does not react immediately.
Her eyes simply hold mine while the meaning of the words unfolds.
“Come with you,” she repeats slowly.
“To the new universe.”
She looks away again, staring out toward the distant stars.
“You are serious.”
“Very.”
She is silent for several seconds.
Then she asks the first practical question.
“…why me?”
“That one is easy,” I say.
I point vaguely toward the distant edge of my domain.
“Customs.”
Her expression shifts slightly.
“The predictive analysis system.”
“Right. The future-betrayal filter.”
I shrug.
“It checked you across every timeline it could meaningfully analyze. No matter what you do, no matter what I show you, no matter how much technology you gain access to… you never become a threat.”
Her gaze narrows thoughtfully.
“So you are confident that inviting me into something like this will not create a future problem.”
“Not just confident,” I reply. “Certain.”
She studies my face.
“And if I refuse?”
“That’s perfectly fine.”
I gesture lightly toward the stars.
“You go back.”
She blinks.
“…just like that.”
“Just like that.”
Her brow furrows slightly.
“You would simply allow me to return to my civilization after seeing all of this.”
“Of course.”
I wave one hand dismissively.
“I’ll give you a ship before you leave. Something comfortable. Something that actually deserves to be called a spacecraft.”
Her eyes flick briefly toward the shipyards we visited earlier.
“Any ship?” she asks carefully.
“Any ship you want,” I say. “Well… almost any.”
“The star movers.”
“Right. I need those relatively soon.”
She almost smiles.
Almost.
Then the weight of the conversation returns.
“And if I come with you,” she asks quietly, “what happens then?”
That part takes a little longer to explain.
“If you come,” I say slowly, “you’ll need some… upgrades.”
Her head tilts slightly.
“Upgrades.”
“Physical and cognitive modifications.”
I gesture vaguely toward the distant gray world that contains the larger half of my mind.
“The biological brain you’re currently using is excellent by evolutionary standards, but it’s not really designed for the sort of physics we’ll be dealing with.”
Her eyes narrow slightly.
“You are suggesting that I would need something similar to your planetary neural structure.”
“Not necessarily a whole planet,” I say with a small grin, “but something in that direction.”
“And the body?”
“That too.”
She watches me carefully as I continue.
“Longevity treatments. Structural reinforcement. A few adjustments to the nervous system so your consciousness can interface with expanded processing networks.”
Her voice becomes very quiet.
“You are describing immortality.”
“Close enough.”
Lilith looks away again.
The ring turns slowly beneath us.
“If you come with me,” I continue gently, “you won’t be limited the way you are now. You’ll be able to move through space the same way I do. You’ll understand the physics I’ve been working on. You’ll be able to build things like the ones you’ve seen today.”
Her gaze drifts toward the distant star-mover shipyards she witnessed earlier.
“…you are offering to make me something like you.”
“More or less.”
She considers that.
“And the cost?”
“That’s the important part.”
I pause.
Because this is the part people usually struggle with.
“If you cross the portal,” I say quietly, “you probably won’t come back here.”
Her head turns slowly toward me again.
“The connection between universes will only exist on this side,” I explain. “Once we pass through, we would need to build an entirely new gateway from the other side to return.”
She frowns slightly.
“That sounds difficult.”
“It is.”
I gesture toward the ring beneath us.
“This machine works because it’s tuned precisely to the physical constants of this universe. The one on the other side follows slightly different rules. We would need to rediscover the correct physics from scratch.”
Her amber eyes widen slightly.
“…how long would that take?”
I shrug.
“A long time.”
“How long.”
I think about it.
“Possibly several million years.”
The silence that follows is heavy.
“And that assumes it’s possible at all,” I add casually.
Lilith exhales slowly.
“So if I go with you…”
“…you leave this universe behind.”
She looks down at the glowing star inside the ring.
“My civilization.”
“Yes.”
“My work.”
“Yes.”
“The entire history of my species.”
“Yes.”
Another long silence settles between us.
Then I add the other side of the equation.
“But if you stay,” I say lightly, “you get a very nice ship.”
She glances back at me.
I grin.
“Pick anything except the star movers. Exploration cruisers, research vessels, personal stations—whatever you want. I’ll even throw in interior expansion fields so it feels like a small city inside.”
Her expression softens slightly despite herself.
“And then I return to my life.”
“Yes.”
“Continue mapping the edges of the galaxy.”
“Exactly.”
She folds her long arms slowly across her chest.
“…while knowing there is an entirely new universe somewhere beyond reach.”
I nod.
“More or less.”
Lilith goes quiet again.
The metallic strands in her dark hair drift slowly through the air while the enormous ring continues its patient orbit around the star.
Eventually she speaks again.
“…you said the customs system examined my future.”
“Yes.”
“And it determined that no matter what choice I make here… I will never become a threat to you.”
“Correct.”
She looks at me very carefully.
“Did it say anything about which choice I make?”
I laugh softly.
“No.”
Her eyes glow faintly in the starlight.
“That,” she says quietly, “would have been useful.”
I shrug.
“Where would be the fun in that?”











For a while after our conversation above the ring, Lilith does not make any decision at all.
Instead, she asks if there is somewhere… quieter.
Somewhere that does not involve structures the size of planets or machines that bend entire star systems into tools.
I understand immediately.
So I bring her home.
The garden orbiting my primary habitat is not particularly large by the standards of my domain, but after the things she has just seen it feels almost peaceful in its simplicity. Warm air drifts lazily beneath a transparent dome of reinforced crystal, carrying the scent of unfamiliar flowers and damp soil. Artificial sunlight filters through layered atmospheric fields that keep the temperature pleasantly stable while the distant stars shimmer quietly beyond the curved horizon of the habitat.
Lilith stands very still when she first steps onto the grass.
Her long silver-blue toes sink slightly into the living surface, and for a moment she simply looks down at it as though the concept of a natural landscape existing inside a structure built by someone who also manufactures star-moving ships requires a few seconds to reconcile.
Then she exhales slowly.
“…this is surprisingly normal.”
I shrug.
“I like plants.”
We spend the first afternoon walking.
Lilith asks questions.
At first they are small ones, the kind a scientist uses to approach something too large to examine directly.
How does the dimensional barrier work?
How large is your domain really?
How many Dyson spheres do you operate?
How do you power the ring?
The answers do not help her sanity much.
But they do help her understanding.
By the second day she starts asking different questions.
Not about the machines.
About me.
How long have you been alive?
How long have you been planning this?
Why did you start building the brain extension?
Did you always intend to leave this universe?
Some of those questions take longer to answer.
We sit beside a small artificial lake while we talk, the water reflecting the slow motion of orbital structures drifting far beyond the transparent dome. Lilith listens carefully, occasionally interrupting when something in the explanation touches a branch of physics she recognizes.
Her mind is quick.
Even without augmentation she already understands far more than most people would.
On the third day she asks to see the planetary brain again.
Not from a distance this time.
So I bring her closer.
We stand together on a small observation platform orbiting the gray world while vast fields of radiators and energy collectors stretch across its surface like geometric continents. Streams of power from the surrounding Dyson sphere feed constantly into the neural lattice beneath the crust.
She watches quietly.
“…and this is part of you,” she says.
“Yes.”
“And if I come with you… I would eventually have something similar.”
“Eventually.”
She nods slowly.
The thought does not seem to frighten her anymore.
It intrigues her.
Later that evening we return to the garden.
Lilith sits beside the lake for a long time without speaking.
The metallic strands in her dark hair drift gently in the warm air while she watches the artificial sunset slowly paint the sky in soft orange light.
Finally she says quietly,
“You knew I would struggle with this decision.”
“Yes.”
“Customs predicted it.”
“Very accurately.”
She smiles faintly.
“That machine seems rather unfair.”
“It’s extremely patient.”
The next day she asks about the universe beyond the portal again.
What do its stars look like?
Do its galaxies form the same structures as ours?
Will there be life eventually?
I answer as best I can.
Mostly with theories.
Because no one has ever gone there.
By the fourth day the conversation begins to change again.
She stops asking only about the future.
She starts asking about the cost.
“If I go,” she says one afternoon while we walk through a corridor of tall alien trees, “I will never see this galaxy again.”
“Probably not.”
“My species.”
“Yes.”
“My friends.”
“Yes.”
Her voice is very calm.
“You understand that I am leaving behind everything that defines my life.”
“I do.”
She stops walking.
Her glowing amber eyes study my face carefully.
“And you are comfortable with that.”
I think about the question for a moment.
Then I answer honestly.
“I’ve been comfortable with it for a long time.”
She nods slowly.
Later that evening she tells me about her parents.
They were planetary surveyors on a distant colony world near the edge of imperial space. She grew up watching starships leave the atmosphere and disappear into the black sky, listening to stories about places no one had ever seen before.
“Cartography felt inevitable after that,” she says with a small smile.
“And they’re gone now.”
“Yes.”
“No partner?”
She shakes her head.
“I spend too much time traveling for that to work well.”
The garden grows quiet again.
On the fifth morning she finds me sitting beside the lake with a cup of coffee.
She walks across the grass slowly and sits down beside me.
For a few minutes we simply watch the water ripple gently in the artificial breeze.
Then she speaks.
“I have made a decision.”
I set the cup down.
“Yes?”
Her voice is steady.
“I will come with you.”
I smile.
But she raises a hand before I can say anything.
“On one condition.”
“Fair.”
She looks out across the garden.
“I want to say goodbye.”
The words are soft but firm.
“I will not disappear from the lives of the people I care about without explanation.”
I nod immediately.
“Of course.”
Relief moves visibly through her posture.
“You understand?”
“Perfectly.”
She studies me for a moment.
Then she adds quietly,
“I know they will not fully understand what I am doing.”
“Probably not.”
“But they deserve the chance to hear it from me.”
“They do.”
Her gaze lifts toward the stars visible beyond the dome.
“I will need a ship.”
I grin slightly.
“I thought you might say that.”
Outside the habitat, space bends gently as one of my shipyards delivers something appropriate.
A sleek white vessel emerges from folded spacetime beside the garden’s docking platform, its elegant hull reflecting the distant starlight like polished stone.
Lilith stands and walks toward the observation window.
“…that looks like a private yacht.”
“It is.”
She turns back toward me.
“You are sending me across the galaxy in a luxury vessel.”
“It has the internal volume of a capital ship,” I say casually. “Standard expansion architecture.”
She shakes her head slowly.
“…of course it does.”
Then she grows thoughtful again.
“How long do I have?”
“The neural integration process takes about two years,” I explain. “Growing your first brain-extension lattice and safely linking it to your consciousness isn’t something we want to rush.”
She nods.
“And when do you plan to leave?”
“About five years,” I say.
Her eyes narrow slightly as she calculates.
“So that leaves…”
“Three years for you to travel.”
Lilith watches the waiting yacht for a moment.
Then she shakes her head.
“I won’t need that long.”
“No?”
“Six months.”
I raise an eyebrow.
“You’re certain?”
She smiles faintly.
“My friends are explorers. They understand strange decisions.”
Her glowing amber eyes meet mine again.
“I will return in half a year.”
I nod.
“I’ll be here.”
She turns toward the docking platform where the yacht waits quietly in the artificial sunlight.
Then she pauses and glances back one last time.
“John.”
“Yes?”
“Do not leave without me.”
I grin.
“Not a chance.”
