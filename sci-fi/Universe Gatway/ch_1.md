I had been ignoring the outer sensors for three hundred and twelve years.
That sounds irresponsible when phrased that way, but one must understand that when you own a private region of space containing one hundred and fifty stars, several million autonomous factories, three experimental realities, and a machine that occasionally invents new laws of physics when it gets bored, *something* must inevitably be placed in the “later” category.
The outer sensors were in that category.
They existed mostly so that nothing unpleasant—such as curious empires, wandering fleets, or extremely determined insurance auditors—would slam face-first into the dimensional boundary surrounding my domain. The wall itself handled most of that automatically, politely redirecting ships, asteroids, and the occasional misguided probe. The sensors merely informed me that such things had happened.
Usually I let the planetary brain extension deal with the reports.
At the moment, however, I was lying in a garden that orbited a perfectly average yellow star, watching a flock of alien birds argue with each other over a bowl of luminous fruit, while a small portion of my mind was trying to calculate whether vacuum energy could be persuaded to inflate *sideways* rather than outward.
It was peaceful.
Then the notification arrived.
Not in my ears, and not on a screen. The message appeared as a quiet thought that was not originally mine, gently inserted by the neural link that connected my biological brain to the much larger, much more patient intelligence housed inside the artificial planet drifting several thousand kilometers below my garden.
**External contact detected.**
I sighed.
Of course it would happen today.
The thought expanded as the planetary mind provided additional details, each piece of information unfolding across my awareness like pages turning inside a very large book.
A ship had arrived at the dimensional boundary.
Small.
Survey class.
Human construction, though heavily modified with imperial navigation hardware.
That was mildly interesting. The Intergalactic Empire tended to stay well away from this region of the Milky Way, largely because several centuries ago I had purchased the surrounding stellar real estate through a combination of legal contracts, economic incentives, and the subtle implication that anyone arguing too loudly about property rights might discover their local star briefly reclassified as experimental infrastructure.
Still, curiosity was healthy.
I let a greater fraction of my awareness drift into the sensor network.
Far beyond my garden world, beyond the swarms of construction satellites and the luminous halos of Dyson collectors wrapped around patient stars, the dimensional barrier curved invisibly through space like a wall made of mathematics. It was not a physical structure so much as a region where spacetime had been persuaded to politely refuse entry to anything that had not been specifically invited.
From the outside it looked like nothing at all.
From the inside it looked like a perfectly reasonable precaution.
The survey ship approached cautiously, its engines producing the delicate distortions typical of modern gravity drives, pushing against spacetime rather than burning fuel like the charmingly primitive rockets of humanity’s early centuries. Its hull carried the long sensor antennae and probe launchers favored by cartographers—those stubborn explorers who continued mapping the galaxy long after most people had decided it was already sufficiently mapped.
I watched as the ship slowed.
Then stopped.
Then nudged forward another few kilometers.
The moment its bow touched the boundary, the dimensional wall reacted. Space around the ship folded slightly, like fabric being smoothed by invisible hands, and the vessel drifted back a safe distance.
The ship tried again.
And again.
I could practically feel the pilot’s confusion radiating through the sensor readings.
The planetary brain offered a helpful summary.
**Probability assessment: vessel believes it has encountered an unknown spatial anomaly.**
“That’s technically correct,” I murmured to the birds.
They ignored me and continued arguing.
The ship launched a probe.
A small silver cylinder slipped from its hull and accelerated toward the barrier, broadcasting telemetry as it went. The probe crossed the final kilometer—
—and vanished.
Not destroyed.
Not deflected.
Simply… removed from the permitted set of objects that were allowed to exist on the outside of my domain.
The probe reappeared safely inside, where a polite automated system collected it for analysis.
The survey ship, however, had no way of knowing that.
Several seconds passed.
Then the ship launched three more probes at once.
I sat up in the garden.
“Well,” I said to nobody in particular, “that’s enthusiasm.”
Inside my mind, the planetary intelligence was already analyzing the vessel in microscopic detail: hull composition, engine signatures, communication patterns, crew life-support cycles. With only a small fraction of its processing capacity, it reconstructed the ship’s internal layout and the likely psychological state of its crew.
The pilot, it concluded, was frustrated.
The mission commander was curious.
The cartographer responsible for the expedition was currently staring at the sensor data with the kind of focused disbelief normally reserved for people discovering that gravity had taken the afternoon off.
Her name, according to intercepted internal communications, was Lilith Veyra.
She ordered the ship closer.
I watched as the vessel drifted forward again, its scanners probing the region where my dimensional boundary curved silently through the void. From her perspective, the readings must have looked bizarre: gravity behaving correctly, quantum noise perfectly normal, spacetime geometry suspiciously *stable* in a way that suggested deliberate engineering.
She launched another probe.
This one carried more advanced instruments.
It reached the barrier.
Disappeared.
Reappeared inside my domain.
The probe’s camera activated for exactly one frame before the collection system scooped it out of space.
That frame was transmitted back to Lilith’s ship.
Even at a distance of several light-seconds, my sensors captured the signal.
The image showed a star.
More specifically, it showed a star surrounded by a vast halo of orbiting structures—millions of energy collectors glittering in the sunlight like metallic dust.
A Dyson swarm.
There was a long pause inside the survey vessel.
Then someone said something that the ship’s microphones rendered as a short, very thoughtful silence followed by a quiet:
“…That shouldn’t be there.”
I leaned back in the garden and smiled.
After three hundred years of peaceful experimentation, quiet construction, and the gentle manipulation of physical law across one hundred and fifty privately owned star systems, it appeared that someone had finally noticed the wall.
Curiosity, it seemed, had arrived at my door.










For several minutes I continued watching the little survey ship hover uncertainly outside the dimensional boundary, its engines pulsing in cautious bursts as its pilot attempted to understand why the universe had suddenly decided to behave like a locked door.
It was charming.
Unfortunately, charm has a very short lifespan when one is in the middle of trying to invent a new method for creating universes.
A thought tugged gently at the edge of my attention.
Not from the sensors this time, but from the colossal flow of calculations currently occupying the vast majority of my planetary brain extension. For the past seventy-two years I had been wrestling with a particularly stubborn detail in the construction of the Universe Seed Engine—specifically the irritating question of how to control the direction of cosmic inflation once a new spacetime bubble was created.
Creating a universe, as it turned out, was surprisingly straightforward.
Keeping it from drifting away into unreachable higher-dimensional topology was… less cooperative.
Until that moment I had been attempting to stabilize the gateway using dark matter scaffolding and reality anchors.
But now, watching the little ship nudge experimentally at the boundary of my domain, a different possibility quietly unfolded in my mind.
What if the inflation field didn’t need to be restrained?
What if it could simply be *tilted*?
The idea opened like a door into a much larger room.
Equations rearranged themselves. Simulations bloomed into existence across stellar processors. Entire theoretical universes began forming and collapsing inside the computational depths of the artificial planet below my garden.
Ah.
Now *that* was interesting.
The survey ship outside the wall immediately became far less important.
Without particularly thinking about it, I sent a brief instruction to the outer systems.
“Handle the visitor politely,” I murmured, mostly to the nearby birds who were currently arguing about fruit. “Standard rejection protocol.”
The planetary intelligence acknowledged the command with its usual calm patience.
Beyond the dimensional boundary, the survey ship launched another probe.
The probe vanished.
Inside my domain, the **Dimensional Customs Gate** quietly collected it.
Customs was one of my more sensible inventions.
The station floated just inside the dimensional wall, a machine roughly the size of a small moon built almost entirely from sensor arrays and probability analyzers. Every object attempting to cross the boundary was examined not only in the present moment but across a wide spectrum of possible futures.
The system checked for weapons.
It checked for hidden artificial intelligences.
It checked for biological hazards, unstable physics signatures, parasitic nanomachines, time travelers, and several categories of problems that had been discovered through unfortunate experience.
Most importantly, it checked whether any version of the visitor might eventually become inconvenient.
If so, entry was denied with extreme politeness.
The probes from the survey ship failed this process repeatedly, which was hardly surprising. Not because they were dangerous—although one did contain an extremely enthusiastic scanning laser—but because allowing random cartographic equipment into a private megastructure cluster would almost certainly lead to bureaucratic questions later.
And I despise bureaucratic questions.
Outside the wall, the lone ship continued its investigation.
Inside my mind, the inflation problem grew more fascinating by the second.
If the inflation energy were tilted relative to the dimensional throat…
No.
Not tilted.
Rotated along the higher-dimensional axis—
Yes.
That might work.
Hours passed quietly while the galaxy continued doing its usual things: stars burned, factories constructed objects the size of continents, and one of my stellar brain lattices briefly reported that a simulation had accidentally developed a religion based entirely on differential equations.
Then my AI companion interrupted.
The message slipped neatly through the layers of calculation occupying my attention.
**John.**
I paused mid-equation.
“Yes?”
**A visitor has entered the domain.**

“…I’m sorry, what?”
**A visitor has been granted entry clearance.**
Now that was unusual.
In the three centuries since I had installed the Dimensional Customs Gate, the system had rejected approximately eight million objects, including diplomatic envoys, smugglers, research drones, two curious alien whales, and one extremely persistent tourist.
“Who authorized that?” I asked.
**The customs gate itself.**
That made me sit up.
The AI expanded the report.
**Probability analysis across all projected timelines indicates with overwhelming certainty that the individual designated Lilith Veyra represents no threat to you, your projects, or the long-term stability of this domain.**
I blinked slowly.
“You’re telling me,” I said carefully, “that out of the entire galaxy we’ve finally found someone who passes the future-betrayal filter?”
**Correct.**
“Well that’s fantastic.”
I stood.
Far beyond the garden world, the ship had just crossed the dimensional boundary and was drifting cautiously into my domain, its sensors undoubtedly screaming in confusion as the interior of the region revealed itself.
Stars surrounded by Dyson swarms.
Megastructures orbiting in impossible formations.
Wormhole highways stretching between systems like threads of light.
The pilot of the ship—Lilith Veyra, apparently—was completely alone inside the vessel.
Which made sense.
The customs gate had only approved *her*.
Everyone else would have been rejected.
I grinned.
“Well,” I said, “I suppose I should greet the first successful visitor in three hundred years.”
Most people would have used a ship.
Or at least a space suit.
But both seemed unnecessarily formal.
So I stepped off the garden platform and fell into space.
Technically I wasn’t falling.
The moment my feet left the artificial gravity field, the planetary brain extension shaped a corridor of distorted spacetime ahead of me, accelerating me smoothly away from the habitat.
The atmosphere vanished behind me.
Stars stretched slightly as the acceleration increased.
My Hawaiian shirt fluttered dramatically.
The flip-flops were perhaps not the most conventional equipment for faster-than-light travel, but I have always believed that comfort is an underrated engineering principle.
Within moments the gravitational corridor folded space ahead of me, and I crossed millions of kilometers with the casual ease of someone strolling down a hallway.
The survey ship appeared ahead.
Small.
Graceful.
And currently drifting through one of the quieter star systems inside my domain.
I slowed and came to a gentle stop beside the vessel.
Through the forward observation window I saw her.
Lilith Veyra stood alone in the cockpit, staring at the surrounding star system with the intense focus of someone trying very hard to remain rational while confronted with something that was clearly not.
She was taller than most humans, with long, elegant proportions suggesting a species accustomed to slightly lower gravity. Her skin held a soft silver-blue tone that shimmered faintly in the ship’s interior lighting, and delicate darker markings curved along her temples and down the sides of her neck like natural patterns rather than decoration.
Her hair—dark as interstellar space with thin metallic strands woven through it—floated lightly around her head in the ship’s reduced gravity.
And her eyes, a bright amber color that almost seemed to glow, widened dramatically the moment she noticed me hovering outside her ship.
Which was fair.
From her perspective, a man wearing cargo shorts, flip-flops, and a very enthusiastic Hawaiian shirt had just arrived outside her spacecraft by flying through interstellar space.
I waved.
She stared at me for several seconds.
Then she moved with quick, professional efficiency toward the airlock.
Moments later the outer hatch opened, and she stepped into the chamber surrounded by a faint personal force field that protected her from the vacuum.
I waited politely.
The inner door closed behind her.

The moment I crossed the threshold the internal gravity field caught me, and I landed lightly on the deck with the quiet slap of flip-flops against metal flooring.
Lilith stood a few steps away, watching me with a mixture of suspicion, curiosity, and what I suspected was a rapidly growing headache.
Up close, the silver-blue tint of her skin was even more striking, and the faint markings along her temples shifted slightly when she moved, like living patterns responding to emotion.

Slowly.
Very slowly.

“Hi,” I replied cheerfully.

“Would you happen to know anything about *that*?”

“Oh,” I said. “Yes, that’s mine.”
She blinked once.
Behind the scenes, my AI companion supplied additional analysis.
**Subject evaluation: Lilith Veyra. Cartographer, Intergalactic Empire Exploration Corps. Highly disciplined. Intellectually curious. Displays strong ethical consistency across projected timelines.**
Interesting.
**Psychological observation:** elevated heart rate detected.

**Reminder: subject Lilith Veyra approved for unrestricted access by dimensional customs.**
Right.

The faint silver-blue tone of her skin caught the starlight beautifully, and the dark patterns along her temples shifted subtly as her expression changed—something between irritation and intense curiosity.
Her amber eyes, however, remained locked on me with the unwavering focus of a professional scientist who had just discovered that the universe was hiding something enormous.
That was a good look.
Curiosity was rare.
And the customs gate had already confirmed something even rarer.
Safe.

It meant she might be able to come with me.

Across the floor.
Up the walls.
Through the control panels.
Across the cockpit window.

A long silence passed.
Finally she said quietly,
“…I’m going to pretend you didn’t just casually offer me a warship the size of a moon.”
“That’s probably wise.”
I turned away from the system.
“Come on.”
She blinked.
“Where are we going now.”
“I want to show you something else.”
The gravitational corridor formed again around us, and space folded softly as the shipyard system stretched into streaks of light behind us.
Lilith looked over her shoulder while we accelerated away.
“…I still don’t understand any of that.”
“That’s fine,” I said. “You don’t need to.”
“Why not.”
“Because that was the boring part.”
She stared at me.
“…The *boring* part.”
The stars snapped back into place a few seconds later.
The system ahead looked strangely empty.
There was a star—large, pale blue, and burning with the quiet intensity of a young stellar giant—but unlike the previous systems there were no Dyson swarms, no construction fleets, no massive orbital habitats.
Just a single structure orbiting far from the star.
Lilith frowned slightly.
“That’s… smaller.”
It was still enormous.
But compared to the shipyard it felt almost delicate.
The structure resembled a vast circular frame, several thousand kilometers across, composed of interlocking rings that rotated slowly around a central sphere of dark, glass-like material.
Thin beams of light stretched between the rings, forming a shifting geometric lattice around the core.
“…What does that do,” Lilith asked.
I smiled faintly.
“That,” I said, “is a Horizon Seeder.”
She looked at the machine again.
“A what.”
Before I could answer, my AI companion spoke quietly inside both of our translation fields.
**The Horizon Seeder is a large-scale cosmological preparation device.**
Lilith blinked.
“…That did not clarify anything.”
We drifted closer.
As we approached, the scale of the structure became more apparent. Each rotating ring was wide enough to contain entire cities, and the dark sphere at the center absorbed light in a way that made it appear strangely flat, as if depth itself refused to exist inside it.
Lilith studied the sphere carefully.
“…That’s not a black hole.”
“No.”
“But it looks like one.”
“Similar concept,” I said.
She glanced at me.
“You built an artificial black hole.”
“Technically it’s more of a controlled horizon generator.”
Her eyes narrowed slightly.
“That sentence is worse.”
The AI continued calmly.
**The device generates localized event horizons and uses them to manipulate spacetime boundary conditions.**
Lilith stopped drifting.
“…Boundary conditions of what.”
“The universe,” I said.
She stared at me.
“You’re adjusting the *boundary conditions of the universe.*”
“Not this one,” I said quickly. “Just… potential ones.”
Her gaze moved slowly back to the machine.
The rings continued their slow rotation while beams of energy passed between them, occasionally dipping into the surface of the dark sphere and vanishing entirely.
“…What is it for.”
I drifted a little closer to the structure, watching the shifting geometry of the horizon field.
“Well,” I said casually, “when you’re exploring extremely young regions of spacetime, the laws of physics can be a bit… unstable.”
She turned her head slightly.
“Unstable how.”
“Oh, things like inconsistent gravity constants, fluctuating particle masses, random dimensional overlap—little annoyances like that.”
Lilith stared.
“…Those are not little annoyances.”
“They are if you prepare properly.”
She gestured toward the enormous device.
“And that prepares things.”
“Yes.”
The AI added helpfully:
**The Horizon Seeder projects a stabilizing field into nearby spacetime volumes, encouraging the formation of consistent physical constants and preventing catastrophic cosmological collapse.**
Lilith blinked again.
“You’re… stabilizing the laws of physics.”
“Only temporarily.”
“For what.”
I shrugged lightly.
“You never know when you might want to visit somewhere that doesn’t exist yet.”
The words hung quietly in the air between us.
Lilith looked back at the machine.
Then at me.
Then back at the machine again.
The dark sphere at the center of the device shimmered slightly as another wave of energy passed through it, briefly distorting the surrounding stars like reflections on moving water.
“…This machine,” she said slowly, “is designed to make *new regions of spacetime habitable.*”
“Something like that.”
Her eyes narrowed slightly as she studied my face.
“You’re not just exploring.”
I smiled faintly.
“Curiosity takes many forms.”
For a moment she didn’t respond.
Then she looked back at the impossible machine orbiting the star.
“…You’re preparing for something.”
I tilted my head slightly.
“Maybe.”
The Horizon Seeder continued its silent work in the darkness, gently shaping the edge of possibility itself.
