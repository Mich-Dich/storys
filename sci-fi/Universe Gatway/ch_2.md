# Chapter 2

Six months pass faster than they should.
When your mind extends across a planet-sized lattice of thought and a network of machines that quietly maintain a few hundred stars, time has a tendency to collapse into something strangely compact, like a book whose pages turn too quickly once you start reading it.
Still, I notice the moment it happens.
Far out at the edge of my domain, where the dimensional barrier curves invisibly around the hundred and fifty stars I claimed as my personal backyard, something approaches.
A ship.
The information reaches me the same way all things reach me now—not through alarms or screens or blinking notifications, but as a quiet awareness forming somewhere within the vast extended territory of my mind.
The **Dimensional Customs Gate** has noticed something.
Customs, being the absurdly paranoid quantum computer that I designed it to be, immediately begins doing what it always does: scanning the approaching vessel across every measurable timeline, examining not only what the ship currently contains but what it might contain in a hundred years, in a thousand years, in ten thousand possible variations of causality.
It checks for weapons.
It checks for hidden artificial intelligences.
It checks for future betrayals.
It checks whether some distant version of the visitor might someday become a problem.
Usually this results in the ship being politely refused entry and redirected away from my barrier with a small automated message suggesting that the rest of the universe is perfectly nice and they should probably try exploring that instead.
But this time…
Customs does something different.
It pauses.
Which, for a machine capable of calculating several trillion potential futures per second, is a surprisingly dramatic gesture.
Then it opens the gate.
The dimensional barrier parts silently.
A colossal circular aperture unfolds in spacetime, large enough to swallow entire fleets, the edges glowing faintly as layered gravitational fields fold the surrounding vacuum into a stable passage.
And through that opening glides a ship I recognize immediately.
The white yacht.
I lean back slightly where I’m floating above the quiet lake in my orbital garden, watching the distant event unfold through the countless sensor nodes threaded throughout my territory.
“Well,” I murmur to nobody in particular, “that answers that.”
For a brief moment—just a very small moment—I had wondered if she would actually return.
Not because I doubted her character.
Lilith had struck me as someone unusually honest the moment she drifted out of that little survey craft and stared at my shipyards like a scientist discovering that reality had quietly broken while she wasn’t looking.
But promises are easy when they involve leaving an entire universe behind.
Actually doing it tends to be… complicated.
Apparently not complicated enough.
The yacht slips through the portal with elegant ease, its sleek hull reflecting the starlight of the outer systems as the barrier quietly seals itself behind it.
Then the ship begins the long glide inward.
It passes Dyson spheres.
It passes shipyards large enough to build things that terrify entire civilizations.
It passes the star movers resting between assignments like patient mechanical giants waiting for their next gravitational puzzle.
And eventually it reaches the garden.
I drift lazily above the water while the vessel settles onto the docking platform just beyond the transparent curve of the habitat dome, its landing struts touching the surface with a soft mechanical sigh.
For a few seconds nothing happens.
Then the airlock opens.
Lilith steps out.
At first glance she looks exactly the same.
She is still slightly taller than most humans, her long elegant proportions shaped by the lower gravity of her homeworld. Her silver-blue skin catches the warm artificial sunlight filtering through the dome, and the darker markings along her temples curve downward across her neck like natural brushstrokes written by evolution rather than decoration.
Her hair—dark as the deep void between galaxies—floats lightly around her shoulders, thin metallic strands woven through it glimmering faintly as they catch the light.
And her eyes.
Those bright amber eyes still glow softly, almost luminous against the calm expression she wears as she steps onto the platform.
But she is different.
The change is subtle.
It isn’t physical.
It’s something quieter than that.
She moves with a calm certainty that wasn’t there before, the kind of quiet gravity that only appears after someone has spent a long time making a very large decision and has finally finished arguing with themselves about it.
She walks across the platform, passes through the airlock into the garden, and stops a few meters away from where I’m floating above the lake.
For a moment we simply look at each other.
Six months ago she arrived here by accident.
Now she has come back on purpose.
Finally she speaks.
“I kept my promise.”
Her voice is calm.
Steady.
As if she had rehearsed the sentence a thousand times somewhere among the stars before finally saying it here.
I smile and drift a little closer.
“I never doubted you would.”
Which is mostly true.
Just not completely.
There had been a small part of me—an annoyingly rational part—that had assumed the probability of her actually returning after spending half a year saying goodbye to an entire galaxy was… perhaps slightly lower than my optimism suggested.
Fortunately, that part of my mind has just been proven wrong.
Lilith studies me for a second, her glowing amber eyes narrowing slightly as if she suspects I am leaving out a small but relevant detail.
Then she smiles.
It’s a very small smile.
But it’s there.
“I think you doubted it a little.”
I laugh.
“Only statistically.”
She glances around the garden, taking in the quiet water, the gently swaying trees, and the warm sunlight that makes the entire place feel almost absurdly peaceful compared to the cosmic-scale engineering projects happening just a few systems away.
“It’s strange,” she says softly.
“What is?”
“I spent six months crossing the galaxy, saying goodbye to everything I have ever known… and somehow this place still feels familiar.”
I shrug.
“Good design.”
She looks back at me.
Then, after a short pause, she adds quietly:
“I came back.”










For a little while neither of us moves.
Lilith stands at the edge of the lake, her silver-blue reflection rippling gently across the water while the artificial sun of the garden drifts slowly toward afternoon, and I float a few meters away trying very hard not to stare at her in the way one stares at someone who has just successfully done something statistically improbable.
Eventually she turns and begins walking along the narrow path that circles the lake.
I follow.
The grass bends softly under her steps, and the warm air carries the faint scent of flowers that technically originate from four different planets and one moon, although the garden’s ecosystem has long since decided to treat that as a minor biological inconvenience.
For a while she says nothing.
Then she sighs quietly.
“Six months is a strange amount of time when you’re trying to say goodbye to an entire galaxy.”
“That does sound slightly inconvenient,” I reply.
She glances at me.
“That’s an understatement.”
We walk a little farther before she continues.
“I started with the easy ones,” she says, her long fingers loosely clasped behind her back as she moves along the path with that same quiet certainty I noticed earlier. “The friends who are currently stationed near the outer survey routes. People who already expect me to disappear into unexplored regions for long periods of time.”
“That seems practical.”
“It was,” she agrees. “Exploration officers are used to the concept of someone announcing they will be gone for ten years.”
Her glowing amber eyes flick briefly toward me.
“So when I told them I was leaving on a long expedition, most of them assumed I meant another mapping assignment somewhere unpleasantly far away.”
I smile slightly.
“And you didn’t correct them.”
“No.”
She pauses for a moment, watching the surface of the lake.
“I told the truth,” she adds after a second. “Just not the entire truth.”
“That’s usually the safest amount.”
A faint smile appears at the corner of her mouth.
“They asked where I was going.”
“And?”
“I said it was difficult to explain.”
I laugh softly.
“That is also technically true.”
She nods.
“Very.”
We walk beneath a line of tall, thin trees whose leaves glow faintly with bioluminescent patterns that shift slowly in the breeze.
“My closest friends were less convinced,” she continues.
“Oh?”
Lilith tilts her head slightly, remembering.
“They’ve known me too long. Apparently I behave differently when I’m planning something ridiculous.”
“That sounds like a useful diagnostic trait.”
“One of them actually said something that made the entire room go quiet.”
I raise an eyebrow.
“What did they say?”
She stops walking.
For a moment she simply watches the distant stars visible through the transparent dome of the habitat.
Then she repeats the sentence very softly.
“You’re not going somewhere…”
Her eyes shift toward me.
“…you’re leaving.”
The words hang in the air between us for a second.
I nod slowly.
“That’s an observant friend.”
“Yes.”
“Did you tell them they were correct?”
She shakes her head.
“No.”
Her expression is calm, but there’s a quiet weight behind the memory now.
“I couldn’t explain it,” she says. “Even if I tried, it would have sounded like madness. A private domain hidden behind a dimensional barrier. A man who builds Dyson spheres the way other people build research stations. A machine that checks whether someone will betray you in future timelines.”
She looks back toward the lake.
“And a ring that opens into another universe.”
“Fair point.”
She exhales slowly.
“So I told them I had found something extraordinary.”
“That’s accurate.”
“And that I was going to explore it.”
“Also accurate.”
Her bright amber eyes shift back to me again.
“I didn’t tell them that the expedition might last longer than the lifetime of their species.”
I consider that for a moment.
“Yes,” I say eventually. “That detail probably would have complicated the conversation.”
Lilith laughs quietly.
“Very.”
We start walking again.
“The last visit was the hardest,” she continues after a moment. “An old navigation instructor from the academy. He taught me my first stellar cartography course.”
“That sounds like someone who might notice things.”
“He did.”
She pauses, remembering the moment.
“When I finished explaining my ‘expedition,’ he looked at me for a very long time and then said something rather inconvenient.”
“Oh?”
Lilith glances sideways at me.
“He said: ‘Lilith, explorers usually look excited when they leave.’”
“And you didn’t?”
“I did,” she says.
Her smile grows a little.
“Apparently I also looked like someone who had just said goodbye to the universe.”
I wince slightly.
“That’s a difficult expression to hide.”
“Yes.”
We walk a little farther in comfortable silence.
Finally I ask the question that has been sitting quietly in the back of my mind since her ship crossed the barrier.
“And after all that,” I say, “after six months of conversations that must have ranged from mildly confusing to deeply suspicious…”
I look at her.
“…you still came back.”
Lilith stops again.
This time she doesn’t look at the lake or the trees or the distant stars.
She looks directly at me.
Her amber eyes glow softly in the warm garden light.
“Yes,” she says simply.
Then, after a brief pause, she adds with a calm certainty that feels heavier than the words themselves:
“I did.”








We stand there for a moment after she says it.
Yes.
Such a small word for a decision that effectively involves abandoning an entire universe.
I drift slowly down until my feet touch the grass beside her, mostly out of habit. Technically I no longer need to stand on things—gravity is more of a polite suggestion at this point—but I have discovered that conversations feel more comfortable when both participants obey at least a few traditional laws of physics.
For a while we walk again along the path circling the lake.
The conversation stretches easily, almost surprisingly so, as if the six months she spent crossing the galaxy were merely a long pause between sentences rather than a separation across thousands of light-years.
Lilith asks questions.
Many questions.
Some of them practical.
“What happens first?” she asks while we pass beneath a cluster of low branches shimmering with faint blue leaves.
“The biological upgrades,” I reply. “Your body needs to be prepared before we connect it to a brain extension. Otherwise the experience would be… unpleasantly overwhelming.”
She raises an eyebrow.
“That sounds like a careful way of saying catastrophic.”
“It’s a polite euphemism, yes.”
She nods thoughtfully.
“And after that?”
“Then we grow your neural seed,” I explain. “A small external cognitive lattice that will link directly to your mind and gradually expand your capacity to handle… larger ideas.”
Her amber eyes narrow slightly.
“How large?”
“Well,” I say casually, “eventually you’ll need to think comfortably about moving stars.”
She considers that.
“…right.”
Then she asks different questions.
Questions about how my own transformation felt the first time.
What it is like to think across a planetary surface.
Whether consciousness changes when it becomes large enough to occupy an astronomical object.
Those questions take longer to answer.
We sit beside the lake while I try to explain something that does not translate easily into ordinary language—that strange moment when your mind expands beyond the fragile boundaries of biology and suddenly the universe feels… smaller.
Lilith listens carefully, occasionally leaning forward with the quiet intensity of a scientist who realizes she is being offered a preview of physics that does not exist anywhere else.
At some point she laughs.
Not loudly, but with genuine amusement.
“I spent my entire career mapping the unknown,” she says, shaking her head slightly. “And somehow I ended up accepting a job that involves leaving the map entirely.”
“That’s usually where the interesting parts begin.”
The artificial sun begins drifting lower toward the horizon of the garden dome.
The light turns softer.
Warmer.
We talk about smaller things after that.
Her ship.
The places she visited during the past six months.
One small research station where three of her friends attempted to convince her to stay for a week-long celebration that slowly evolved into a mild interrogation about what she was actually planning.
“They assumed I had discovered some classified imperial project,” she says with quiet amusement.
“That’s flattering.”
“Yes,” she agrees. “Apparently I look like someone who occasionally steals secrets from governments.”
“Good posture helps.”
Eventually her voice slows slightly.
Her words come with small pauses between them.
Lilith rubs her eyes once, clearly trying not to appear tired.
I pretend not to notice for about thirty seconds.
Then I gesture lightly toward the far end of the garden where a small structure sits partially hidden behind a row of flowering trees.
“You should probably sleep.”
She opens her mouth to protest.
Then stops halfway through the sentence and sighs.
“Yes,” she admits reluctantly. “That might be wise.”
Standing again, she stretches slightly, her long arms rising above her head while her hair drifts gently in the warm evening air.
“Where?”
I point toward the building.
“Guest habitat. Pick any room you like.”
She studies me for a moment.
“You are surprisingly relaxed about letting strangers wander around your home.”
I grin.
“Customs already verified that every possible version of you across multiple timelines refuses to betray me.”
“That still feels unfair.”
“It’s extremely convenient.”
Lilith laughs softly and begins walking toward the building.
Halfway there she stops and looks back at me.
“John.”
“Yes?”
Her glowing amber eyes hold mine for a second.
“I’m glad you waited.”
I smile.
“I told you I would.”
She nods once, then disappears through the doorway.
A few seconds later the lights inside dim automatically as the habitat senses her presence and prepares a sleeping environment suited to her species.
The garden grows quiet.
I float upward again, rising slowly above the lake until the entire habitat spreads out beneath me like a peaceful island suspended in orbit.
Lilith sleeps.
And while she does, I prepare the medical systems she will need tomorrow.
Not the procedure itself.
Just the preparation.
Because the changes she is about to undergo are not something I would ever perform on someone who is unaware of what is happening.
By the time the artificial sun rises again inside the garden dome, everything is ready.
---
The next morning Lilith finds me waiting near the docking platform, floating a few meters above the ground while reviewing the final diagnostic projections for her upgrade process.
She approaches quietly, the early light catching in the metallic strands woven through her dark hair.
“You’re already working,” she says.
“I rarely stop.”
“That sounds unhealthy.”
“It would be, if my brain were still only the size of a melon.”
She stops beside me and looks out through the transparent dome toward the distant stars.
“So,” she says calmly, “today I become something unusual.”
“That’s one way to phrase it.”
Her amber eyes shift toward me.
“What exactly are you going to do to me?”
I gesture toward a structure just outside the garden habitat—a smooth white facility suspended above the nearby lake, its architecture simple and almost understated compared to the absurd machinery that fills the rest of my territory.
“That,” I say, “is a medical platform. Inside it we’ll perform the first stage of your transformation.”
Lilith studies the building.
“And I should assume this involves removing parts of me.”
“Yes.”
“How many parts?”
“Mostly the inefficient ones.”
She sighs softly.
“Of course.”
We drift toward the platform together.
As we enter the facility she pauses beside the central chamber—a wide circular room filled with quiet medical machinery whose surfaces glow faintly with soft blue diagnostic lights.
Lilith looks at the equipment.
Then at me.
“I would like to understand what is happening before we start.”
“That’s fair.”
So I explain.
“The first change is energy,” I begin. “Your body currently runs on chemical reactions—food, oxygen, water, the usual biological nonsense. It works, but it’s inefficient once you start doing things like traveling through vacuum or manipulating gravitational fields.”
She crosses her arms thoughtfully.
“So you’re replacing my metabolism.”
“Exactly.”
I gesture toward a projection forming above the chamber floor.
Her internal anatomy appears as a holographic model.
“The digestive system goes first,” I continue. “Your stomach and most of the surrounding organs will be replaced with an energy conversion core that draws power directly from my Dyson sphere network.”
Her eyes widen slightly.
“You’re powering me with a star.”
“A very tiny fraction of one,” I reassure her. “Less energy than a household appliance on most colony worlds.”
“That is still an absurd sentence.”
“It will feel completely normal.”
She studies the projection while I continue.
“Your lungs will also be replaced,” I say. “Respiration becomes unnecessary when your cells draw power directly from an external source. Instead you’ll have atmospheric processors that can extract useful elements from almost any environment—or simply shut down if you’re drifting through vacuum.”
Lilith looks up.
“So I can survive in space.”
“Yes.”
“Without a suit.”
“Yes.”
She nods slowly.
“That seems useful.”
Next I expand the projection to show her skeletal structure.
“A mechanical lattice will wrap around your spine,” I explain. “It links directly to your nervous system and allows you to manipulate local gravitational gradients for movement.”
She tilts her head slightly.
“You mean I’ll move through space the way you do.”
“Exactly.”
“No engines.”
“No engines.”
Her eyes brighten faintly.
“That sounds fun.”
“Very.”
I continue.
“Your muscles will be replaced with electromechanical fibers that function exactly like biological tissue but can produce far greater force.”
“So I’ll be stronger.”
“Much.”
Finally I zoom the projection toward the surface of her skin.
“A microscopic reinforcement mesh will grow beneath your dermal layer. It’s made from a carbon lattice far stronger than nanotubes—something a little more advanced than anything your civilization has developed so far.”
“And that does what?”
“It distributes force across your entire body.”
She considers that.
“How much force?”
I shrug.
“If a ship crashes into you, you’ll probably survive.”
Lilith stares at me.
“…probably?”
“I try not to make absolute promises.”
She laughs despite herself.
For a moment she simply looks at the chamber around us.
Then she exhales slowly.
“All right,” she says.
Her amber eyes meet mine again.
“Let’s begin.”










Lilith stands in the center of the medical chamber for a moment before the process begins.
There is a quiet stillness to the room that feels almost ceremonial, as if the machines themselves understand that what is about to happen is not merely a medical procedure but the deliberate rewriting of the limits that biology placed on a thinking being millions of years ago.
She looks around once, slowly.
The chamber is circular, its walls lined with smooth white structures that appear simple at first glance but are in fact clusters of microscopic fabrication systems capable of building or reshaping matter atom by atom. Above us the ceiling is transparent, allowing the pale light of the garden’s artificial sun to spill softly into the room.
Lilith flexes her fingers once.
Then she looks at me.
“You’ve done this before.”
“Several times,” I reply. “Although most people stop long before the point where they start installing planetary brain extensions.”
“That is strangely comforting.”
A circular platform rises slightly beneath her feet, forming a shallow cradle that adjusts itself to her body’s proportions.
Thin arcs of machinery extend outward from the walls, moving with quiet precision until they form a loose sphere around her.
She watches them for a moment.
Then she inhales slowly.
“All right,” she says. “I trust you.”
That sentence carries a surprising amount of weight.
I nod once.
The machines begin.
At first nothing dramatic happens.
The systems simply scan her.
Trillions of microscopic sensors pass through her body in waves, mapping every nerve, every cell cluster, every microscopic variation in her anatomy so the modifications can be performed without disturbing the structure of her mind.
Lilith closes her eyes briefly as the scan passes through her skull.
“Strange,” she murmurs.
“What does it feel like?”
“Like someone is reading a book inside my head.”
“That’s essentially correct.”
The first changes begin a few seconds later.
Her digestive system is removed first, just as I described earlier. The machines disassemble the biological tissue molecule by molecule, redirecting the raw material into storage while a compact crystalline structure grows in its place.
Lilith watches the projection hovering beside her as it happens.
“That’s my stomach,” she says.
“Formerly.”
“And now?”
“A stellar energy interface.”
The new structure finishes forming, a small geometric core embedded safely within her torso.
A faint shimmer passes across its surface as the connection opens.
Somewhere far beyond this system, one of my Dyson spheres adjusts its power routing by a fraction so small that the surrounding star does not even notice.
Lilith inhales sharply.
“What was that?”
“The first power connection.”
She frowns slightly.
“I felt it.”
“Your nervous system noticed the energy shift.”
“How much power am I drawing?”
I glance at the diagnostics.
“Roughly the same as a desk lamp.”
She stares at me.
“I am powered by a star.”
“Yes.”
“That sentence still feels ridiculous.”
“You’ll get used to it.”
Next the respiratory organs disappear.
Her lungs dissolve gradually while replacement structures form along her ribcage—thin networks of atmospheric processors capable of filtering gases, recycling trace elements, or shutting down completely when exposed to vacuum.
Lilith experiments with breathing for a moment.
Then she stops.
“You’re telling me I don’t actually need to breathe anymore.”
“You can if you want to.”
She considers that.
“…I’ll keep the habit. It feels polite.”
The skeletal work begins next.
The machines move more slowly here.
Her spine remains untouched—its natural connection to her nervous system is too important to replace entirely—but everything branching outward from it is gradually reinforced.
Her ribs shift first.
The biological bone dissolves and reforms into a layered composite structure grown from interlocking crystal fibers and metallic lattices far stronger than anything nature evolved.
Lilith watches the projection again.
“My skeleton is being replaced.”
“Most of it,” I confirm.
“Should I be worried about that sentence?”
“Not particularly.”
Her collarbones reform.
Then the bones of her arms.
The material looks almost organic in the projection, but its internal structure is closer to engineered diamond reinforced with flexible energy-absorbing layers.
Lilith slowly rotates one shoulder as the process finishes.
“That feels… exactly the same.”
“Good.”
“That seems suspicious.”
“The upgrades are not supposed to feel dramatic,” I say. “You’re still you.”
Her legs follow next.
The long bones restructure themselves with the same composite framework, strengthening every joint while maintaining the natural flexibility of her movement.
She carefully shifts her weight.
Then bends one knee experimentally.
“I think I just became extremely difficult to injure.”
“That was the goal.”
The muscle replacements begin immediately afterward.
Her biological fibers dissolve and are replaced by electromechanical structures that look nearly identical on the outside but contain microscopic actuator networks capable of producing vastly greater force.
Lilith flexes her fingers.
Then tightens one hand slowly.
Her eyes widen.
“I was trying not to move the air.”
“You’re currently exerting about five times your previous strength.”
She relaxes quickly.
“That will require practice.”
“Quite a lot of it.”
The spinal lattice comes next.
A delicate structure of articulated mechanical rings grows gradually along the outside of her spine, linking itself directly into the nerve pathways that control balance and movement.
When the connection activates, Lilith suddenly lifts a few centimeters off the platform.
She blinks.
“…I did not do that intentionally.”
“That’s the gravity interface,” I explain. “Your brain just discovered a new muscle.”
She focuses.
Very slowly she drifts back down.
Her expression is somewhere between fascination and mild alarm.
“That is going to take getting used to.”
“Yes.”
Finally the dermal reinforcement begins.
A microscopic mesh spreads beneath the surface of her silver-blue skin, weaving itself between the cells with impossible precision. The material forms an invisible lattice stronger than any conventional structural fiber, distributing force across her entire body.
Lilith watches the final diagnostic display appear above the chamber.
“That’s everything?”
“Almost.”
She looks at me.
“What else?”
I gesture toward her nervous system projection.
“Your sensory network just received a few upgrades.”
Her eyes narrow.
“What kind of upgrades?”
“Expanded visual range, improved neural signal processing, enhanced reaction time.”
She blinks once.
Then suddenly turns her head toward the far wall of the chamber.
“…I can hear the coolant pumps in the floor.”
“Yes.”
“And the wind outside the garden dome.”
“Yes.”
“And…”
She pauses.
“…the orbital shipyard several kilometers away.”
I smile.
“You’re thinking a little faster now.”
Lilith stands quietly for a moment while the last medical systems retract.
The chamber opens.
She steps off the platform slowly.
Then she looks down at her hands.
Flexes her fingers.
Lifts gently off the floor for a second before settling again.
Finally she looks at me.
Her glowing amber eyes are brighter than before.
“So,” she says.
Her voice is calm.
But there is a quiet excitement behind it now.
“What happens next?”














Lilith looks down at her hands again.
Flexes her fingers once more.
Then she lifts a few centimeters off the floor completely by accident.
She quickly settles back down and gives me a look that sits somewhere between curiosity and suspicion.
“So,” she says slowly, “you mentioned that my brain just discovered a new muscle.”
“That’s correct.”
“And that muscle allows me to manipulate gravitational gradients.”
“Yes.”
She folds her arms.
“And you expect me to simply… figure that out.”
I consider the question.
“Well,” I say after a moment, “we could schedule several months of theoretical training, develop a careful simulation program, and gradually ease you into the concept of gravitational locomotion.”
“That sounds reasonable.”
“Yes.”
We stand there for a second.
Then I gesture toward the door.
“Or we could do something faster.”
Her eyes narrow slightly.
“I am beginning to suspect that your definition of ‘faster’ may not be entirely safe.”
“Nonsense,” I say cheerfully. “Come on.”
A few minutes later we reach the outer edge of the garden habitat.
The dome ends here.
Beyond the curved transparent wall lies open space—an enormous sweep of stars, distant orbital structures, and the quiet glow of a Dyson sphere hanging far out in the system like a golden halo around its captive sun.
The garden platform extends outward slightly beyond the dome, forming a narrow observation deck suspended directly over the vacuum.
Lilith walks beside me until we reach the railing.
She looks out at the stars.
Then she looks down.
The platform floats several kilometers above the surface of a quiet blue ocean world that happens to occupy the center of this system.
She tilts her head slightly.
“Why are we here?”
“Training.”
She nods thoughtfully.
“That makes sense.”
I rest my elbows casually on the railing.
“The spinal lattice we installed allows you to generate localized gravitational distortions. Your brain already knows how to control muscles—this is basically the same process, just applied to spacetime instead of biological tissue.”
She listens carefully.
“The trick,” I continue, “is realizing that falling is optional.”
Lilith glances at the edge of the platform.
Then back at me.
“And how exactly do you propose I learn that?”
I smile.
“Well—”
And then I shove her.
Not violently.
Just a quick, efficient push.
Lilith’s expression goes through a fascinating sequence of emotional states in about half a second.
Confusion.
Shock.
Immediate betrayal.
Then she disappears over the edge of the platform.
For a brief moment there is silence.
Then, from somewhere below us—
“JOHN!”
I lean over the railing and watch.
Lilith is falling.
Not gracefully.
Not even remotely under control.
Her arms are flailing slightly while the ocean below grows larger at an alarming rate.
The gravitational interface is clearly trying to respond to her nervous system, but at the moment her nervous system is mostly busy screaming.
“Stop falling!” I call down helpfully.
“That is extremely unhelpful!”
She drops another hundred meters.
Then something changes.
The spinal lattice reacts.
A faint distortion ripples through the air around her as her brain finally manages to activate the field generators embedded along her spine.
Her fall slows.
Not smoothly.
More like a series of confused gravitational hiccups.
Down.
Stop.
Down again.
Then suddenly—
She stops.
Hovering about two hundred meters above the ocean.
For a moment she simply hangs there, breathing rapidly while the water below reflects the distant starlight.
Then her head slowly tilts upward toward the platform where I’m watching.
Even from this distance I can see the exact expression she’s making.
It’s the expression of someone who is currently reconsidering several aspects of a friendship.
I wave.
“You’re doing great!”
A few seconds pass.
Then she begins rising.
Slowly at first.
Then faster.
The gravitational fields around her stabilize as her brain starts figuring out the new control system, and soon she’s gliding upward toward the platform with slightly wobbly but steadily improving precision.
When she finally reaches the deck she lands lightly beside me.
Very lightly.
Too lightly.
Her feet barely touch the surface.
For a second she just stands there staring at me.
Then she says, very calmly,
“You pushed me off a cliff.”
“Yes.”
“I could have died.”
“Statistically unlikely.”
Her glowing amber eyes narrow.
“You are enjoying this.”
“Immensely.”
She exhales slowly.
Then she looks back over the edge of the platform.
After a moment she steps closer.
“Do it again,” she says.
“Oh?”
“This time I jump.”
She climbs onto the railing before I can respond.
Then she pauses.
Turns back toward me.
“If I crash into the ocean, I am blaming you.”
“Fair.”
She nods once.
Then she jumps.
This time the fall looks different.
Still chaotic.
Still slightly panicked.
But halfway down she stabilizes much faster than before, the gravitational distortions around her smoothing out into a controlled hover.
She laughs.
The sound carries clearly through the open air.
Then she shoots upward far too quickly, overshoots the platform by about twenty meters, spins slightly in midair, corrects herself, and drifts back down again.
I clap.
