Artificial sunlight slowly rose across the vast garden habitat, painting the rolling fields in warm gold. Morning mist drifted above the river as it wound lazily through the valley. Dew sparkled on the tall grass, and a gentle artificial breeze moved through the trees at the forest’s edge.
For the first time since their escape, the Lunari woke to something resembling peace.
Small groups had already begun shaping a temporary settlement near the riverbank. Some of the older Lunari carefully assembled simple shelters using modular materials Jake’s drones had delivered overnight. The construction was quick and strangely elegant—light structural frames that snapped together with soft magnetic clicks, walls forming from flexible composite sheets that sealed themselves once in place.
Others wandered farther into the fields, exploring cautiously at first, then with growing excitement. The habitat was enormous—far larger than any space they had ever been allowed to inhabit on the colony.
Near the edge of the forest, several children were running through the tall grass, laughing breathlessly as three bird-shaped maintenance drones swooped playfully overhead. The machines adjusted their flight patterns with surprising gentleness, occasionally diving just low enough that the children could try to catch them before gliding away again with soft metallic chirps.
The sound of laughter carried far across the valley.
Lucy paused on a small path overlooking the river.
She closed her eyes for a moment and simply listened.
Laughter. Conversation. The quiet rhythm of construction. The distant splash of someone discovering the river’s shallow banks.
For years her people had lived under constant supervision. Every movement monitored. Every conversation restricted. Even joy had been something dangerous—something that could draw unwanted attention from the overseers.
But here…
Here there were no guards.
No orders.
Just open sky.
Lucy’s tail swayed slowly behind her as she walked through the growing settlement. Several Lunari greeted her as she passed. Some bowed their heads respectfully; others simply smiled. She returned each greeting warmly.
She still wasn’t used to that either.
Leadership had once meant making sure everyone survived the next shift cycle.
Now it meant something else entirely.
Something hopeful.
As she moved through the camp, she noticed something missing.
Or rather… someone.
Jake.
Lucy looked around again, scanning the valley.
The children were playing.
Several Lunari were speaking with LYRA through floating holographic interfaces about agricultural soil composition.
A group near the river had started experimenting with fishing using improvised nets.
But Jake was nowhere to be seen.
Which was strange.
The human had a habit of appearing everywhere at once—dropping in to help, offering strange tools, or explaining some piece of technology that sounded impossible.
Lucy eventually spotted a faint trail leading toward one of the low hills that overlooked the valley.
She followed it.
The climb was gentle. Grass brushed softly against her legs as she reached the top.
And there he was.
Jake stood near the crest of the hill, one hand in his jacket pocket while the other held a steaming mug. The familiar smell of coffee drifted faintly through the air.
Floating in front of him was a large holographic display—dozens of star systems represented as glowing spheres suspended in a three-dimensional map. Lines of light connected them in complex patterns. Data streams scrolled along the edges of the projection.
Jake wasn’t moving.
He simply stood there quietly, staring at the map.
Lucy slowed her steps.
From a distance, he looked… different.
Not the relaxed, joking captain she had seen yesterday while introducing them to the garden habitat.
He looked thoughtful.
Focused.
Almost… concerned.
She approached slowly, her footsteps soft in the grass.
“Good morning,” she said gently.
Jake glanced over his shoulder.
His expression brightened instantly.
“Hey, Lucy. Morning.”
He lifted the mug slightly in greeting before turning back to the star map.
Lucy stepped beside him and looked out over the valley below.
The sight was beautiful from up here.
The river shimmered like a silver ribbon through the fields. Small structures were beginning to form near its banks. Lunari figures moved through the tall grass like tiny shadows beneath the rising artificial sun.
For a moment neither of them spoke.
Lucy’s gaze drifted back to the floating holographic display.
“Looking for something?” she asked.
Jake took a slow sip of his coffee before answering.
“Resources.”
He gestured casually toward several glowing points on the map.
“Metal-rich asteroids. Frozen volatiles. Exotic elements. That sort of thing.”
Lucy blinked.
“For what?”
Jake gave her a small grin.
“Well… building starships tends to require a lot of material.”
He rotated the holographic map with a flick of his fingers. Several nearby asteroid belts highlighted themselves in soft blue.
“I figured if you’re going to rebuild your civilization, we might as well start with a proper fleet.”
Lucy stared at the projection.
There were dozens of marked resource sites.
Mining estimates.
Transport calculations.
Construction schedules.
Her ears slowly tilted forward in realization.
“You started planning this already?”
Jake shrugged.
“Last night.”
Lucy looked at him again.
“You did all of this… overnight?”
“Well, technically LYRA did the heavy math,” Jake admitted. “I mostly just told her what I wanted.”
A faint voice drifted from the air beside them.
“Captain Jake Harrison finalized the preliminary industrial expansion plan at 02:14 ship time,” LYRA said calmly.
A small holographic avatar appeared near the star map.
Lucy blinked.
Jake groaned slightly.
“LYRA.”
“Yes, Captain?”
“You don’t have to announce the exact time.”
“I believed the additional detail provided useful context.”
Lucy couldn’t help the small laugh that escaped her.
Jake pointed at the AI with mock accusation.
“See what I deal with?”
LYRA’s avatar tilted its head.
“My function is to assist the captain in achieving his objectives as efficiently as possible.”
Lucy looked between them.
“And building ships for us is now one of those objectives?”
Jake took another sip of his coffee.
“Seems like the logical next step.”
Lucy watched him quietly for a moment.
“You are doing a lot for people you met less than a day ago.”
Jake didn’t answer immediately.
Instead, he stared out over the valley again.
At the Lunari settlement slowly growing beside the river.
At the children chasing drones through the tall grass.
His voice, when he finally spoke, was softer.
“Yeah.”
Another pause.
Then he added quietly,
“Someone should have done it sooner.”









Lucy looked again at the glowing star map floating in the morning air.
Dozens of systems surrounded them. Asteroid belts, moons, resource clusters—each highlighted with neat annotations and projected extraction yields.
The scale of the plan was difficult to grasp.
“You are searching for materials,” she said slowly.
Jake nodded and took another sip of coffee.
“Yep.”
Lucy studied the map again.
“For the ships you mentioned yesterday?”
“Exactly.”
He zoomed the projection outward slightly, revealing several additional systems connected by thin glowing travel routes.
Lucy tilted her head.
“How many ships are you planning to build?”
Jake shrugged casually.
“Not sure yet.”
Lucy waited.
Jake thought for a moment.
“Enough.”
That answer did not help.
Lucy tried again.
“You promised we would have ships capable of leaving this region.”
Jake nodded.
“Right.”
She gestured toward the map.
“But this level of planning seems… excessive.”
Jake looked mildly surprised.
“Excessive?”
Lucy folded her arms lightly.
“You spoke yesterday about providing usable ships.”
“Yes.”
Lucy pointed toward the map again.
“This looks like preparation for a fleet capable of colonizing entire star systems.”
Jake blinked once.
Then twice.
“…Yeah?”
Lucy stared at him.
“You said usable.”
Jake looked back at the map as if double-checking something obvious.
“Well… yeah.”
He gestured at the projection.
“I mean, if you’re going to give someone a ship, it should actually be able to do something.”
Lucy’s ears twitched.
“What exactly do you believe a ship should do?”
Jake answered immediately.
“Explore.”
He rotated the map again, zooming farther out.
Hundreds of stars appeared now.
“I want you guys to be able to go anywhere.”
Lucy frowned slightly.
“Anywhere?”
“Sure.”
Jake gestured broadly.
“Deep space. Uncharted systems. New galaxies eventually if you feel like it.”
Lucy stared at him.
Jake continued speaking as if describing a weekend road trip.
“The ships should be able to operate independently for a few thousand years. Self-sustaining habitats, industrial fabrication, research labs, the works.”
Lucy’s brain struggled to keep up.
“A few… thousand years?”
Jake nodded.
“Give or take.”
Lucy slowly turned toward him.
“…That is what you call usable?”
Jake looked genuinely confused.
“Yeah?”
Lucy laughed softly in disbelief.
“That is not usable.”
Jake tilted his head.
“What would you call it then?”
Lucy spread her hands slightly.
“Unrealistic.”
Jake raised an eyebrow.
“Why?”
Lucy gestured helplessly.
“My lifespan, for example.”
Jake stared at her.
“…What about it?”
Lucy blinked.
“What do you mean ‘what about it’?”
Jake seemed honestly puzzled.
“Well… while you’re on this ship the medical systems will halt your aging anyway.”
Lucy froze.
“…What?”
Jake pointed upward toward the sky.
“The medical wing runs constant cellular regeneration scans.”
Lucy’s ears slowly lifted.
“You… stop aging?”
Jake shrugged.
“More or less.”
Lucy stared at him in stunned silence.
Jake glanced toward the hovering AI avatar beside the map.
“LYRA, how long does the medical system estimate my current lifespan again?”
The AI answered calmly.
“With existing technology and current biological stability, you may expect several thousand additional years of life.”
Lucy turned her head very slowly.
“…Several thousand.”
Jake nodded thoughtfully.
“And that’s just with what we have right now.”
He took another sip of coffee.
“Imagine what medical technology will look like in a thousand years.”
Lucy’s tail stopped moving entirely.
Jake continued casually.
“So realistically, that timeline will keep extending.”
He paused for a moment.
Then shrugged.
“Honestly at this point I might just be immortal.”
Lucy stared at him.
Her mind tried to connect several ideas at once.
Four hundred and thirty-nine Lunari had escaped slavery hoping for something very simple.
Safety.
Food.
A place to rest.
Instead they had encountered a human who casually talked about **thousands of years of life** as if it were a minor logistical detail.
Lucy spoke very carefully.
“You are offering my people functional immortality.”
Jake blinked.
“Huh.”
He thought about that for a second.
“…I guess I am.”
Lucy covered her face briefly.
“And your reasoning for doing this…”
Jake pointed at her ears again.
“Still the same.”
Lucy’s entire face turned red.
Before she could respond, LYRA’s calm voice interrupted.
“Captain.”
“Yes?”
“If you intend to construct a fleet capable of multi-millennial independent exploration, significant resource acquisition will be required.”
Jake nodded.
“Right.”
Lucy turned back to the holographic map as the projection shifted.
A new data panel appeared beside it.
Material requirements scrolled downward in a long list.
Lucy’s eyes widened.
“Estimated structural mass requirements: several billion tons of metallic materials,” LYRA explained.
The list expanded further.
Element symbols appeared in rapid sequence.
Iron. Titanium. Nickel. Cobalt. Vanadium. Tungsten. Platinum. Osmium.
Lucy slowly realized the list was continuing.
Lithium. Carbon. Silicon. Copper. Aluminum.
The list kept going.
At least ninety percent of the periodic table appeared across the scrolling display.
Lucy blinked.
“…That is most of the known elements.”
“Yes,” LYRA confirmed.
Additional categories appeared.
“Rare superconductors.”
“Exotic heavy elements.”
“Antimatter containment components.”
Lucy slowly turned toward Jake.
He was staring at the list calmly while drinking his coffee.
She pointed weakly at the display.
“That… that is the requirement for your fleet?”
Jake nodded.
“More or less.”
Lucy looked back at the endless list of materials.
Billions of tons.
Exotic matter.
Industrial production on a scale she had never even imagined.
Jake finished his coffee and tossed the empty cup into a small recycling drone that floated nearby.
Then he clapped his hands once.
“Well.”
Lucy looked at him.
Jake smiled.
“Guess we better go mining.”









The ship glided silently into the asteroid belt.
Beyond the massive bridge viewport, space was filled with drifting stone and metal. Thousands of asteroids slowly rotated in the darkness, some small enough to toss with one hand, others the size of mountains. Sunlight from the nearby star reflected off metallic surfaces, scattering faint glimmers across the void.
It looked like a frozen storm.
Lucy stood near the observation window, her tail moving slowly behind her as she stared at the endless field of rock.
She had never seen anything like it.
Jake stood a few meters away at the central control console, casually finishing another cup of coffee while scrolling through a tablet.
The bridge itself was enormous—far larger than the command decks Lucy had known on the refugee ships. Transparent displays floated in the air, while quiet drones moved between consoles performing minor adjustments.
Lucy spoke first.
“…There are thousands of them.”
Jake glanced up at the belt outside.
“Yep.”
“Are they all… valuable?”
Jake tilted his head slightly.
“Most of them.”
Lucy looked back at the drifting rocks.
“And you plan to mine them?”
“Some of them.”
Lucy waited.
Jake kept reading his tablet.
She frowned slightly.
“…With drones?”
Jake looked up.
“Nah.”
Lucy blinked.
He set the coffee mug aside and tapped a control on the console.
The ship hummed softly.
Outside the viewport, the massive hull shifted slightly as a section along the ventral side opened.
A weapon—or tool—extended from the ship’s underside.
Lucy leaned closer to the glass.
“What is that?”
Jake pressed another control.
A beam of brilliant orange light burst from the ship.
It crossed the distance to a nearby asteroid in less than a second.
The rock was enormous—at least two kilometers wide.
The beam touched its surface.
For a moment nothing happened.
Then a glowing line appeared across the asteroid.
The line deepened.
Widened.
And the entire mountain of stone slowly split apart.
Two massive halves drifted away from each other as molten rock glowed along the cut.
Lucy’s ears shot straight up.
“…Jake.”
“Yeah?”
“…That asteroid was two kilometers wide.”
Jake shrugged.
“Good starter rock.”
Before Lucy could respond, the ship’s tractor systems activated.
Invisible forces reached out into space.
The two halves of the asteroid slowed… stopped… and then began drifting toward the ship with perfect control.
Chunks of ore broke away as automated cutters sliced the rock into manageable sections.
Lucy watched the process in stunned silence.
Then Jake turned around.
“Actually, this part is where you guys come in.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Us?”
Jake tapped a control.
The large doors at the rear of the bridge opened.
Three Lunari stepped hesitantly inside.
Lucy recognized them immediately.
Taren.
Mika.
Soril.
All three had been industrial workers on the colony.
Miners.
They paused near the entrance, clearly unsure if they were allowed to approach the central console.
Jake waved them over.
“Hey guys.”
They moved closer carefully.
Taren, the oldest of the three, spoke first.
“You asked for us, Captain?”
Jake nodded.
“Yep.”
He turned the main holographic display toward them.
The asteroid belt expanded into a detailed three-dimensional map.
Hundreds of asteroids were highlighted in different colors.
Composition percentages floated beside them.
Iron. Nickel. Platinum. Rare metals.
Jake placed his own tablet on the console.
Lucy glanced at it.
It displayed a perfectly optimized mining plan.
Extraction routes.
Energy usage calculations.
Maximum efficiency paths through the belt.
Jake slid the tablet aside.
“Alright.”
He picked up three smaller tablets and handed one to each of the Lunari miners.
They accepted them cautiously.
Soril looked down at the screen.
“…What is this?”
Jake pointed at the holographic asteroid field.
“Targeting controls.”
The three miners looked up simultaneously.
Taren blinked.
“You want… us to select the asteroids?”
Jake nodded.
“Sure.”
Lucy looked between them.
“…Jake.”
“Yeah?”
“Your tablet already shows the most efficient extraction pattern.”
Jake glanced at his tablet.
“Oh yeah.”
He casually pushed it farther away across the console.
“Super efficient.”
Lucy frowned.
“Then why—”
Jake pointed at the miners.
“Because that one’s boring.”
The three Lunari stared at him.
Jake leaned back against the console.
“You guys spent years mining, right?”
Taren nodded slowly.
“Yes.”
Jake gestured toward the asteroid field.
“Well.”
He pointed at the tablets.
“Have fun.”
There was a long pause.
Soril cautiously tapped the screen.
A targeting reticle appeared over one of the asteroids outside.
Lucy watched as the massive mining beam adjusted its aim slightly.
Soril looked up nervously.
“Should I…?”
Jake grinned.
“Do it.”
Soril tapped the confirmation icon.
The orange beam fired again.
Another asteroid split cleanly in half.
Soril’s ears shot up in shock.
Mika leaned closer to the viewport.
“…That was my shot?”
Jake nodded.
“Nice aim.”
Lucy slowly realized what he was doing.
For years these Lunari had worked inside brutal industrial systems where every movement was controlled by overseers.
Now Jake had handed them the controls of a ship capable of dismantling entire celestial bodies.
Taren carefully selected a larger asteroid.
The beam fired again.
The rock shattered apart like glass.
The three miners stared at the display… then at each other.
And for the first time since Lucy had known them—
They smiled.
Jake crossed his arms and watched the asteroid belt explode into drifting fragments of ore.
“See?” he said casually.
“Mining can be fun.”












Over the next few days, the asteroid belt became a very busy place.
What had begun as a simple mining operation slowly turned into something far more lively.
The Lunari had discovered that Jake truly meant it when he said they could help.
Miners rotated through the bridge in small groups, learning the targeting systems and guiding the enormous mining beam across the drifting asteroid field. Outside the panoramic viewport, massive rocks slowly split apart under the glowing orange beam before being captured by the ship’s tractor systems and drawn inward for processing.
Lucy often stood beside Jake, watching the activity unfold.
The bridge no longer felt like a sterile command center.
It felt almost… communal.
Lunari moved between consoles discussing mineral compositions, comparing scan readings, or arguing playfully over which asteroid looked most promising.
Jake’s original perfectly optimized mining plan still rested abandoned on a nearby console.
Lucy had asked him about it once.
His answer had been simple.
“Mine’s efficient.”
He had gestured toward the Lunari gathered around the targeting controls.
“But this one’s more fun.”
At one point two Lunari children had climbed onto a secondary targeting station.
Lucy had noticed immediately.
“…Jake.”
“Hmm?”
“The children.”
Jake glanced over just as one of them pressed the confirmation control.
The mining beam fired.
A small asteroid simply vanished in a flash of molten vapor.
The bridge went completely silent.
The children slowly turned around.
Jake stared at the empty patch of space.
Then burst out laughing.
“Nice shot!”
Lucy had buried her face in her hands.
But the children were already giggling and searching for their next target.
Eventually the steady rhythm of asteroid harvesting began to slow.
Most of the high-value material nearby had already been collected.
The ship processed the final chunks of ore while the Lunari miners watched the display.
Then LYRA spoke calmly over the bridge speakers.
“Captain. Current resource density has dropped below optimal extraction thresholds. The majority of useful materials in this region have been harvested.”

“Nice.”

Dozens of Lunari were still present, gathered around consoles and observation windows.

“Big metal deposits down there.”

“Perfect picnic location.”
Several Lunari exchanged confused looks.
Jake leaned casually against the console.
“Alright listen up.”

“Three hours.”

“Go tell everyone in the habitat that we’re having a picnic.”
Lucy blinked.
“…A picnic.”

“On the moon.”
More confused glances.

“Anyone who wants to come should gather at Hangar Bay Seven.”

“Three hours.”

“Bring snacks.”

“…You are serious.”

“Of course I’m serious.”

“Well? Go spread the word.”

Three hours later Hangar Bay Seven was full.
Nearly four hundred Lunari had gathered beneath the enormous hangar ceiling.
The open space was large enough to house several starships, but now it was filled with curious refugees holding baskets of food, blankets, and various improvised picnic supplies.
Excited whispers echoed across the bay.
Lucy stood near the front beside Jake.
Her tail moved nervously behind her.
“…Jake.”
“Yeah?”
“…You did not explain how we are supposed to reach the surface of that moon.”
Jake shrugged.
“Easy.”
Above them the hangar doors slowly began to open.
Beyond the widening gap stretched the endless darkness of space.
Gasps rippled through the gathered Lunari.
The ship hovered roughly fifty kilometers above the moon’s surface.
The grey landscape curved gently below them.
Lucy looked at Jake.
“…We cannot simply walk out there.”
Jake smiled.
“Sure we can.”
Before anyone could ask another question—
He stepped forward.
Straight out of the open hangar.
Lucy’s heart stopped.
Jake immediately fell.
His body dropped downward into the open void.
For a brief second the entire hangar froze in stunned silence.
Then chaos erupted.
“JAKE!”
Lucy ran forward toward the edge of the hangar.
Several Lunari cried out in shock.
But a moment later something strange happened.
Jake stopped falling.
His descent slowed… then reversed.
He floated upward again, drifting gently back toward the ship.
Around him a faint shimmering sphere became visible.
A barely perceptible bubble of air surrounded him.
Jake crossed his arms and laughed at the horrified expressions staring down at him.
“Relax!”
He floated casually a few meters below the hangar opening, standing in empty space as if gravity had become optional.
“Field bubble!”

“Portable atmosphere.”
Lucy leaned over the edge of the hangar, staring at him.
“…You jumped out of the ship.”

“Seemed faster.”

“Come on!”
Several of them hesitated.
Jake grinned.
“It’s safe.”

He swallowed nervously.
Then carefully stepped out of the hangar.

Instead he floated gently beside Jake inside the invisible sphere.
His eyes widened.
“It… works.”
That was all the encouragement the others needed.
Soon dozens of Lunari drifted out of the hangar.
Then hundreds.
The massive field bubble expanded automatically, surrounding the growing crowd.
Four hundred Lunari now floated together in open space.
Jake clapped his hands excitedly.
“Alright!”
He pointed toward the moon far below.
“Let’s go.”
The invisible bubble began descending.
They glided gently downward through the silent darkness.
The moon slowly grew larger beneath them.
Mountains rose along the horizon.
Cratered plains stretched for kilometers.
Eventually their feet touched the grey surface.
The massive bubble settled across the lunar landscape like an invisible dome.
Lucy stepped carefully onto the dust-covered ground.
She looked up.
Above them the enormous shape of Jake’s ship hovered quietly in orbit.
Stars filled the sky.
Around her the Lunari were laughing again.
Children bounced in low gravity, leaping meters into the air.
Others spread blankets across the moon dust and unpacked baskets of food.
Some simply lay on their backs staring up at the stars.
Lucy turned slowly in place.
A picnic.
On a moon.
Jake kicked a small rock that bounced lazily across the surface.
He seemed perfectly comfortable.
As if the entire universe was simply a playground waiting to be explored.
Lucy watched him for a moment.
“…Jake.”
“Yeah?”
“…You treat space like a garden.”
Jake shrugged.
“Why not?”
Lucy shook her head slowly.
Behind her LYRA’s calm voice emerged from a small hovering drone that had followed them down.
“This behavior is statistically common among the wealthiest individuals within human civilization.”
Lucy glanced at the drone.
“…It is?”
“Yes.”
LYRA paused briefly before adding,
“When one possesses the resources to reshape entire star systems, the universe often becomes… recreational.”
Lucy looked back at Jake.
He was currently teaching a group of Lunari children how to jump high enough to do slow-motion flips in lunar gravity.
For the first time Lucy truly understood something.
To Jake—
The universe was not something distant or terrifying.
It was simply a place to play.









The picnic slowly settled into a relaxed rhythm.
Lunari sat in small groups across the grey lunar plain, blankets spread over the dust while baskets of food were passed around. The low gravity made everything slightly surreal. Children bounced across the surface in slow graceful arcs while adults experimented with careful jumps that carried them far higher than they expected.
The invisible atmosphere bubble stretched across a wide section of the landscape, large enough that the edges of it were barely noticeable.
Lucy sat on a small rise beside Jake.
From there they could see most of the gathering.
Several Lunari had begun inventing games involving long slow leaps across the surface. One group was trying to see who could throw a small rock the farthest without losing it to lunar orbit.

“See?” he said, gesturing with a piece of bread in his hand. “Good picnic spot.”

“We are standing on a moon.”

“Yep.”

“Also true.”

“Oh right.”

Hundreds of meters wide.
Thousands of tons of material.
Floating upward like feathers.

“Well.”

“That’ll be fun later.”









The picnic lasted much longer than anyone expected.

Lucy immediately tensed.
“Jake—”

Without a suit.
Without hesitation.
He fell.
Lucy’s heart stopped for a moment—

The transparent atmosphere bubble.

Water sprayed upward.

“Water’s great!”

Then one child jumped.
They splashed into the water.
The bubble expanded automatically.
Soon dozens followed.
Then hundreds.

The protective bubble extended beneath the water surface, forming a massive spherical pocket where air and water mixed freely.
Lunari cautiously entered the water.
At first many of them simply floated.
Then someone realized the low gravity and buoyancy made movement incredibly easy.
Soon splashing began.
Children swam in wide circles.
Adults laughed nervously as they discovered how to stay afloat.
Jake floated on his back, watching the clouds above.
In the distance several alien naval vessels cautiously approached.
Submarines moved beneath the water surface.
Missiles continued streaking overhead toward the ship.
The explosions looked like distant fireworks.
Lucy floated nearby, staring in disbelief.
“…We are swimming.”
Jake nodded.
“In the deepest ocean on their planet.”
Another explosion flashed far above.
“…While they are actively trying to destroy us.”
Jake shrugged.
“Multitasking.”
Nearby a group of Lunari children were experimenting with diving beneath the water surface.
One of them suddenly had an idea.
He raised his hand and spoke toward the sky.
“LYRA?”
“Yes?”
“Could you move a bubble of water up into the air?”
Jake looked over curiously.
“How big?” LYRA asked.
“Maybe… ten meters?”
“And my two friends!”
A moment later a perfectly spherical bubble of ocean water rose slowly upward from the surface.
Three very surprised Lunari children floated inside it.
The water sphere drifted upward like a giant floating aquarium.
Jake burst out laughing.
“Nice one.”
Lucy stared upward at the floating water bubble.
Her last fragments of rational thought finally collapsed.
Something inside her mind simply gave up trying to process the situation logically.
Missiles were exploding overhead.
Alien warships were circling them.
And hundreds of Lunari were happily swimming.
She slowly turned toward a nearby service drone floating above the water.
Her voice was very quiet.
“…May I request equipment?”
“What equipment?” the drone asked.
Lucy thought for a moment.
“…A water pistol.”
The drone paused briefly.
Then complied.
A small colorful water pistol emerged from its storage compartment.
Lucy took it slowly.
She floated quietly through the water.
Jake was still laughing at the floating water sphere.
He didn’t notice Lucy approaching behind him.
She raised the pistol.
A perfectly aimed stream of water blasted directly into his face.
Jake sputtered.
“Hey—!”
Lucy crossed her arms, ears slightly tilted forward.
“You deserved that.”
Jake wiped water from his eyes.
Then he grinned.
“Oh it’s like that now?”
Lucy suddenly realized what she had started.
A moment later Jake grabbed a nearby drone.
“LYRA.”
“Yes?”
“Arm me.”
The drone extended several brightly colored water blasters.
Lucy’s ears rose in alarm.
“Oh no.”
Jake grinned wider.
“Oh yes.”
The next several minutes became complete chaos.
Hundreds of Lunari joined the battle.
Water splashed everywhere as teams formed spontaneously.
Floating water bubbles drifted through the air like strange aquatic balloons.
Children launched surprise attacks from above.
Jake proved extremely dangerous with dual water blasters.
Lucy managed to hit him several more times anyway.
At one point she tried sneaking up behind him again—
Jake turned suddenly.
They both fired at the same time.
Both were completely soaked.
They stared at each other for a moment.
Lucy’s ears were dripping.
Jake’s hair was completely wet.
Then they both started laughing.
Around them the Lunari continued splashing and shouting happily.
Missiles exploded harmlessly high above.
Alien warships circled in complete confusion.
And in the middle of it all Lucy realized something strange.
For the first time in years…
She wasn’t thinking about survival.
She wasn’t thinking about strategy.
Or politics.
Or the future of her people.
She was simply laughing.
Jake floated beside her in the water.
He nudged her lightly.
“You started the war, you know.”
Lucy flicked water at him with her tail.
“…I regret nothing.”
Jake grinned.
Somewhere above them another missile detonated like a distant firework.











The ocean calmed again after the long water battle.
Missiles still flashed far above the clouds, and distant alien warships cautiously circled the enormous ship hovering in the sky. None of them dared approach the strange gathering of hundreds of Lunari playing in the water below.
Gradually the splashing slowed.
People floated lazily in the warm surface water. Some rested on drifting equipment drones. Others watched the strange floating water spheres the children had convinced LYRA to create.
Jake stretched in the water and looked down toward the deep blue below them.
Then his eyes lit up again.
“Oh.”
Lucy immediately felt a faint sense of danger.
Jake raised his voice.
“Hey everyone!”
Hundreds of ears turned toward him.
“I want to go to the sea floor.”
Lucy closed her eyes briefly.
Of course he did.
Jake continued cheerfully.
“Anyone want to come along?”
A wave of excited murmuring spread across the Lunari.
Some immediately looked nervous. The deep ocean below them was dark, vast, and mysterious.
Lucy floated quietly for a moment.
Yesterday she would have questioned everything.
Today she simply sighed softly.
Then she raised a hand.
“I will go.”
Jake grinned at her.
Soon groups began discussing the idea.
Many Lunari looked down into the deep blue water and shook their heads.
Dark.
Cold.
Endless.
Not everyone liked that idea.
Eventually about 150 Lunari chose to remain near the surface. They preferred the sunlight, the waves, and the distant fireworks of alien missiles streaking through the sky.
The remaining 290 gathered closer to Jake.
Children bounced excitedly in the water.
Adults looked curious but slightly cautious.
Jake suddenly rose.
He stood up.
On the surface of the ocean.
Perfectly balanced.
Lucy blinked.
“…Of course.”
Jake gestured casually.
“If you want to come down, just stand on the water.”
Several Lunari stared at the surface uncertainly.
One child tried first.
They slowly pushed themselves upright.
To their surprise, their feet did not sink.
The water surface behaved like a soft but stable floor.
More Lunari tried.
Soon dozens were carefully attempting to stand on the ocean surface.
It quickly became chaotic.
Four loose groups naturally formed as people helped each other balance.
Children giggled while adults tried to remain steady.
Lucy managed to stand after a few attempts.
The sensation was strange.
The water surface flexed slightly beneath her feet but held firm.
Jake watched the groups with quiet amusement.
Then the ocean surface beneath them began to move.
At first it looked like the water was simply sinking.
The surface slowly lowered.
All 290 Lunari standing on it descended together.
Lucy realized what was happening.
The water itself wasn’t moving.
A gigantic field bubble had formed beneath them.
The “surface” they stood on was the flat top of that bubble.
The ocean flowed around them as they descended.
After a few seconds the shape stabilized.
Four massive air bubbles now surrounded the four groups.
Each bubble had a perfectly flat bottom that looked like a platform made of invisible glass.
Lucy looked around in quiet amazement.
Above them the sunlight faded slowly.
Blue water stretched in every direction.
One of the younger children standing near the edge suddenly lost balance.
They slipped slightly sideways.
Several adults gasped—
But the child didn’t hit any wall.
Instead the bubble itself shifted.
The boundary expanded smoothly outward.
The child simply slid into the newly formed space.
The bubble had adjusted instantly.
The child blinked.
Then grinned.
A dangerous idea appeared on his face.
He looked toward the next group drifting a short distance away.
Before any adult could stop him—
He ran.
The moment he stepped beyond the main bubble, a smaller bubble formed around him.
A transparent sphere of air followed him like a running bubble helmet.
The child sprinted through the water toward the other group.
Lucy watched in disbelief.
“LYRA…”
“Yes?”
“Was that intentional?”
“Yes.”
The child reached the next group triumphantly.
A moment later several other children tried the same thing.
Soon small bubbles were darting back and forth between the four descending groups like bouncing soap bubbles.
Adults attempted to maintain order.
It did not work.
By the time they reached halfway down, almost all the children were running wildly between groups.
Some adults joined them.
The bubbles shifted continuously, merging, stretching, and separating.
Meanwhile small drones arrived, distributing equipment.
“Flashlights for low-light visibility,” LYRA announced calmly.
Everyone received one.
The beams cut through the deepening darkness as they descended further.
Above them the surface light had faded to faint blue.
Below them stretched endless black.
Eventually the bottom appeared.
A vast plain of dark sediment.
Their bubbles touched the ocean floor gently.
For a moment everyone simply stood there.
The deep ocean was quiet.
Incredibly quiet.
No currents.
No sounds except distant muffled vibrations of the surface world far above.
The plain stretched in every direction.
Empty.
Vast.
Almost alien in its stillness.
The Lunari slowly began exploring.
Flashlight beams moved across the ground.
Occasionally they discovered strange clusters of deep-sea plants.
Tall thin structures with faint bioluminescent tips.
Lucy crouched beside one.
It glowed softly when touched.
“Beautiful,” she whispered.
Farther away someone shouted softly.
“I found something!”
A small creature drifted through the darkness.
A fish.
Its body was long and pale, with wide translucent fins and faint glowing lines along its sides.
Clearly adapted for the deep.
Jake floated closer, fascinated.
“Huh.”
Lucy tilted her head.
“What do the locals call this species?”
Jake glanced upward.
“LYRA?”
“The planetary civilization has not catalogued this species,” LYRA replied. “It does not currently possess an official name.”
Jake grinned.
“Well that’s unacceptable.”
He turned toward a nearby group of about thirty Lunari.
“Alright, naming contest.”
Immediately everyone started offering suggestions.
The fish drifted slowly nearby while dozens of possible names were debated.
Some suggestions were poetic.
Others ridiculous.
Eventually the group settled on a name that made everyone laugh.
Jake recorded it proudly.
“Congratulations,” he said. “You’ve officially discovered and named a species.”
The Lunari looked quietly proud.
Eventually the groups spread out further across the ocean floor.
Flashlights moved like wandering stars across the darkness.
Lucy explored slowly beside Jake.
They found a small depression in the sediment.
At its edge was a narrow cave entrance.
Jake crouched.
“Ooh.”
Lucy sighed.
“…We are going inside, aren’t we.”
Jake grinned.
“Of course.”
They stepped down into the small cavern.
The bubble followed them automatically, shaping itself through the entrance.
Inside, faint bioluminescent organisms covered the cave walls.
Soft green and blue lights shimmered in the darkness.
It was quiet.
Peaceful.
For a while they simply explored.
Then Lucy stopped walking.
Jake turned back.
“You okay?”
She hesitated.
Her ears lowered slightly.
“…Jake.”
He waited.
Lucy looked around the glowing cave slowly.
Then back at him.
“I have not properly thanked you.”
Jake blinked.
“For what?”
Lucy laughed softly.
“For everything.”
She gestured vaguely upward.
“For rescuing us.”
“For giving us a place to live.”
“For helping our people rebuild.”
Her voice grew quieter.
“For giving us hope again.”
Jake shifted slightly, suddenly looking a bit uncomfortable.
Lucy stepped closer.
Her ears tilted forward gently.
“But most importantly…”
She paused.
“…for seeing us.”
Jake frowned slightly.
“Seeing you?”
Lucy nodded.
“Yes.”
She looked down briefly.
“For a long time the galaxy did not see the Lunari as people.”
“Only workers.”
“Property.”
“Numbers.”
Her eyes lifted again.
“But you saw us.”
She gave a small smile.
“Even before you knew anything about us.”
Jake scratched the back of his head awkwardly.
“Well… yeah.”
Lucy studied him for a moment.
Then she laughed quietly.
“…You also took us swimming in the middle of a planetary defense battle.”
Jake shrugged.
“Good ocean.”
Lucy smiled again.
The bioluminescent cave lights shimmered softly around them.
For a moment neither of them said anything.
Far away above them, missiles still exploded harmlessly against the ship’s shields.
But down here at the bottom of the alien ocean, the universe felt very quiet.














Several weeks later the ship left the troubled system behind.
The alien planet grew smaller behind them until it disappeared into the distant starfield. The confused civilization below would likely spend decades trying to understand what had happened. Their weapons had fired. Their sensors had recorded impossible things. Their oceans had briefly contained hundreds of laughing strangers.
Then the enormous ship had simply… left.
Now it traveled through regions of space where no known navigation charts existed.
The stars here were unfamiliar.
Navigation beacons were absent.
Even long-range sensor sweeps found very little artificial activity.
It was quiet space.
Unmapped space.
Unclaimed space.
Lucy stood on the observation deck as the ship slowed near a small yellow star surrounded by a handful of distant planets and scattered asteroid belts.
“Where are we?” she asked softly.
Jake leaned against the railing beside her.
“Somewhere nobody cares about.”
Lucy looked at him.
“That sounds deliberate.”
Jake nodded.
“Very.”
Outside the vast observation window the ship slowly rotated.
Then enormous structures began unfolding from its hull.
Lucy had seen some of them before during maintenance operations.
But never like this.
Massive docking frameworks extended outward like mechanical cities blooming from the ship’s surface. Kilometer-long arms unfolded. Gigantic platforms separated and drifted into position around the vessel.
The quiet star system suddenly filled with motion.
“LYRA,” Jake said casually, “deploy shipyard configuration.”
“Acknowledged.”
More structures emerged.
Construction gantries.
Industrial reactors.
Assembly scaffolds.
Lucy watched as hundreds—then thousands—of drones began moving in coordinated swarms.
Containers opened.
Raw materials flowed outward in streams of metal and composite structures.
Asteroid ore that Jake had mined weeks earlier began feeding into enormous fabrication systems.
Lucy slowly stepped closer to the glass.
“What are you doing?”
Jake shrugged lightly.
“Building ships.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Ships.”
“Yep.”
Outside, the first hull framework appeared.
A skeletal structure several kilometers long slowly assembled itself in open space.
Segments locked together with perfect precision.
Massive curved plates followed, drifting into position before sealing against the frame.
Lucy’s ears tilted forward.
“That hull is larger than our entire refugee transport.”
Jake glanced at it.
“Yeah, that’s one of the medium ones.”
Lucy turned slowly toward him.
“…Medium.”
More hulls appeared.
Some long and sleek.
Others wide and layered with large internal habitat sections.
Engines the size of small mountains were assembled piece by piece by thousands of drones working in perfect coordination.
Energy cores slid into place.
Shield arrays unfolded.
Docking ports and cargo structures formed along the outer hulls.
Lucy realized the scale of what she was watching.
He wasn’t building a few ships.
He was building dozens.
Each one powerful.
Self-sufficient.
Capable of sustaining thousands of passengers for long voyages.
Weapons platforms appeared next.
Not aggressive warship arrays.
But defensive systems.
Shield emitters.
Point defense grids.
Escort drones.
Every vessel looked designed to survive long journeys through uncertain space.
Lucy watched silently as more components floated into place.
Habitat modules assembled along the inner sections of several ships.
Entire living ecosystems began forming inside transparent domes.
Gardens.
Water systems.
Residential structures.
Schools.
Medical facilities.
Lucy’s voice was barely a whisper.
“…This is a fleet.”
Jake nodded.
“Pretty much.”
Lucy stared at the construction swarms.
“But… why here?”
Jake gestured toward the empty star system.
“No one will bother you out here.”
He leaned slightly against the railing.
“You’ll have ships.”
“Resources.”
“Navigation charts.”
“And enough infrastructure to keep expanding if you want.”
Lucy’s eyes slowly widened.
“You are building us a civilization fleet.”
Jake shrugged again.
“Seems easier than arguing with politicians about giving you a planet.”
Outside, another enormous hull rotated into place as its engine core ignited briefly during testing.
The light illuminated dozens of other vessels under construction nearby.
Lucy felt something tighten in her chest.
She had known Jake intended to help them.
She had seen his generosity.
His casual disregard for wealth.
But seeing this…
Seeing an entire future being assembled piece by piece in open space…
That was different.
Kilometer-long hull segments floated past the observation deck.
Drones welded structural plates together with precise bursts of energy.
One of the first completed engines ignited softly as its calibration systems activated.
Lucy watched the glow reflect across the glass.
“You truly intend to give us a future.”
Jake looked slightly confused by the statement.
“Of course.”
Lucy turned toward him slowly.
“For many civilizations that would be the greatest act of charity in their history.”
Jake scratched the side of his head.
“Well… I had the materials.”
Lucy stared at him.
“…You dismantled part of a planet to get those materials.”
Jake considered that.
“Technically I replaced the missing parts.”
Lucy laughed quietly despite herself.
She looked back out at the growing fleet.
Ships were beginning to take recognizable shapes now.
Soon they would be ready for crews.
For families.
For explorers.
For people searching for lost Lunari colonies across the galaxy.
Or perhaps for people who simply wanted a safe place to live.
Lucy rested her hands against the observation rail.
For the first time since their rescue, she could clearly see what their future might look like.
Not refugees.
Not survivors.
But a people moving forward again.
Behind her Jake watched the shipyards with quiet satisfaction.
More drones launched from the main vessel.
More hulls began assembling.
A small civilization was slowly taking shape in the empty star system.








The first hull of the new fleet had already begun to glow with internal lights when Lucy approached one of the Garden’s quiet control nodes.
She looked around briefly.
The river flowed softly nearby. The artificial sky above the habitat was bright with late afternoon light. Groups of Lunari moved between the growing settlement structures, unaware of the decision that was about to shape their entire future.

“LYRA?”

“I need your help.”

Somewhere else on the ship Jake suddenly received a long list of complicated engineering questions from LYRA regarding shipyard alignment tolerances and reactor calibration.

An hour later the entire Lunari population had gathered in the Garden valley.
Nearly five hundred people stood or sat across the wide grassy field beside the river.
Lucy stood near the small wooden bridge.

These were her people.
Survivors.
Families who had escaped slavery, war, and extinction.

“You all know what Captain Jake is doing.”

The drones.
The growing fleet in orbit.

Lucy gestured toward the sky.
“Ships that can carry our people across the stars again.”
“Ships that can defend themselves.”
“Ships that can explore.”

“He is giving us the tools to rebuild our civilization.”
The valley was very quiet now.
Lucy took a slow breath.
“But this means something important.”
She looked across the gathered Lunari.
“For the first time in generations, we have choices.”

“We could settle on a planet.”

Lucy raised a second finger.
“We could travel.”

Lost colonies.
Scattered survivors.

“With Jake.”

“If we want to travel, he will give us ships and supplies.”

Respectful.
Many voices sharing thoughts.

Warmer.
