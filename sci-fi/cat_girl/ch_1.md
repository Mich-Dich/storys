Deep space was silent in a way that only the outermost edges of civilization could be.
No trade lanes.
No patrol fleets.
No friendly stations broadcasting navigation signals.
Just distant stars and six damaged ships running for their lives.

Inside the lead refugee vessel, the command deck glowed with tired emergency lighting. Alarms had been sounding for hours, their harsh tones blending into a constant background noise that no one could ignore anymore.
The convoy ships were transport craft—long-range civilian vessels never meant for combat. Their engines were pushed far beyond safe limits, heat bleeding through exhausted cooling systems.
Behind them, twenty pirate ships closed in.
On the main command deck, Acting Captain Lucy stood beside the tactical display, one hand braced against the console. A medical bandage wrapped around her shoulder where shrapnel had cut through earlier in the chase.
She forced herself to stay upright.
A bright flash lit the sensor screen.
Another pirate weapon had just struck near the rear of the convoy.
The navigation officer spoke quietly.
“Captain… pirate formation tightening.”
Lucy watched the tactical display. Red icons slowly surrounded the fragile cluster of refugee ships.
They were being herded.
Hunted.
“How long until they can fire again?” she asked.
“Two minutes… maybe less.”
Two minutes.
Lucy nodded once, calm despite the exhaustion in her eyes.
“At least they’re patient,” someone muttered.
At the communications station, another officer suddenly straightened.
“Captain… we’re still broadcasting the distress signal.”
Lucy turned.
“Any response?”
The officer hesitated.
“…None.”
No fleets.
No allies.
No help.
They were far beyond the reach of friendly space.
Lucy closed her eyes for a moment, then spoke quietly.
“Keep broadcasting.”
It was the only thing left to do.

Out in the darkness, the pirates advanced.
Their ships were smaller, faster, built for intimidation and quick kills. Their leader watched the fleeing convoy with relaxed confidence.
The refugees were slowing.
Engines were failing.
Soon they would stop running.

Back on the refugee flagship, the communications officer suddenly gasped.
“Captain!”
Lucy turned immediately.
“What is it?”
“I… I think we have a response.”
Every head on the bridge snapped toward the console.
“Source?” Lucy asked.
The officer stared at the sensors.
“…One ship.”
Lucy frowned.
“Military?”
“I… don’t know.”
The tactical display expanded.
A new contact appeared on the edge of the system.
The bridge fell silent.
The vessel was enormous.
Not just large.
Enormous.
Its elegant shape stretched for kilometers, long and sleek like a predatory animal gliding through the void.
Someone whispered, barely audible.
“That’s a capital ship…”
Another officer leaned closer to the screen.
“But whose fleet builds ships like that?”
Lucy studied the unknown vessel carefully.
Its design did not match any military she recognized.
Still…
It was powerful.
Hope flickered for the first time in hours.
“Open a channel,” she said quickly.
“Send the full distress signal.”
The officer nodded and activated the broadcast.
The screen in front of Lucy shifted as the connection attempted to stabilize.
Static flickered.
Then a camera feed began to form.

Several light minutes away, inside a vast ship that felt more like a quiet palace drifting through the stars, a man sat comfortably in a reclining chair.
Beyond him stretched a massive observation window framing a bright distant star.
A cup rested on a nearby table.
The calm voice of the ship’s AI interrupted the silence.
“Captain.”
He didn’t look away from the view.
“Yes?”
“We are receiving a distress signal.”
He sighed.
“Pirates again?”
“Most likely.”
He thought for a moment.
“How many ships?”
“Six civilian vessels. Twenty pursuing contacts.”
He leaned his head back.
“Hmm.”
A pause.
“Put them on screen.”

Back on the refugee flagship, the communications screen flickered.
The connection established.
Lucy straightened, preparing to speak.
Her people needed protection.
She would beg for it if she had to.
The image of the human appeared on their screen.
He looked relaxed. Curious.
Behind him stretched what appeared to be an enormous command deck.
Lucy opened her mouth.
“Unidentified vessel, we—”
But at that exact moment, the camera on the human’s side finally focused properly.
And the human captain saw her clearly.

He froze.
On his screen stood a young woman.
She looked barely older than twenty.
Her face was delicate, framed by long black hair that fell past her shoulders.
But what truly captured his attention were the ears.
Instead of human ears, two soft triangular cat ears rose from her hair—black like midnight, with soft white fur visible inside.
Behind her command chair, a long bushy black tail moved slowly with tension.
And when she opened her mouth to speak again…
For a brief second, small sharp fangs were visible.
The human captain blinked.
Once.
Twice.
Then he leaned forward slowly.
“…No.”
The AI spoke calmly.
“Captain?”
His eyes widened in disbelief.
“No way.”
The AI waited.
The human whispered like someone witnessing a miracle.
“…Cat people.”
The AI paused.
“Yes.”
The captain stared at the screen as if the universe had just delivered the greatest discovery in human history.
Lucy was still speaking, unaware of the emotional collapse happening on the other side of the connection.
“—request immediate assistance. We have civilian vessels and injured—”
The captain suddenly jumped to his feet.
“LYRA.”
“Yes, Captain.”
“FULL COMBAT MODE.”
The AI paused briefly.
“…Captain?”
“FULL. COMBAT. MODE.”
“Confirmed.”
Across the enormous ship, dormant systems exploded into life.
Weapons armed.
Drone bays opened.
Targeting arrays stretched across the system.
Power surged through reactors designed for wars no one could afford.
Meanwhile, the captain leaned toward the screen again, eyes shining with pure excitement.
“They’re real.”
The AI said nothing.
“LYRA.”
“Yes?”
“CAT PEOPLE ARE REAL.”
“Yes.”
The captain pointed at the screen like an overexcited child.
“WE HAVE TO SAVE THEM.”
“You were already considering intervention.”
“NOT LIKE THIS.”
On the refugee bridge, the transmission suddenly cut out.
The screen went dark.
Lucy blinked in confusion.
“…What?”
The communications officer checked the console.
“They disconnected.”
Silence filled the room.
No explanation.
No response.
Lucy slowly lowered her head.
Perhaps the unknown ship had decided the situation was hopeless.
Perhaps they simply didn’t want to get involved.
Behind them, the pirates finished charging their weapons.
A harsh alarm sounded across the bridge.
“Incoming attack.”
Lucy sat down slowly in the command chair.
This time the pirates were firing to destroy them.
Across the convoy, people understood what that meant.
Some began to pray.

The tactical officer counted down quietly.
“Five seconds.”
Outside, twenty pirate ships opened fire.
Energy weapons surged toward the helpless convoy.
Four seconds.
Three.
Two—
Every alarm on the bridge suddenly stopped.
Not quieted.
Stopped.
The tactical officer stared at his console.
“…What?”
The incoming weapons had vanished.
Not intercepted.
Not destroyed.
Just… gone.
Across the sensor display, something impossible began happening.
One pirate ship split cleanly in half.
Another twisted inward as if crushed by invisible gravity.
A third detonated in a sudden flash of expanding plasma.
More followed.
One after another.
Ships died faster than the sensors could track.
The bridge crew stared in stunned silence.
Within two minutes…
All twenty pirate vessels were gone.
Only drifting wreckage remained.
The communications console beeped.
Incoming transmission.
Lucy slowly activated the screen.
The same human appeared again.
He looked extremely pleased with himself.
He gave a small, slightly awkward wave.
“…Hi.”
The refugee bridge remained silent.
He scratched the back of his head.
His eyes flicked briefly toward Lucy’s ears again.
“…So… uh…”
He smiled, clearly trying to sound professional.
“…Do you guys maybe need an escort?”









For several seconds after the transmission reopened, no one on the refugee bridge said a word.
The human on the screen looked relaxed. Almost cheerful.
Behind him stretched a massive command deck filled with softly glowing displays. The scale alone made it clear that the ship around him was enormous.
Lucy studied him carefully.
This man had just erased an entire pirate fleet in minutes.
And now he was smiling like someone who had just finished a pleasant afternoon task.
“…Hi,” he repeated, giving another small wave.
The silence on the refugee bridge stretched a moment longer before Lucy finally spoke.
“You… destroyed them.”

“Yeah.”

Six ships.
Damage reports scrolling across their displays.
He frowned.
“…Okay wow.”

“What?”

“You guys can’t stay in those.”
The bridge crew looked at one another.
Lucy’s ears twitched slightly.
“Those?”

“The ships.”
A pause.
“They’re basically lifeboats.”

One of the officers muttered quietly, “Lifeboats…?”
Lucy spoke slowly.
“These are long-range civilian transports.”
Jake blinked.
“…Yeah.”

Because he was correct.

“Your ships are broadcasting diagnostics through half the spectrum.”

“…They are?”

“Anyway.”

“You should probably just dock with my ship.”

“Dock?”

“I’ve got space.”

“That ship is bigger than our entire convoy…”

“Why would you take us aboard?”
Jake looked genuinely confused by the question.
“Well… because you’re stranded.”
Lucy said nothing.
Jake tilted his head.
“Oh.”

“Right. Stranger in a giant warship.”

“Technically not a warship.”
Lucy’s tail flicked once.
“You destroyed twenty pirate vessels.”
Jake shrugged.
“They attacked you.”
That apparently settled the matter in his mind.
Lucy exchanged a quiet glance with her officers.
Their situation was clear.
Their engines were exhausted.
Fuel reserves nearly empty.
Hull damage spreading through several ships.
Even if the pirates were gone…
They were not going far.
And the vessel in front of them could have destroyed them just as easily as the pirates.
If it wanted to.

“…We accept your offer.”

“Great!”

“LYRA.”

A gentle force wrapped around each vessel.
Their ships began moving.

And larger.

Empty.
Miles of polished deck stretching beneath towering ceilings filled with quiet lights.

Atmosphere filled the space.
Gravity stabilized.
And for the first time in days…
Everything was quiet.

A large landing ramp extended from Lucy’s ship.
Her crew gathered near the exit hatch.
No one spoke.
Lucy adjusted the bandage on her shoulder and stepped forward.
“Stay alert,” she said calmly.
The door opened.
Cool artificial air drifted into the corridor.
They stepped out onto the polished hangar floor.
The scale of the place was overwhelming.
Their entire convoy sat in a single corner of the massive chamber.
The rest of the hangar stretched away into the distance.
Completely empty.
Footsteps echoed.
Lucy looked toward the far side of the hangar where a single human figure was approaching.
Jake walked across the floor toward them, hands in his pockets, looking extremely curious.
Beside him, a faint holographic projection flickered into existence.
A soft blue female figure.
The AI.
Jake slowed as he reached them.
For a moment he simply stared.
Up close the details were even clearer.
Soft black ears.
White fur inside them.
Long black hair.
A large fluffy tail slowly moving behind her.
And those faint fangs when she breathed.

“…This is the best day of my life.”

“He has already failed.”

“Mute yourself.”

Lucy’s tail flicked slightly.

At the six ships.
Then back at him.

“Oh.”

“Yeah.”
Lucy spoke slowly.
“This ship is larger than most military capital ships.”
Jake tilted his head in confusion.
“…Really?”
He looked around again as if noticing it properly for the first time.
“Huh.”
He looked back at Lucy.
“Well.”
He smiled again.
“Welcome aboard.”







For a few moments after Jake’s last words, nothing happened.
Lucy and the other refugees simply stood on the vast hangar floor, trying to understand the scale of the place they had just entered. Their six transport vessels rested behind them like small metal islands in an ocean of polished deck plating. Towering walls stretched upward into shadow, crisscrossed with maintenance rails, cranes, and silent machinery.
The air smelled faintly clean and artificial, like a newly built station.
One by one, more hatches opened along the refugee ships.
Crew members began stepping out.
Engineers. Pilots. Families. Elderly civilians who had endured the long flight from the war zone. Children clung nervously to their parents as they stared at the cavernous space around them.
Within minutes dozens… then hundreds… of people were slowly gathering on the hangar floor.
They spoke quietly among themselves, voices echoing in the enormous chamber.
Lucy watched them carefully. Her tail moved slowly behind her, betraying her tension.
Jake watched as well, though his expression was different. Curious. Thoughtful.
He looked back at the row of battered transport ships.
Hull plating scorched.
Engines cracked from overuse.
Fuel tanks nearly empty.
The ships had done their job. They had carried their passengers to safety.
But they were finished.
Jake exhaled slowly.
“LYRA.”
“Yes, Captain.”
He nodded toward the transports.
“We’ve got a lot of injured people here.”
“Correct.”
“Let’s spin up some medical support.”

“Oh yeah.”

“…But that would take time.”
Lucy frowned slightly.
“What do you mean?”

Then he casually said something that made several nearby refugees stiffen.

Lucy’s ears snapped upright.
“…Excuse me?”
Jake raised a hand toward the ships.
“They’re basically scrap at this point.”

Lucy stepped forward.
“These vessels carried our people across multiple star systems. They are not—”

“But they’re done now.”
Lucy’s tail lashed slightly.
“Those ships are all we have.”

And suddenly…
The ships began to move.

Perfectly.
Gracefully.

Sensor arrays.
Reactor regulators.

Life-support modules.

Her emotions were difficult to read.
A mix of awe…
Fear…
And disbelief.
Because if this ship could casually convert six interstellar transports into a swarm of advanced medical drones…
What else could it do?

Nearly half an hour passed.
The drones finished treating the wounded one by one.
Lucy’s shoulder felt almost normal again.
The machine drifted away once its work was done.
Around the hangar, the remaining pieces of the refugee ships still floated in loose clusters.
Bent hull fragments.
Empty fuel tanks.
Structural beams.

“Oh! Right.”
He suddenly sat forward again.
“Next problem.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Problem?”
Jake looked around the massive hall at the hundreds of Lunari.
“Living arrangements.”
Several nearby ears perked up.
Jake clapped his hands together once.
“Alright. Where do you want to live?”
The Lunari exchanged uncertain glances.
Lucy tilted her head.
“…Live?”
Jake nodded.
“Yeah. Cabins. Rooms. Apartments. Whatever.”
He gestured casually around them.
“You’re staying on the ship for a while, so we should probably figure that out.”
Lucy hesitated slightly.
She had expected… something else.
Small crew quarters, perhaps.
Shared sleeping areas.
Temporary housing.
She spoke carefully.
“We do not require much.”
Jake waited.
Lucy chose her words politely.
“If there are unused crew cabins… we could share them.”
Jake stared at her.
“…Share them?”
“Yes.”
She gave a small apologetic smile.
“We do not wish to become a burden.”
Jake blinked.
Then blinked again.
“…Burden?”
Lucy nodded gently.
“You have already done more for us than we could ever repay.”
Jake slowly leaned back in his chair, looking genuinely confused.
He glanced around the enormous hall.
Then back at Lucy.
“Burden?”
He gestured widely at the empty space surrounding them.
“This ship is mostly empty.”
Lucy followed his gesture.
The hall alone could probably house their entire population.
Jake scratched his chin thoughtfully.
“Actually, come to think of it…”
He looked up toward the ceiling.
“LYRA.”
“Yes, Jake.”
“How much of the ship is currently occupied?”
“Two point three percent.”
Jake pointed upward triumphantly.
“See?”
Lucy blinked.
“…Two percent?”
“Two point three.”
Jake looked very pleased.
“That includes the pizza ovens.”
Several nearby Lunari quietly murmured to each other.
Lucy tried to process that number.
Two percent.
This ship was so large that four hundred and thirty-nine people barely registered as an increase in population.
Jake leaned forward again.
“Okay. Let’s do this properly.”
He pointed at Lucy like someone conducting a survey.
“What kind of environment do Lunari prefer?”
Lucy hesitated.
“…Prefer?”
“Yeah.”
Jake gestured casually.
“Noise levels. Climate. Space. Lighting. Stuff like that.”
Lucy thought for a moment.
Her tail moved slowly as she considered.
“Well…”
She lifted a finger slightly.
“Not too loud.”
Jake nodded seriously.
“Reasonable.”
Another finger.
“Somewhat spacious.”
Jake nodded again.
“Good taste.”
Lucy thought a little more.
“…Clean air.”
Jake smiled.
“Oh good.”
He leaned back in his chair with visible satisfaction.
“That’s easy.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Easy?”
Jake nodded confidently.
“Very easy.”
He stood up and stretched his arms.
“LYRA.”
“Yes, Jake.”
“Prepare residential environments for four hundred and thirty-nine Lunari.”
A brief pause followed.
Lucy expected LYRA to ask for clarification.
Instead the AI replied calmly:
“Parameters?”
Jake pointed at Lucy.
“Not loud.”
Another finger.
“Spacious.”
Another.
“Clean air.”
A pause.
Then he added thoughtfully:
“…Also probably comfortable.”
Lucy blinked.
“That is very vague.”
Jake waved a hand.
“You’ll like it.”
LYRA responded immediately.
“Residential sectors are available.”
The lights in the dining hall shifted slightly as a holographic projection appeared above one of the nearby tables.
Lucy gasped softly.
A three-dimensional model of the ship rotated slowly in the air.
It was enormous.
Far larger than she had imagined.
Whole sections were highlighted.
“Multiple habitation zones are currently unoccupied,” LYRA explained calmly.
Jake leaned over the projection.
“Oh hey, we never finished setting up that park section.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Park?”
Jake nodded casually.
“Yeah.”
He pointed at a massive highlighted area.
“Artificial ecosystems. Lakes. Trees. Stuff like that.”
Lucy stared at the hologram.
“…Inside the ship?”
Jake shrugged.
“Sure.”
He pointed to another section.
“Or we could use one of the habitat rings.”
Lucy slowly turned toward him.
“…Rings?”
Jake nodded.
“Rotating gravity environments.”
Lucy looked at the hologram again.
Entire districts of the ship were being highlighted as potential living areas.
Forests.
Open residential zones.
Apartment structures.
Large garden environments.
Her ears slowly lifted.
“…Jake.”
“Yeah?”
“…How big is this ship?”
Jake scratched his head.
“Hm.”
He looked up at the hologram.
“…Pretty big.”
Lucy stared at him.
Jake grinned.
“Don’t worry.”
He gestured toward the Lunari around them.
“We’ll find you a nice place.”
Lucy looked back at the hologram.
Then around the massive dining hall.
Then back at Jake.
She had the strange feeling that the answer to the question “Where do you want to live?” might be far more complicated than she had expected.







Jake looked around the dining hall one last time.
Hundreds of Lunari were still seated at the long tables, some finishing their meals, others clearly struggling to stay awake. The emotional storm of the day had finally caught up with them.
Jake clapped his hands once.
“Alright.”
Several ears turned toward him.
“Field trip.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Field trip?”
Jake stood up and stretched his arms casually.
“Yep.”
He nodded toward the huge exit corridor.
“Follow me.”
The Lunari exchanged uncertain glances, but curiosity quickly overcame hesitation. Slowly, the entire group began moving again, following Jake out of the gigantic dining hall.
The same massive corridor awaited them.
The scale still felt unreal.
Four hundred and thirty-nine refugees walked through a passage large enough to move heavy cargo vehicles. Their footsteps echoed softly against the polished walls as overhead lighting guided them deeper into the ship.
Lucy walked beside Jake again.
“…Where are we going?”
Jake grinned slightly.
“Your new neighborhood.”
Lucy’s ears tilted forward.
“My… what?”
Jake just kept walking.
After several minutes the corridor widened even further, eventually ending at something that looked less like a door and more like a fortress gate.
The massive structure stretched across the entire width of the corridor.
Layers of thick metallic panels overlapped like armored petals.
Lucy slowed her steps slightly.
“…That is a door?”
Jake nodded casually.
“Yep.”
He stopped in front of it.
“LYRA.”
“Yes, Jake.”
“Open the garden.”
Lucy blinked.
“…Garden?”
A deep mechanical hum filled the corridor.
The enormous doors began to move.
Layer by layer, the thick panels separated and slid apart with a smooth, almost silent motion. Light began pouring through the widening opening.
Not artificial corridor lighting.
Real light.
Warm.
Golden.
The doors opened completely.
And the Lunari froze.
Because beyond the doorway—
There was sky.
An endless blue sky stretched high above them, soft clouds drifting lazily through the air.
A warm breeze rolled gently across their fur and hair.
Lucy’s breath caught.
They stepped forward slowly.
Grass.
Real grass.
It covered rolling fields that stretched far into the distance. A wide river flowed calmly through a dense forest. Small hills rose along the horizon, dotted with tall trees whose leaves shimmered softly in the light.
Bird-like shapes glided overhead.
Lucy’s ears twitched in surprise until she realized they weren’t birds at all.
Small maintenance drones shaped like birds drifted through the artificial sky, quietly maintaining the environment.
And above everything—
Far above—
Was a massive curved dome.
The material was perfectly transparent, revealing the blackness of space beyond it. Stars glittered across the endless void outside, while the artificial sky shimmered beneath the dome’s protective surface.
Lucy slowly stepped forward onto the grass.
Her tail moved slowly behind her.
“…This…”
More Lunari stepped past the doorway, their reactions spreading like ripples.
Gasps.
Whispers.
Some simply stared upward.
Jake walked out onto the grass and turned back toward them proudly. He spread his arms wide.
“My garden.”
Lucy looked at him.
Then slowly back at the landscape stretching for kilometers in every direction.
“…This… is inside the ship?”
Jake nodded.
“Yep.”
Lucy blinked again.
“You… built this?”
Jake shrugged.
“Sure.”
He looked across the fields thoughtfully.
“I got bored during a three month mapping mission.”
A quiet voice echoed gently from above.
“It required fourteen percent of the vessel’s internal volume.”
Lucy slowly turned toward the sky.
“…Fourteen percent?”
LYRA’s voice continued calmly.
“Total surface area: eight hundred and twelve square kilometers.”
The Lunari fell silent.
Several of them slowly sank down into the grass, overwhelmed by the scale of what they were seeing.
Eight hundred square kilometers.
Inside a ship.
Lucy turned slowly in place.
Forests.
Fields.
Rivers.
Open land as far as she could see.
Her people had lived their entire lives inside cramped industrial habitats on the colony.
And now—
They were standing inside something larger than most cities.
Jake looked back at the refugees.
“So.”
He gestured casually across the landscape.
“You can use it.”
Lucy stared at him.
“…Use it?”
Jake nodded.
“Sure.”
He waved a hand toward the horizon.
“Build houses. Make villages. Whatever you want.”
He shrugged.
“Plenty of space.”
A few Lunari looked like they might faint.
Jake added casually:
“Just until we build you some real ships.”
Lucy barely heard the last sentence.
She had slowly walked a few steps away from the group.
The grass moved gently around her feet.
A soft breeze brushed through her hair and across her ears.
Her tail swayed slowly behind her.
For the first time since escaping the colony—
There were no walls pressing in around her.
No industrial noise.
No commands.
No fear.
Just open space.
Sky.
Wind.
Stars above.
Lucy closed her eyes for a moment and breathed deeply.
Clean air filled her lungs.
When she opened her eyes again, the endless fields stretched before her people.
And for the first time since the day they fled—
Lucy realized something.
They weren’t just surviving anymore.
They were safe.







The great doors of the habitat remained open behind them, but most of the Lunari had already wandered away from the entrance.
Curiosity slowly overcame exhaustion.
Small groups spread across the fields. Some carefully touched the grass as if afraid it might disappear. Others walked toward the river that wound through the valley like a ribbon of silver.
A few younger Lunari had already started running.
Their laughter carried softly across the open land.
Near the entrance, however, two figures remained still.
Lucy stood beside Jake at the edge of the meadow.
For several minutes neither of them spoke.
Lucy watched her people.
One of the elders knelt beside a patch of flowers, gently touching the petals. Another group had reached the riverbank and were staring at their reflections in the clear water.
A young Lunari splashed the surface and jumped back in surprise when the water rippled.
The simple joy of it made Lucy’s chest feel tight.
Jake watched quietly as well, hands resting casually in his jacket pockets.
For once he wasn’t talking.
The breeze moved through the tall grass, gently brushing against Lucy’s legs. Her long black tail swayed slowly behind her, catching the light of the artificial sun.
Eventually she spoke.
Her voice was soft.
“…Jake?”
“Yeah?”
She hesitated for a moment before asking the question that had been quietly forming in her mind since the moment he appeared in the darkness of space.
“Why are you helping us?”
Jake glanced at her.
Lucy kept her eyes on the distant river where several Lunari were now cautiously stepping into the water.
“You did not know us,” she continued quietly.
“You destroyed the pirates without hesitation.”
She paused.
“You destroyed the colony that enslaved us.”
Her ears tilted slightly downward.
“And you gave us… all of this.”
The wind rustled through the grass around them.
Lucy finally looked at him.
“…Why?”
Jake didn’t answer immediately.
Instead he tilted his head slightly, as if considering the question from several angles.
Then he shrugged.
“Well…”
He lifted one hand and raised a finger.
“Several reasons.”
Lucy watched him carefully.
Jake raised a second finger.
“First reason.”
He spoke with the same casual tone he had used all evening.
“Pirates annoy me.”
Lucy blinked.
Jake looked out toward the horizon.
“They shoot at my ships sometimes.”
He shrugged again.
“Very rude.”
Lucy stared at him, unsure how to respond to that.
Jake raised a second finger.
“Second reason.”
He spoke with slightly more emphasis.
“Slavers annoy me even more.”
Lucy’s ears slowly lifted.
Jake’s expression was still relaxed, but there was a faint edge in his voice now.
“I really hate slavers.”
He glanced at her briefly.
“And colonies that treat an entire species like disposable labor.”
The breeze moved across the fields again.
Lucy felt her tail curl slightly behind her.
Jake raised a third finger.
“And…”
He paused.
His eyes drifted toward Lucy.
Specifically—
Toward the soft black ears on top of her head.
Lucy immediately noticed.
Her ears twitched nervously.
Jake’s gaze moved slightly lower to the long black tail swaying slowly behind her.
He nodded thoughtfully.
“…Cat people are objectively adorable.”
Lucy froze.
Her entire face turned red instantly.
“W–what?”
Jake nodded with complete seriousness.
“Scientifically speaking.”
Lucy’s ears flattened slightly.
“I do not think—”
“Adorable ears.”
He gestured casually.
“Adorable tail.”
Another nod.
“Faintly visible fangs.”
Lucy immediately covered her mouth.
Jake finished his analysis.
“Very high adorableness rating.”
Lucy looked like her brain had temporarily stopped functioning.
“I— that— you—”
From somewhere above them, LYRA’s calm voice cut in.
“Captain.”
Jake glanced upward.
“Yes?”
“Your diplomatic methods remain questionable.”
Jake shrugged casually.
“Hey.”
He pointed lightly toward Lucy.
“You can’t hide the truth.”
Lucy stared at him in disbelief.
Her ears were still bright red.
Several seconds passed before she finally managed to speak again.
“…You destroyed an entire star colony today.”
Jake nodded.
“Mhm.”
“You defeated twenty pirate ships.”
“Mhm.”
“You built a planetary habitat inside a spacecraft.”
“Mhm.”
Lucy gestured helplessly toward the endless fields around them.
“And your explanation for helping my people…”
She paused.
“…is that we are adorable?”
Jake tilted his head thoughtfully.
“Well.”
He raised a finger.
“That’s reason number three.”
Lucy stared at him.
“And the other reasons?”
Jake looked out across the landscape again.
The Lunari were spreading farther into the fields now. Some had reached the small forest near the river. Others were lying in the grass, staring up at the artificial sky.
He watched them quietly for a moment.
Then he spoke again, a little more softly.
“…Also because someone should have helped you sooner.”
Lucy blinked.
Jake shrugged lightly.
“But nobody did.”
He turned and started walking slowly toward the fields.
“So now I am.”
Lucy stood still for a moment.
The wind brushed through her hair again.
She watched him walk a few steps ahead before finally following.
Her tail swayed slowly behind her.
For the first time since she had met him, she began to realize something.
Jake’s reasons might sound absurd.
But beneath the jokes…
He meant every word.
