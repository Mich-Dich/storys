

Julia’s voice trembles through the door.
“Dave… please. I can’t feel my hands anymore.”
I stand in the narrow hallway of my apartment, staring at the steel door like it’s the only thing holding the world together. Frost creeps along the inside edges of the frame. The power has been out for days, maybe weeks—I’ve lost count. My breath fogs in front of my face.
Outside the wind howls through the stairwell like some starving animal.
“Dave…” she whispers again, weaker now. “I just… I just want to be with you.”
My chest tightens. I’ve imagined hearing those words from Julia for months. Maybe longer. In a world before the snow swallowed everything, before the temperatures dropped so low the city itself froze solid.
I grip the handle.
“Hold on,” I say, trying to sound calm. “I’m opening it.”
The latch clicks.
Cold explodes into the hallway as I pull the door open.
Julia stands there, half buried in drifting snow that’s blown into the corridor. Her hair is stiff with frost. Her cheeks are pale, cracked by the cold. For a moment she just stares at me.
Then she smiles.
It’s not the smile I remember.
Something moves behind her.
I don’t even register the man until the fire-axe comes down.
The blade crashes into my shoulder.
A wet crack echoes through the hallway.
I don’t feel the pain right away. Just a violent shove that throws me backward onto the floor. My arm doesn’t respond when I try to push myself up. Warmth floods down my side, steaming in the freezing air.
Julia steps inside.
The man follows her, boots crunching on the frost coating my floor.
“Shit,” he mutters. “Thought you said he had supplies.”
“I thought he did,” Julia snaps back. Her voice is different now. Flat. Annoyed.
I try to speak.
Blood fills my mouth.
My vision swims as they move through my apartment like they own it. Drawers open. Cabinets slam. The mattress is flipped. My backpack is dumped onto the floor.
The man holds up the only water bottle I have left.
Half full.
“That’s it?” he growls.
Julia pulls open the freezer. A chunk of bread drops onto the counter, stiff as a brick, spotted with dark mold.
She stares at it.
Then she looks back at me.
For a moment our eyes meet.
There’s no guilt there. No hesitation.
Just calculation.
The man sighs. “Ten floors down we found three bodies already picked clean.”
Julia nods slowly.
“Well…” she says. “At least this one’s fresh.”
Something cold spreads through my chest that has nothing to do with the temperature.
My hearing fades in and out. The room tilts. Blood pools beneath me, creeping across the tiles.
I try to move.
My body doesn’t listen.
The man crouches beside me and checks my pulse.
“Still alive.”
Julia picks up the axe.
My mind refuses to understand what I’m seeing.
Not Julia.
Not the girl who used to laugh in the elevator. Not the girl who borrowed my charger and never returned it. Not the girl whose smile used to make my day feel warmer in a world that’s slowly freezing to death.
She raises the axe.
“Meat’s meat.”
The blade comes down again.
White pain explodes through what’s left of my shoulder.
I can’t scream.
My vision is narrowing now, shrinking into a tunnel filled with red and snow-white flashes. Blood runs over my face, warm for a moment before it cools in the freezing air.
Through half-closed eyes I see her kneel beside me.
Her hands are steady.
Methodical.
The axe rises.
Falls.
Rises again.
My arm separates with a dull, wet crack.
Julia grabs it and drags it toward the kitchen.
Darkness closes in around the edges of my sight.
The last thing I see is her turning the axe again, preparing for the next cut.
And the last thing I feel…
is the cold.
I wake up gasping.
Air tears into my lungs like I’ve been underwater. My body snaps upright in the bed, sheets tangled around my legs. My heart is hammering so hard it feels like it’s trying to punch its way out of my chest.
My shoulder.
My arm.
I grab them both with shaking hands.
Whole.
Uncut. Unbroken. Warm.
For a long moment I just sit there, breathing like a hunted animal, staring at my hands as if they might vanish.
The room is quiet.
The heater hums softly.
Sunlight slips through the curtains.
No frost on the walls. No drifting snow in the hallway. No blood soaking into the floor tiles.
My throat tightens.
Images crash through my mind.
Julia’s smile.
The axe.
My arm sliding across the floor.
Her voice saying *“Meat’s meat.”*
Something in my chest twists.
I stagger out of bed and stumble to the window.
Outside the city moves like nothing is wrong. Cars roll through intersections. People walk their dogs. Someone laughs somewhere down the street.
My phone lights up on the desk.
October 18.
I stare at the date.
Ten days.
Ten days before the snow starts.
Ten days before winter arrives and never leaves.
Ten days before the world begins to freeze solid.
My hands slowly curl into fists.
At first a strange sound escapes my throat.
A quiet breath.
Then another.
It takes me a moment to realize what it is.
A laugh.
Soft.
Broken.
“Ha…”
My shoulders begin to shake.
“Ha… hah…”
The sound grows, spilling out of me in uneven bursts. Months—no, years—of fear, pain, betrayal, starvation, and death boil up from somewhere deep inside my chest.
I lean against the wall as the laughter spreads.
“HAHA…!”
It gets louder.
My stomach hurts from it. My ribs ache. Tears blur my vision.
But I can’t stop.
“HAHAHAHA—!”
The sound fills the apartment, bouncing off the walls, rising and rising until it feels like thunder rolling through the room.
I clutch my stomach, nearly doubling over.
Because now I understand.
The world gave me another chance.
Not to survive.
No.
To **settle a debt.**
I straighten slowly, wiping tears from my eyes.
My voice is hoarse when I speak.
“Julia…”
Just saying her name makes something cold settle into my bones.
I walk back to the bed and sit down, breathing slowly.
“I loved you.”
The words feel strange now.
Like something said by a different person.
A weaker one.
My lips curl.
“But you…” I whisper, almost fondly. “You chopped off my arm while I was still breathing.”
The laugh comes again, quieter this time.
“So killing you would be… what?”
I tilt my head, imagining it.
An axe.
One swing.
Silence.
My smile fades.
“No.”
Too quick.
Too easy.
Too kind.
Images drift through my mind. Dark ones.
A chair. Ropes. A cold room.
A bucket pressed against someone’s stomach. Three desperate mice trapped inside. A fire slowly heating the metal while the animals claw and panic, searching for escape.
In another time, another world, people used that method.
Eventually the mice always chose the only path left.
Forward.
Through flesh.
I picture Julia screaming.
Begging.
My fingers slowly tighten.
For one single moment the thought feels… perfect.
But then I exhale.
And shake my head.
“No.”
My voice is calm now.
Cold.
“That would end too fast.”
Pain like that burns bright, then it’s gone. The mind breaks. The body fails. Death follows.
And then it's over.
    Three years cannot fit inside one moment.
    Three years of fear.
    Three years of hunger.
    Three years of waiting for the next terrible thing to happen.
I lean back and stare at the ceiling.
“No, Julia… we’re going to take our time.”
The words come out slowly, thoughtfully, like a craftsman planning a project.
“I want you to wake up every morning and wonder what I’ll do that day.”
A quiet smile spreads across my face.
“I want you to hope I’ll finally kill you.”
My voice drops to a whisper.
“And then realize I won’t.”
Silence fills the room.
My breathing steadies.
The storm inside my chest settles into something colder. Sharper.
Focused.
Then something strange happens.
The air in front of my desk ripples.
A dark oval tear opens in the air itself, floating silently like a hole cut into reality.
I stare at it.
A thought forms in my mind without words.
**Storage.**
I reach over, grab the empty glass on my desk, and push it toward the darkness.
The moment it touches the surface—
It disappears.
My mind floods with sensation.
Space.
A vast gray expanse stretching outward like an empty world.
The glass rests there, perfectly still.
Untouched.
Unchanging.
Above it floats faint glowing text.
LEVEL 1.
My breathing slows.
A space larger than a shopping mall.
Time inside does not move.
Ten days before the apocalypse.
Slowly… very slowly… my smile returns.
Because revenge requires patience.
Preparation.
Control.
And now I have something no one else in this dying world will ever possess.
Time.
I stand up and look around my apartment.
Food.
Water.
Medicine.
Tools.
Generators.
Fuel.
Everything people will kill each other for once the cold arrives.
My laugh returns, softer now.
Satisfied.
“Alright,” I say to the empty room.
“Let’s begin.”
